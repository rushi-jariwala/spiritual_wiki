Every element of Gurudev's early life—from the auspicious signs
at his birth to his professional affiliations—was strategically
reverse-engineered to sculpt him into the spiritual titan he was
to become. His journey was not merely a path he traversed but a
destiny carefully crafted, uncovering the elaborate blueprint of a
life designed for the Guru of Gurus.

        •

Water was one of the leitmotifs in Gurudev's life. He was born
through the blessings of jal¹ and, in later years, healed through it.

Gurudev's sister Rashmi ji shares the fascinating story of her
grandfather's pilgrimage to Gurudwara Santoksar Sahib in
Amritsar, Punjab. The pilgrimage had one goal—to obtain
blessings for his daughters-in-law so that they could give birth
to boys. A sage at the Gurudwara gifted him two fruits and jal,
hinting at the name of a future child: Santprakash. However, the
pages of the Bhrighu Samhita whispered a different prophecy.
The ancient Rishi² Bhrigu foretold the rise of a divya aatma³ with
a different name who would be born in the small town of Hariana
in Hoshiarpur, Punjab.

An unusual downpour—a sign seen as auspicious—coincided
with Gurudev's birth. Initially intended to be named Santprakash,

      Gurudev relocated to Delhi after high school, their lives diverged,
      but fate had a way of reconciling them. Subbhash ji reached out
      to his former friend after learning that he had become a guru
      capable of guiding, helping and healing others. He sought and
      received spiritual direction and became an integral member of
      the administrative team at Gurudev's sthan² in Gurgaon. Despite
      the evolution of their relationship dynamics, the essence of their
      childhood bond remained intact.

"Gurudev never had a keen interest in academics," Subbhash ji
recalls, the amusement in his tone evident. "To be honest, neither
of us were at the top of our class."

The notion that the future shapes the strategies of the past finds
a compelling illustration in young Gurudev's disinterest in
conventional education. Though he did not know it at the time, he
was destined for omniscience, a state he would achieve through
the awakening of his third eye. As a mahaguru, his extraordinary
powers enabled him to view the past, present, and future in
ways others could not comprehend. He revealed significant
visions to his disciples, such as the chemistry of blood and the
precise blueprint of a drainboard sink. He healed ailments that
were deemed incurable by modern science. Traditional subjects
such as history, chemistry, biology, and physics were reinvented
through his cosmic lens. How could mundane geography interest
someone who, in the future, would regularly and wilfully traverse
astral dimensions?

"Our shared adventures often led us to nearby fields, where we
would pick fruits or get up to some other mischief, only to face
the consequences when we got home," Subbhash ji chuckles. "On
our way home from school was a dargah.¹ We would visit it to
pluck bers² from the trees that grew nearby. The fakir³ who lived
there would say, 'You create a lot of trouble; at least bow your
head here'. I found out much later that Gurudev often visited the
dargah alone."

"Every Thursday, without exception, Gurudev would light a diya⁴
at the dargah," Gaggu ji, Gurudev's nephew, recalls. "Even when
nature's fury caused floods, or traditional diyas were hard to
come by, he would innovate—sometimes using a handmade mud
or clay lamp."

Born into Hinduism, the boy destined to be a mahaguru
embodied spiritual universality from the beginning. Throughout
his life, he generously shared his wisdom with individuals from
every faith and walk of life. His path, marked by alliances with
many spiritual luminaries, epitomised a seamless fusion of faiths.
At the heart of his philosophy was a fundamental principle: to
respect and cherish all spiritual paths, to connect rather than
convert. In today's fractured world, this ethos of harmony and
inclusiveness thrives in every sthan linked to him.

        •

A defining episode in Gurudev's formative years was his
unsettling encounter with black magic.

Gurudev's wife, reverentially known as Mataji, shares an insightful
episode from her husband's childhood: "When he was in the fifth
or sixth grade, an acquaintance gave him something infused
with negative energy to eat. This made him quite ill, and he was
bedridden for about two years. When conventional medical
treatments failed to heal him, my mother-in-law proposed
seeking help from the Baba Balak Nath temple in Hamirpur,
which is about 103 kilometres from Hariana. Despite my father-
in-law's reservations, my mother-in-law's conviction won out,
and together, they embarked on the challenging journey to the
temple, hopeful for a cure. At the temple, a devotee of Baba Balak
Nath gave Gurudev jal to drink, leading him to vomit a mixture
of undigested buttermilk and ashes from a masaan.¹ Only after
this purge did his health begin to improve."

According to Mataji, this ordeal was a turning point in Gurudev's
spiritual journey: "He struggled with the idea of an external force
wielding such power over his body for so long. This unsettling
realisation catalysed his deep dive into spirituality."

 Two pivotal figures who significantly influenced Gurudev's
 spiritual upbringing during this time were Sitaram ji of Dasua

 Together, they provided young Gurudev with a structured
 approach to spirituality. They guided him in acquiring quite
 a few siddhis,¹ which allowed him to heal diseases and grant
 wishes. While these early abilities were instrumental in teaching
 him mental discipline, focus, perseverance, and resilience, they

 these initial siddhis, embarking on a spiritual path that led to his
 eventual emergence as a mahaguru.

Gurudev's strong inclination towards spirituality over academics
persuaded his family that moving to Delhi might steer him
towards a more conventional and successful career path. After
completing his tenth grade, Gurudev relocated to Delhi, where
he lived under the care of his uncle in Shahdara.

Life in India's bustling capital was not without its trials for young
Gurudev. Ever the pragmatist, he embraced any available job
to support himself. At his uncle's home, he was not merely a
guest but an integral part of the household, contributing to daily
chores. Frequently, he would carry heavy sacks of wheat to the
grinding mill, epitomising the spirit of dedication as he sought to
repay his gratitude towards his uncle and his family.

Throughout his life, the mahaguru embodied and championed a
lifestyle free from debt. He exemplified the virtues of generosity
over personal gain, setting a powerful example for others.

        •

Gurudev's early life was woven from a series of events that
seemed to stitch together the pieces of his destiny. During a walk
in Shahdara, he aided a stranger who turned out to be the spouse
of the principal at Delhi's PUSA Institute. This meeting led to
Gurudev's enrollment in a technical course at the Bharat Sevak
Samaj, setting him on a path to becoming a soil surveyor for the
Government of India. This job seemed designed to extend his
future spiritual influence into India's remote areas, reaching far
beyond urban centres.

The initial group of Gurudev's disciples—RC Malhotra ji, Dr
Shankarnarayan ji, FC Sharma ji, RP Sharma ji, Bade Jain Saheb,
RK Sharma ji, Dutta Saheb, and Suraj Sharma ji—emerged
strategically from his workplace. Born during the same era as the
mahaguru, these men were destined to be pivotal in his spiritual

     At work, one of the first people Gurudev formed a friendship

     Nagpal ji resided in a modest apartment in East Delhi, sharing

In the realm of human abilities, one can aspire to master 16
or more kalas.¹ However, a mahaguru's range of abilities often
exceeds these. A sharp sense of humour is essential among these
kalas, and Gurudev was endowed with it in spades.

As time unfurled, I observed Gurudev's adeptness at weaving
humour and wit to leave a lasting impression. On the surface,
his comments seemed light-hearted, yet the underlying messages
always landed, resonating deeply with those who understood.

        •

"One trait of his that profoundly impacted me was his complete
detachment from money," Dwarkanath ji recalls.

From a young age, Gurudev spent his pocket money on feeding
animals and birds or treating his friends to sweets and soft
drinks. He rarely spent money on himself, preferring to use it
to benefit others. In the later years, I noticed that Gurudev's
approach to money was paradoxically unique. Despite earning
modestly, he gave generously, always making ends meet in
seemingly mysterious ways. One notable habit illustrating his
detachment was his reluctance to accept leftover change during
transactions. Having tried to adopt this habit myself, I can attest
to its difficulty—it is far more challenging than it appears.

Occasionally, Gurudev would reach into his pockets and, to
everyone's surprise, pull out a bundle of notes. I wonder if he
begrudgingly wielded some power to conjure up the currency,
using it solely in circumstances that demanded nisvarth seva.² It
seems plausible that this extraordinary ability was granted to him
later in life as an acknowledgement of his steadfast altruism and
complete detachment from money.
