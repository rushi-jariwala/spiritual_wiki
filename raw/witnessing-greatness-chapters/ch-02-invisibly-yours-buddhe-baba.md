    Chapter 2
    Invisibly Yours,
    Buddhe Baba



In the world of spiritualism, where identities often blur like
wisps of incense, the role of a guru in guiding beings along
their evolutionary journey is crucial. Our conscious awareness,
typically limited to a single lifetime, requires the enlightenment
of a guru to awaken and nurture the latent qualities of Shiv¹
consciousness. Even manifestations of Shiv, such as Adi
Shankaracharya, the Buddha, Guru Gobind Singh, Guru Nanak
Dev, and many others, needed a guru during their earthly lives to
shepherd their spiritual evolution and fulfil their destined roles.

At the heart of Gurudev's spiritual journey stands his guru,
Buddhe Baba. The mahaguru deeply revered his spiritual mentor,
yet he maintained an aura of secrecy around his identity, fueling
ongoing speculation and intrigue.

        •

Gurudev's journey from spiritual aspirant to a revered mahaguru
was one of remarkable transformation. In his youth, under the
tutelage of Sitaram ji of Dasua, he mastered many siddhis. These
were not parlour tricks of an average practitioner; they were
fruits of deep spiritual practices. However, Buddhe Baba saw that
for someone destined to embody the finest qualities of Shiv, these
powers, though impressive, were preliminary achievements—
significant, yet not the zenith of spiritual development.

The mahaguru remained reserved about his mentor's identity.
This silence fuelled widespread speculation and assumptions
about who Buddhe Baba truly was.

FC Sharma ji, one of Gurudev's humblest disciples, states, "For
many of us, Gurudev stood alone in his spiritual journey. Yet,
he occasionally shared anecdotes about his guru, who would
guide him, even restraining him from certain actions. He often
directed those in need to seek his guru's blessings at the sthan in
Sector 7, Gurgaon. He advised them that lighting a dhoop¹ there
would bring comfort and fruition. Even when presented with a
wedding invitation, he gently instructed, 'Place the card at the
sthan, and ensure Buddhe Baba is invited first.'"

While many disciples perceived Buddhe Baba as a manifestation
of Shiv, others contend that he was Sitaram ji of Benaras.

Sitaram ji of Benaras was an exceptionally evolved saint who
departed from this world seh sharir¹—an extraordinary feat
achieved by only a handful of spiritualists, including Jesus and
Saint Kabir.

Raji Sharma ji, a disciple, explains, "I have never personally met
Buddhe Baba, yet I understand him to be the Shiv roop² of our
par dada³ guru, Sitaram ji of Benaras. Our par dada guru is
one with Shiv. Let me elaborate on their connection. Gurudev
often posed the question: What truly is Shiv? Is Shiv simply the
figure perched on a mountain peak with a trident in hand and
matted hair? In reality, Shiv symbolises Brahman⁴ consciousness
or cosmic consciousness—this is the true essence of Shiv. This
cosmic consciousness is what our par dada guru embodies."

Gurudev's sister-in-law, affectionately known as Chachi ji,

other than the mahaguru's enlightened jivaatma.⁵ She asserts,
"Gurudev was Shiv himself, and he referred to his evolved form
as 'Buddhe Baba'."

The mystery of Buddhe Baba endured throughout Gurudev's
eighteen years as mahaguru and remains to this day. Only a
select few, such as Gurudev's eldest daughter Renu ji, have had
the privilege of seeing him in physical form.

"My first encounter with Buddhe Baba was during my childhood
at our home in Shivpuri, Gurgaon. Until that day, I had only
heard about him. Daddy would ask us to silently share any deep-
seated wish with Buddhe Baba at the sthan," she shares, her voice
tinged with nostalgia. "I was home alone, sick with a fever, while
Mummy and Daddy were at work. Suddenly, a man dressed in
white appeared from the storeroom. He had long white hair and
a flowing beard. I don't recall his height, but his upright posture
made him seem imposing. Terrified, I covered myself with a
blanket, hoping he wouldn't see me. When Mummy returned, I
clung to her, saying, 'I am scared.' She reassured me, 'Why are you
afraid? This is our home, and we have the sthan here. Chant your
mantra'. I told her that even while chanting, I felt his presence
linger. 'That was Buddhe Baba,' Mummy calmly assured me, 'He
watches over this sthan and all of us.'"

It was widely believed that Buddhe Baba watched over the sthan,
shielding both its inhabitants and visitors from negative forces.

A common thread in the stories of those who have seen Buddhe
Baba physically or in visions is his portrayal as an elderly, slim,
tall figure dressed in white with white hair and a white beard.

Puran ji, a disciple who spent many years at Gurudev's home
in Gurgaon, vividly recalls his encounter with Gurudev's guru:
"I was privileged to see Buddhe Baba in my dreams. He was a
towering figure, robust, dressed in pure white, with a mane of
white hair and a distinguished white beard. He was distributing
grains to the needy, a sight that still fills me with awe."

In the captivating records of Gurudev's life, his sister Rashmi ji
recounts a compelling tale that blends misunderstandings, grief,
and mystical experiences.

During a session in which Gurudev imparted wisdom to his
disciples, he mentioned that the true depth of his teachings
would only be understood after he was 'gone'. Rashmi ji, who
overheard this, misinterpreted his words as a forewarning of his
imminent departure, which plunged her into deep despair.

Recounting the experience, she says, "The thought of losing my
brother left me grief-stricken, and I found myself weeping on
the floor of his empty bedroom. Then, a luminous white light
materialised, revealing an older man dressed in white with long
flowing white hair. Seeing my distress, he gently encouraged me
to eat. When I refused, he inquired about my reluctance."

Overcome with emotion, Rashmi ji replied, "How can I eat when
my brother will no longer be with me?" The compassionate figure
reassured her, "Your brother is not leaving you; he is embarking
on a soil survey tour and will return in a few months." With that,
the being faded away.

translucent figure before my eyes. Dressed in a tehmat,¹ revealing
a slender yet strong physique, he moved swiftly and gracefully.
Malhotra ji later confirmed my sighting, acknowledging that the
figure was indeed Buddhe Baba, the revered guru of Gurudev."

The notion of bodily repercussions originating from dreams or
astral realms is indeed perplexing, almost defying the bounds
of logic for many. To entertain the idea that a wound sustained
in a dream could manifest physically upon waking stretches the
imagination. Yet, I have experienced this phenomenon. During
an out-of-the-body experience, I moved upward through a
ceiling fan to the room above. On my descent, my astral form
briefly intersected with the fan blades. I heard a distinct cracking
sound at my ankles, accompanied by sharp pain. By morning, my
ankle showed clear signs of injury, leaving me with a limp that
lasted several weeks.

The spiritual path toward embodying Shiv involves transcending
conventional boundaries linked to names and societal roles.
The pursuit of fame and recognition is not merely a worldly
distraction but poses significant spiritual risks, potentially
rekindling the ego and ensnaring the soul in materialism.

        31

Buddhe Baba chose anonymity as a strategic means to sidestep
the constraints and diversions associated with a public identity.
He understood that being known could undermine his deeper
spiritual objectives, specifically his role in developing Gurudev
into a mahaguru and guiding his spiritual growth. He likely
stressed to Gurudev the importance of remaining unknown,
highlighting that the true worth of their spiritual endeavours
extended beyond personal recognition. Selfless service without
attachment to one's own identity exemplifies true seva.

In our hyper-connected world, retaining anonymity is
challenging, yet it is crucial to recognise that self-promotion
and self-glorification will impede spiritual advancement. As
the saying goes, "Hum yahaan apna astitva mitaane aaye hain,
banaane nahin"—we are here to erase our identity, not to
construct it.
