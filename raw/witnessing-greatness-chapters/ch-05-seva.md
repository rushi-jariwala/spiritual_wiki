During an insightful conversation with Rajpal ji, Gurudev shared
his wisdom: "God or param-aatma¹ lives within every being. If
you want to please God, please the gods in human form. Every
person faces struggles, be it pain, illness, or adversity; your
mission is to ease their suffering and minimise their distress."

Gurudev's transformative view on seva focused on serving the
divine essence within each person, not merely the individuals
themselves. This method proposes that by achieving a higher
state of consciousness and recognising aatmic equality, one
can connect with the inner divinity of others while remaining
anchored in the material world. While this concept might seem
straightforward, its implications are profound.

This spirit of generosity and accessibility extended beyond
Gurudev's neighbours and encompassed his colleagues as
well. Despite earning a modest salary, the mahaguru regularly

by Haribabu Gupta ji, who operated a tea stall at Gurudev's office
block at Curzon Road in Delhi.

While Gurudev attended to visitors at the tea stall, he offered
tea as a warm gesture to those around. A few colleagues took
advantage of his kindness, ordering tea and quietly adding the
expense to his tab. When Dwarkanath ji pointed out that some
opportunistic co-workers were exploiting his generosity, the
mahaguru responded with his characteristic philosophy: "Yeh kal
bhi peete the, hum kal bhi peelate the. Yeh aaj bhi peete hain, hum
aaj bhi peelate hain. Yeh kal bhi piyenge, hum kal bhi pilayenge".
The response metaphorically highlighted the enduring nature of
selfless service. Gurudev viewed it as the fate of his co-workers
to receive and his to give, reinforcing the idea that the act of
giving should remain unaffected by the nature or behaviour of
the receiver.

Gurudev was a model of resourcefulness and simplicity. Instead
of building elaborate temples or ashrams, he introduced the
concept of sthans, or home-based spiritual centres for help and
healing. These rooms, set up in the homes of disciples, became
mini spiritual hubs, making it easy to engage in seva across
various locations without straining finances.

Giri Lalwani ji elaborates on Gurudev's visionary approach: "He
understood how hard it was for many people to get to Gurgaon.
That is why he led the way in setting up sthans worldwide—from
big cities to small villages. His intention was clear-cut: to make
it as easy as possible for everyone to benefit from seva while
reducing travel discomfort and costs."

In his pursuit to bring seva to all corners of the world, the
mahaguru embodied the famous saying: if the mountain won't
come to Muhammad, then Muhammad must go to the mountain.

During my early years in spiritualism, Gurudev asked me to be
present at the Gurgaon sthan on every Bada Guruvar to perform
seva. My name was always on the duty chart, but each time I
arrived, the person assigned before me would ask to continue

A few months into this routine, Gurudev called me into his
room to discuss my experiences with seva. Initially hesitant, I
eventually shared my frustration. To my surprise, he listened
with an amused smile and then offered a puzzling insight: "Son,
what I need from you is not tied to specific tasks. I need you to
be available so when the time comes, I can channel your energy
into my work."

Initially, his words were cryptic, yet their significant implications
emerged upon thoughtful consideration. Gurudev frequently
shared that he would meditate on us during patient treatments,
channelling our collective energies to assist others. In time, I
realised that the true value of our contribution extends beyond
mere physical deeds—it rests in the preparedness and openness
of our spirits to integrate with the sthan's broader purpose.
