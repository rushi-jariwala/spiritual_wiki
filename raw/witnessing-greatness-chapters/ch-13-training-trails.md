In 1973, a routine soil survey camp in Guna, a small town in
Madhya Pradesh, transformed into a remarkable scene of hope,
faith, and healing. Initially just an observer, Nagpal ji found
himself in the middle of an unexpected event as his colleague
and friend revealed an extraordinary ability to heal large groups
of people. He recalls, "The camp was flooded with people
arriving in bullock carts, each carrying 15–20 individuals, all
desperate to experience Gurudev's healing touch. The crowd
was as diverse as their issues—some wrestling with personal
turmoil while seemingly incurable physical conditions afflicted
others. Gurudev tended to them all. Some of those who arrived
struggling to walk found themselves able to return home with
newfound strength in their steps!"

Nagpal ji was struck by the stark contrast between Gurudev's
ordinary life and his extraordinary abilities as a spiritual healer.
The events he witnessed compelled him to seek an explanation
from the mahaguru regarding the nature of these healings.
However, every attempt to unravel the enigma was met with
Gurudev's hearty laughter, leaving Nagpal ji's curiosity piqued
and the mystery unsolved.

Gurudev's healings in Guna at the age of 35 fulfilled the prophecy
that he would become 'someone like Shiv' at that age.

During my time with Gurudev, his camps were like gateways to
understanding how the physical and spiritual worlds intertwine.
He embodied a deep commitment to guiding not only human
beings but also wandering spirits. He sometimes chose sites close
to cremation grounds or within dense forests—traditionally
believed to be the haunts of such entities—for his camps. In
search of solace and direction, these unseen beings found a
guiding light in him. He was instrumental in either facilitating
their rebirth or freeing them from their spiritual binds, playing a
crucial role in their journey towards liberation.

Gaggu ji shares a particularly vivid memory from a camp near
Bharatpur, Rajasthan: "Gurudev had chosen an unconventional
location for his camp—right within a cremation ground,
entrusting us with the duty of performing seva. Throughout the
day, he attended to the needs of those seeking his assistance. In the
evening, he summoned me, pointing towards the dense forest at
the edge of the site. 'Do you see those flickering lights?' he asked.
My gaze followed where a series of lights danced in the darkness,
blinking on and off in a rhythmic pattern. Gurudev shared that
these lights were the manifestations of the Syeds, entities without
heads that communicated via these mysterious glowing signals.
Despite the intrigue they presented, he counselled caution,
explicitly advising against attempting to approach them."

Pravesh ji shares a story highlighting the mahaguru's enigmatic
presence: "We went to a remote cremation ground with Gurudev.
Considering how isolated it was, we were confused about why
we were there and who would come to a place like that. Gurudev
casually asked, 'Should I call a crowd?' I just said, 'As you think
best'. Then, Gurudev meditated for thirty minutes. Amazingly,
people started showing up looking for him. Soon, there were
queues of people waiting for his help!"

     Kathog in Himachal Pradesh bore witness to a pivotal chapter
     in the spiritual journey of Gurudev and his fledgling disciples
     from nearby regions. Here, a series of remarkable events
     unfolded at a soil survey camp in 1976, marking the inception of
     a transformative journey for those who crossed the mahaguru's
     path. Among the seekers who came there were Suresh Kohli ji,
     Santosh ji, and Shambhu ji.

     Suresh Kohli ji, a school teacher who transferred to Kathog
     in March of the same year, recounts his first encounter with
     Gurudev. As he shares his story, he speaks of a local tantric
     named Thapa, who lived near the school. Thapa was the bearer
     of news about a man who had established camp across the school
     premises, bearing the sacred symbol of the OM on his hand and
     chest, along with a trishul. Suresh ji doubted Thapa's words,
     suspecting the claims were merely drunken ramblings. However,
     curiosity got the better of him, and he decided to visit the camp.
     There, at the gate, stood Gurudev—a man with a "commanding
     presence". Gurudev not only confirmed Thapa’s assertions but
     also revealed the OM symbol embossed on his right hand.

     Suresh ji recalls their conversation: "I asked Gurudev for a cure
     for tonsilitis and an opportunity to meet God. He advised me to
     meditate at night with my eyes open and a glass of water nearby.
     That night, I saw Gurudev's moving image in front of my almirah,
     which was filled with photographs of deities. I was stunned. I had
     just met him and had no prior devotion to him. Yet, there he was,
     appearing in my sacred space. I had so many questions!"

     This extraordinary vision continued for seven nights. On the
     eighth night, however, the image did not appear. Suresh ji
     approached Gurudev the next day, asking, "What happened last
     night? I did not see you."

     Gurudev replied, "If you see me that way again, you will have met
     God". Suresh ji remembers feeling excited, realising, "This is the
     God I was looking for. I have finally found him!"

spirituality and the acceptance of miracles are as familiar as the
breath of the wind. However, as devotion to Gurudev grew in
Kathog and its neighbouring regions, it stirred undercurrents of

setting the stage for a story of spiritual recognition.

Raji ji recounts: "It is said that there was a pair of khadaus,

temple. These khadaus were so powerful that only someone
with immense spiritual strength could take even a single step in
them. This became a way for the temple priests to differentiate an
evolved spiritualist from a pretender.

"As Gurudev's reputation for miracles grew in Kathog, the priests
at Jwalaji decided to put him to the test," he continues. "They
led him to the basement, presented him with the khadaus, and
asked him to walk in them. With his characteristic calmness,
Gurudev put on the khadaus and not only took one step but
three, unequivocally proving his spiritual stature."

     In 1979, the town of Renuka, nestled in the Simaur district of
     Himachal Pradesh, hosted Gurudev's largest mass healing camp.

     Bittu ji recalls the early days spent there: "We established our
     camp on a piece of uncultivated land owned by Chandramani
     Vashisht ji. Within the camp's boundaries, we discovered a
     neglected Shivling. With Gurudev's approval, I restored, cleaned,
     and painted it. Every morning, as per his instructions, I would
     offer water and light a dhoop there."

     Chandramani Vashisht ji has an interesting back story that Bittu
     ji shares: "Gurudev had left for Delhi on official business, and
     I was at the Renuka camp. Vashisht ji came to the Shivling to
     pray one morning. Afterwards, while drinking tea, he asked if
     'Sharma ji' wore spectacles—he used to call Gurudev 'Sharma

     ji met Gurudev the following day, he inquired whether Gurudev

When Gurudev first arrived in Renuka, he instructed his
disciples not to acknowledge him as their guru. He aimed to keep
his identity hidden until he had established a significant spiritual
alliance with Parshuram ji.

Many spiritual luminaries often test each other's spirituality before
recognising each other formally. Bittu ji shares a story involving
Renu ji, who was seriously ill in Gurgaon and wished to see her
father, who was then in Renuka: "Mataji, along with Subbhash ji
and Pal ji, brought her to Renuka to meet Gurudev. Upon their
arrival, Gurudev was visibly displeased. Mataji explained, 'This
wasn't solely my decision; your daughter, who is ill, wanted to
see you.' Gurudev responded sternly, 'If it is her destiny to die, we
must accept it.' He then immediately instructed them to return
to Gurgaon."

As evening neared, Bittu courageously asked if they could
spend the night at Bayri. Gurudev consented but imposed one
condition: they had to leave before he woke up the following day.
Later, Gurudev revealed that Parshuram ji was testing him to
assess his emotional ties to his family. Gurudev demonstrated
his detachment by meeting this challenge, earning Parshuram
ji's approval. This event signified the beginning of a lasting
partnership between him and the immortal one.

Gurudev utilised his camps to establish several spiritual alliances,
not only with the venerable Parshuram ji and his mother, Renuka
ji, but also with other devi-devtas and many other spiritual
entities yet to be unveiled.
