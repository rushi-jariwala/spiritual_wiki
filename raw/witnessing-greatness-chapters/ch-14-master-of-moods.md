Gurudev had an extraordinary ability to rise above human
emotions, a challenge that most of us grapple with.

FC Sharma ji talks about two eye-opening events. The first took
place at a sthan in Himachal Pradesh. "While visiting Hamirpur,
Gurudev decided to witness the seva at the sthan incognito.
Transforming his appearance to that of an older man dressed in
tattered clothes leaning on a walking stick, he joined the queue
outside. When it was his turn, the sthan sanchalak dismissively
told him to step aside and wait. Days later, after revealing his
true identity, Gurudev confronted the sthan sanchalak, saying,
'You thought I was just an old man? It was me—your Guru! How
can you perform seva properly if you judge people based solely
on their appearance?' The sthan sanchalak, realising his error,
humbly apologised for his misjudgment."

FC Sharma ji shares a profound insight, "Gurudev often said,
'If you serve a thousand but neglect even one, all your efforts
amount to nothing'. In the practice of seva, there is no room
for partiality. A guru must rise above personal feelings to serve
everyone equally. Gurudev maintained that one swayed by
emotions cannot make consistent and impartial decisions. In
spirituality, emotions must not obscure the core objective of
aiding those in need."

The mahaguru's level-headedness was not just for trying times;
it extended into all his relationships. Puran ji, who spent over
a decade in Gurudev’s home and witnessed the intricacies of
his daily life, offers a candid perspective on the mahaguru's
relationship with his family. When asked about Gurudev's
attachment to his kin, he states emphatically, "He was detached
from everyone and everything". Given Puran ji's reputation for
unfiltered honesty, his words carry the weight of authenticity.

Many find it challenging to differentiate parental feelings from
the intricate weave of karmic bonds, but Gurudev did so with
unparalleled clarity. He viewed his family as souls with whom
he shared karmic connections from their past lives. While
such rebirth narratives might overwhelm most with emotion
or caution, Gurudev navigated them with a perfect balance of
detachment and compassion.

Gurudev and Mataji shared an endearing and enduring bond.
Despite his intense devotion to seva and its accompanying
challenges, she stood steadfast as his life partner and spiritual
companion. While she diligently managed her household and
professional responsibilities as a teacher, their relationship was
tinged with an ever-present sense of detachment, like a distant
melody playing in the background.

Mataji remembers, "He was unwavering in his duties as a
husband. Even with our children, he always ensured they felt his
affection. On days when our home was crowded with disciples
and followers, and the children craved his attention, he would
gently remind them, 'People hug and show affection, but I would
much rather spend time with you'. His parenting style was not
conventional or demonstrative. His love was internal and deep-
seated, not outwardly expressive."

     In the eyes of the world, Gurudev was paradoxical. While he
     seemed to belong to everyone, he was bound to none. Every
     disciple felt a cherished bond, believing themselves to be his
     chosen one. The fact that an emotionally detached individual
     could foster such intense affection in countless people was a
     testament to his adeptness at role-play.

     Unlike many, who reserve their deepest feelings for a select
     few, Gurudev's unemotional love had an expansive reach. He
     regarded himself as a part of all creation yet remained personally
     unattached. His distinct vibe, interpreted by many as love,
     embodied care, empathy, and connection while maintaining an
     undercurrent of detachment.

complex character, but understanding his true nature requires
hearing from those who know him well. Pappu ji and Nikku
ji provide such a perspective. Despite their youth, they had
the unique opportunity to observe Gurudev's daily life closely.
Their memories and insights are an important link to his world,
offering us a window into his teachings and way of life.

everyone feel valued and important. People around him felt seen
and loved, a rare quality not many possess, even towards their

of his family. No one was left out; everyone was embraced."

   When recounting his days under Gurudev's protective wings,
   Pappu Pahadia ji paints a vivid tableau of relationships: "While
   we looked up to him as our guru, the ways he showed love were
   diverse—it could be the gentle affection of a grandparent, the
   protective love of a parent, or the closeness of a friend. He got
   my teenage quirks and connected with me in ways that resonated
   deeply. As I matured, he evolved his approach, guiding me like
   a wise friend. It was not until 1982, after years of absorbing his
   teachings, that I genuinely understood the meaning of a 'guru'."

   Gurudev's mastery over his emotions, in this life, was not
   achieved overnight or through some magical elixir. It was the
   fruit of relentless effort and introspection.

   Taming his temper, just as it was with many revered saints before
   him, was a challenge. Yet, the latter part of his spiritual journey
   bore testimony to the significant strides he made, building upon
   the invaluable lessons from his earlier years.

 Mataji, providing insight, explains an unconventional yet
 impactful exercise he deployed for anger management: "If
 someone provoked him, he chose not to rise to the bait. He had
 trained himself to subdue those angry impulses. Occasionally,
 he would deliberately use harsh language towards someone.
 He would respond with laughter when they responded angrily.
 Much of his effort to temper his anger and remain unruffled
 when provoked was practised at his workplace."

   When I sought advice on overcoming sorrow, the mahaguru
   imparted a remarkable insight, "Agar dukh par kaaboo paana hai,
   toh pehle sukh par kaaboo paana seekho (If you want to conquer
   sorrow, first learn to conquer joy)". Years later, reflecting on this
   wisdom, I coined a term that encapsulates the essence of his ek
   vakhya—happylessness.
196

Commonly, happiness is associated with joy, yet this form of
happiness is temporary and inherently carries the risk of leading
to sorrow. Imagine happiness as a seesaw: pleasure sits on one
end, and sorrow on the other. Bliss, however, signifies the centre
balance point, a state of equilibrium where emotions are in
balance and harmony prevails. This condition can be described
as 'happylessness', surpassing the dualistic nature of happiness
and sadness.

Many of Gurudev's devotees came from modest backgrounds,
hailing from various villages and towns, each with unique
professional and social experiences. Often, they would
enthusiastically volunteer to serve at the events held at the sthan.
Yet, like all humans, even the most well-intentioned among
them faltered. And when they did, especially if it meant treating
visitors at the sthan with anything less than utmost respect,
Gurudev took notice. He had little tolerance for any neglect or
arrogance shown towards guests. As a result, many sevadaars
faced his gentle correction, which led them to be more cautious
and considerate in their duties.

FC Sharma ji notes, "Gurudev's emotional outbursts were
rare and reserved primarily for when someone erred during
seva. Otherwise, he was a picture of calm. And if he did have
to reprimand, he would always do it discreetly, away from the
public eye."

I once confessed to FC Sharma ji that Gurudev often corrected
me publicly, to which he responded, "There was always a purpose
behind it. Perhaps he wanted to help you overcome certain karmic
hurdles. He never acted out of emotions. He was detached from
worldly gains and losses."

   While Gurudev might have grappled with anger in his earlier
   years, arrogance never found room in his character.

   He was aware of the pitfalls of ego and recognised how sentiments
   of 'Me, mine, and ours' have historically hindered seva, bhakti,
   and gyan. Over four decades, I have observed that disciples
   rooted in humility often surpass those who boast seniority,
   experience, or knowledge. Humility fosters self-acceptance and
   elevates one's sense of self-worth. It reminds us of our connection
   to the broader mosaic of life, prompting us to look inward and
   acknowledge our spiritual core beyond the limitations of the
   material world.

   •

   Gurudev's heightened awareness gave him a panoramic view of
   the past, present, and future. It was not just clairvoyance but a
   deep, interconnected bond with everyone and everything. His
   ability to foresee future events, particularly tragic ones, without
   the freedom to issue direct warnings, must have been deeply
   stifling, requiring an inner strength beyond comprehension.

   While he had the unique capability to sense thoughts from afar,
   he often chose to veil this skill. But sometimes, traces of this
   knowledge would inadvertently emerge. He played a delicate
   balancing act, being omniscient on one hand and feigning
   ignorance on the other.

   I remember a time he advised me against a scooter ride to Delhi.
   Only later did I understand that he had anticipated a potential
   accident. Thankfully, the accident that unfolded was less severe
   than what might have been.

 Yet, this knowingness was not without its merits. With the
 capability to glimpse into the lives of disciples and followers,
 he could discern their spiritual trajectories. This innate
 understanding positioned him as an incomparable guide,

allowing him to pre-emptively counter his emotional responses.
His acceptance and anticipation of unfolding events neutralised

showed deep compassion for our struggles. Aware of our

Dr Shankarnarayan ji recounts an insightful experience: "One
day, while leaving for work, I wished to pay my respects to
Gurudev. At the time, Gurudev was in paath. After waiting a bit,
I sought Mataji's permission to pay my respects at his feet before
leaving. She agreed, and I left soon after. As I crossed a vast open
field near their home en route to the bus stop, I heard children,
including Gurudev's son, rushing towards me. They shouted,
'Doctor Uncle, Gurudev is calling you. Return immediately.'"

When Shankarnarayan ji returned, Gurudev said, "Your touch
drew me back from an astral meeting. Had I not called you back,
you might have been in an accident or killed on the way because
the ganns¹ at the astral meeting were furious with you for the
unexpected disruption."

When Shankarnarayan ji touched Gurudev's feet while the
mahaguru was in paath, it instantly connected Gurudev's physical
form and his astral form, which was then participating in an astral
meeting with the ganns. This unexpected connection prompted
the mahaguru to quickly return to his body, interrupting the
astral meeting. The ganns, disturbed by this disruption, turned
their anger towards Shankarnarayan ji. Nonetheless, Gurudev
intervened just in time to protect him.
