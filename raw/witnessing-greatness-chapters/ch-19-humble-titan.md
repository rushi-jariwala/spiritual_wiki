    Chapter 19
    Humble Titan





Navigating the seas of humility when millions revere and
worship you is no small feat. When placed on pedestals of power,
many succumb to the intoxication of arrogance and egotism. Yet,
Gurudev stands as a remarkable exception to this norm.

Reflecting on the mahaguru's demeanour, Suresh Prabhu ji notes,
"Gurudev never sought the limelight. While the world boasts of
many gurus, meeting one like Gurudev, who never claimed to be
a guru or showcased his abilities to impress, is rather rare."

Nitin Gadekar ji echoes Suresh ji and paints a vivid picture of
Gurudev, highlighting the contrasts between him and some
conventional spiritual leaders: "Among the many attributes that
made Gurudev stand apart, his commitment to living a routine
life was particularly striking. While I harbour no criticism for
spiritual leaders who choose ashram life, Gurudev was different.
He preferred the simplicity of his home over the grandeur of an
ashram. Unlike many gurus who adorn themselves in saffron
and project a certain image, he shied away from such displays.
He never wore religious beads, and you could often find him
casually smoking a cigarette in public view. He seemed more
intent on avoiding attention than attracting it. Yet, underlying all
his actions was immense compassion."

     had printed pamphlets titled 'Gurudev: The Ultimate Truth' and
     placed them on vehicles parked around the sthan. "When they

     this the car Gurudev frequently uses?' I replied, 'Indeed, Gurudev

     pamphlet on it.'"

     While driving Gurudev back from the farm the following day,
     Pehelwanji shared the events from the previous day. Gurudev
     nodded and said, "Son, I do not appreciate such publicity either."

     Like his guru, Buddhe Baba, the mahaguru held that keeping a
     low profile was crucial for steering clear of unwanted attention
     and remaining focused on his spiritual mission.

While Gurudev occasionally permitted photographs, he did
so with an understanding of humanity's need for a tangible
connection. Many sought his photograph to feel his presence,
communicate, and worship. Though they could sense his power,
they often wanted a physical representation to reaffirm their
faith. However, Gurudev remained modest, steering clear of any
deification and always wary of adoration or undue prominence.

        •

Speaking of his junior at work, Pratap Singh ji paints a portrait
of an unassuming man: "His spiritual abilities were hidden from
most. He lived in simplicity, and his compassion was limitless.
Whenever he learnt of someone in need, he would discreetly
extend his help in any way possible."

FC Sharma ji, aligning with Pratap ji's insights, presents a
striking juxtaposition of Gurudev's demeanour in personal
and professional realms. He observes, "Beyond the professional
sphere, Gurudev was held in high spiritual esteem, yet within the
workplace, he was particularly conscious of formalities."

He elaborates, "Gurudev sought permission before entering
his superiors' office and addressed them with deference as 'Sir'.
What stands out is the remarkable scenario where Gurudev's
seniors, who revered him for his spiritual guidance outside work,
witnessed him observing strict office protocols as their junior."

Gurudev epitomised 'devoted to devotion'—a phrase that could
well be his legacy. He sensed its delicate nuances as well as its
fervour. At a camp in Bathri, a unique white flower on a bush
caught my eye. While fetching it involved a bit of trepidation and
rock climbing, I silently laid it at his feet during his paath, and
discreetly left to avoid drawing attention. Hours later, when he
beckoned me, I found him appreciating the flower. His knowing
gaze met mine, leaving words superfluous.

The mahaguru was known to bend his own rules to honour
devotion occasionally. When an elderly and frail devotee brought
him home-made gulab jamuns, the mahaguru, who typically
did not accept food paid for by others, graciously accepted the
offering, humbly likening it to "Shabari ke ber."¹

Gurudev lived under constant scrutiny from other powers. It
was vital for him to emanate strength and remain unemotional.
Mastery in roleplay was essential, ensuring he was always several
steps ahead despite the challenges looming. Thus, his actions
often took unexpected turns, a testament to his strategic insight
and constant awareness.

Giri ji recalls, "One night in Gurgaon, Gurudev abruptly entered
my room at 3 am. Rousing me from sleep, he insisted we
immediately depart for Sonipat. En route, I learnt of an accident
involving Gurudev's family—his two daughters, nephew, and
nephew's wife. Upon our arrival in Sonipat, we discovered his
elder daughter, Renu ji, had fractured ribs, while his younger
daughter, Ila ji, had suffered a broken shoulder. Seeing them
in tears, he gently chided, 'Why the tears? Remember, you are
daughters of a guru'. Seeking to comfort Mataji, he reassuringly
said, 'Master, they will be fine'. Our visit was brief, and we soon
exited Santlal ji's residence."

"While revered by many as a mahaguru, Gurudev never let his
status overshadow his respect for elders," Nikku ji says, reflecting
on the mahaguru's sense of protocol. "His deference to his elder
cousin, Kishan ji from Gandhinagar, was evident. The respect he
showed my father was unbelievable. I remember him discreetly
hiding his cigarette when my father was around, jokingly saying,
'Paaji¹ should not see I smoke.'"

Nikku ji's father, Rudra ji, cherished his brother-in-law but was
oblivious to the depth of his spiritual powers. While many yearn
for acknowledgement, Gurudev was a study in duality, seamlessly
and subtly managing two facets of his life.

Rudra ji recalls, "He appeared so ordinary to me. Rajpal ji once
probed if I knew of his spiritual side. I replied that to me, he was
just my brother-in-law. He never flaunted his supposed miracles
or behaved like a big shot. When he visited Ludhiana, he always
regarded me as a senior and would always heed my advice. His
respect for me was unparalleled."

True greatness is inherently self-aware and does not seek to
display its brilliance. Gurudev's humility was genuine and
natural, emanating effortlessly from his being. Despite his deep

engage in self-reflection and enhance their qualities.
