# Analogies

Master collection of analogies and metaphors used across the Hingori Sutras tradition. Organised by concept cluster. Each entry notes what the analogy illuminates, the source quote, and backlinks.

---

## Karma & Accounting

### The Karmic Balance Sheet
*Illuminates: [[concepts/karma]], [[concepts/sanchit-karma]], [[concepts/jivaatma]]*

> "The sum total of the assets and liabilities of your personal karmic balance sheet is called sanchit karma. The profits and losses are both carried forward to the balance sheet where you cannot deduct one from the other."
> — Hingori, *Karma Sutra*, Ch.1, p.4

Every karma creates a ledger entry. Assets (positive karma) and liabilities (negative karma) are tracked separately — they cannot cancel each other out. The jivaatma carries this balance sheet across lifetimes.

---

### The Judge and the Murderer
*Illuminates: [[concepts/doer-ship]], [[concepts/karma]], [[concepts/non-doership]]*

> "A judge sentencing a murderer to the gallows has a different karma and does not pay for it, but the murderer does. Both performed a similar act — both responsible for someone's death. The difference is that the judge is doing his duty based on the rules of the land. He has no emotional connect or personal agenda against the convict. He does not take ownership for his act."
> — Hingori, *Karma Sutra*, Ch.1, pp.1–2

Two identical outward acts, two entirely different karmic consequences. Karma does not bind to the act — it binds to the ownership claimed over it. The judge's exemption rests on three conditions: duty, no emotional connect, no personal agenda.

---

### Life as a Movie
*Illuminates: [[concepts/sanchit-karma]], [[concepts/prarabdh-karma]], [[concepts/jivaatma]]*

Hingori's extended metaphor: life is a movie made long ago, witnessed in the present. The sanchit karma is the screenplay, assembled from accumulated karmic data across lifetimes. Prarabdh is the reel playing in this life. The actors — those with strong positive sanchit — are born into roles of high morality, philanthropy, or spiritual intuition (Krishna, Mother Teresa, Raja Janak). The script includes all the interactions in which old debts are squared off.

*(Karma Sutra, Ch.1 — paraphrase; no single condensed quote)*

## The Guru

### Winning the Lottery
*Illuminates: [[concepts/guru-disciple]]*

> "Getting a sadguru is like winning a lottery."
> — Hingori, *Karma Sutra*, Ch.1, p.19

A sadguru is not a given. The fortune of finding one who takes full responsibility for a disciple's enlightenment is of that order of rarity.

---

### The Umbrella
*Illuminates: [[concepts/guru-disciple]]*

> "The blessings of the guru may not avert the rain of bad luck, but it could provide an umbrella, by neutralising the negativity of the planetary effects. By getting attached to the aura of the disciple, his ability to withstand the negative rays gets enhanced."
> — Hingori, *Karma Sutra*, Ch.1, p.21

The guru does not usually cancel the disciple's destiny — the rain still comes. What changes is the disciple's capacity to pass through it. Two live examples: [[stories/gurus-umbrella-wife-delivery]], [[stories/scooter-accident-vision]].

## Mind & Kleshas

### The CCTV Camera
*Illuminates: [[concepts/kleshas]], [[concepts/non-doership]], [[practices/karmic-worksheet]]*

> "It was like putting up a CCTV camera outside me to note what kleshas were afflicting me, when and how. Whenever this imaginary camera was on, I became aware of being a victim of one or a combination of kleshas. I would laugh at myself and the strength of the klesha would fade."
> — Hingori, *Karma Sutra*, Ch.7, p.114

Witnessing one's own mind from the outside — as a CCTV observer, not as its subject. Used both for overcoming kleshas and as the principle underlying the daily karmic worksheet. Awareness itself weakens the klesha's grip.

---

### The Seesaw
*Illuminates: [[concepts/kleshas]] (raga/dwesa)*

> "It is only at the centre of a seesaw that emotions are at equilibrium. There is no joy at the centre, but then there is also no sadness. If you want to reduce sorrow, you must first reduce joy."
> — Hingori, *Karma Sutra*, Ch.7

Raga (attraction) and dwesa (aversion) are the two ends of the same seesaw. Swinging toward either end guarantees an equal swing back to the other. Equanimity — the aspiration of the sattvic — is not joy but the still centre where neither end dominates.

---

### The Glass and the River
*Illuminates: [[concepts/kleshas]] (asmita), [[concepts/maya]], [[concepts/jivaatma]]*

> "Asmita... is the glass that identifies the water of a river with a separate identity."
> — Hingori, *Karma Sutra*, Ch.7, p.108

The jivaatma is like water scooped into a glass: temporarily enclosed, identifying as "this glassful" rather than as the river it came from. Asmita is the glass — the ego-boundary that creates the illusion of a separate self. The water has not changed; only the container creates the sense of division.

## Samskars & Causal Body

### The USB Stick and USB Port
*Illuminates: [[concepts/samskars]], [[concepts/kaarna-sharir]]*

> "The karmashaya or the USB stick is attached to the USB port called the kaarna sharir or causal body. This is our third body, below the astral body (sukshma sharir). The kaarna sharir and its karmashaya pass from one incarnation to another, and become the storehouse of our conditioning, our samskars."
> — Hingori, *Karma Sutra*, Ch.8, p.130

The karmashaya (samskar storehouse) is the USB stick; the kaarna sharir (causal body) is the port it plugs into. Both pass through death intact. The goal: exhaust data from the stick over successive lives until it approaches blank — which would be moksha.

---

### The To-Do List
*Illuminates: [[concepts/samskars]], [[concepts/vasna]]*

> "Your list of things to do for today are your samskars. The motivation to do them is your vasna and fulfilling the list is your karma. A dash of emotion or a sense of doer-ship converts them back into samskars."
> — Hingori, *Karma Sutra*, Ch.8, p.129

Samskars are not vague impressions — they are specific, encoded tasks awaiting completion, like a to-do list written in a past life. Vasna is what makes you actually want to do them. Fulfilling each item exhausts that stored data — unless doer-ship re-files it as a new task.

---

### Paap ka Ghada (The Pot of Sin)
*Illuminates: [[concepts/samskars]], [[concepts/negative-karma]]*

> "He used to say 'Everybody's Paap ka Ghada has to get filled up'. (Your pot of sin needs to get filled.)"
> — Hingori reporting Gurudev's words, *Karma Sutra*, Ch.8, p.131

Negative samskars fructify faster than positive ones. The pot fills before it empties, which is why most people encounter bursts of negativity even amid spiritual progress. Gurudev used this phrase to explain why even sincere seekers go through difficult stretches — the negative karmic data surfaces and burns off faster by design.

## Liberation & Practice

### Plastering a Wall
*Illuminates: [[concepts/non-doership]], [[concepts/positive-karma]], [[concepts/negative-karma]]*

> "If you can give up ownership of karma, do it. If you can't, take the easier path and just plaster your negative karmas with an overload of positive ones, so that in relative comparison they are much less and you can have a better life or lives in the future."
> — Hingori, *Karma Sutra*, Ch.5, p.90

The cracks (negative karma) cannot be removed — they can only be covered over with enough plaster (positive karma) that they cease to dominate the surface. Plastering is the fallback path for those who cannot yet achieve full non-doership. It does not break the samskar loop, but it changes the texture of future lives.

---

### Cricket Practice
*Illuminates: [[concepts/non-doership]]*

> "Like cricket, it needs rehearsals and years of practice. But people have reached there, so why not you?"
> — Hingori, *Karma Sutra*, Ch.5, p.90

Full non-doership — the stature of the Buddha — is not an overnight achievement. It is a skill developed through repeated practice over many lives, like mastering a sport. The aspiration is not dismissed as impossible; it is reframed as a long-form discipline. People have reached there.
