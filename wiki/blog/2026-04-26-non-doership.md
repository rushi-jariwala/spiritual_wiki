Thirty years ago, I sat alone in a room, a meat chopper in my hand, consumed by guilt over having slapped a domestic worker. In a fit of remorse, I was planning to chop off my own hand as punishment. In that moment of anguish, a voice reached me: *"Who are you to blame me? You are my painting. I am the artist. By blaming yourself, you are blaming me."* *(→ [[stories/hingori-meat-chopper-guilt|full account]])*

That moment became the seed of one of the most liberating ideas I have encountered on the spiritual path — non-doership.

**What Is Non-Doership?**

Every action we perform in this life is the fructification of karma accumulated in previous lives. It is the fruit of a tree whose roots were planted long before this birth. When that fruit falls — whether sweet or bitter — we are not its gardeners; we are merely present when it ripens. The trouble begins the moment we claim ownership. That ownership is what I call doer-ship *(→ [[concepts/doer-ship]])*,  and it is precisely the mechanism by which fresh karma is generated and the cycle continues. Non-doership, therefore, is the practice of withdrawing that ownership — not as an intellectual position we agree with in theory, but as something embedded deep enough in the mind to become a way of living.

**The Judge and the Murderer**

To understand this more clearly, consider a judge who sentences a murderer to death, and the murderer himself — both have been instrumental in a man's death, yet their karmic consequence is entirely different. The judge acted without emotional connect, without personal agenda, doing only what his duty required. He did not take the act home with him. The murderer did. Karma does not bind to the act itself — it binds to the ownership we extend over it *(→ [[concepts/karma]])*. This is equally true for us in everyday life: the same action, coloured differently by ownership, creates entirely different karmic consequences.

**Nipping Guilt in the Bud**

I recall how Gurudev demonstrated this in practice. His disciple Giri once came to him deeply embarrassed, having had an indecent dream that he felt was contrary to the spiritual path. Gurudev listened patiently, told him not to feel guilty, and said such occurrences would stop shortly. Nothing more. Gurudev understood that if Giri took ownership of a thought he had neither invited nor controlled, the guilt would crystallise into a samskar — a future obligation. The thought would pass; the guilt would remain and compound. By withdrawing the ownership, Gurudev removed the problem at its root *(→ [[stories/giri-embarrassing-dream|Giri's Embarrassing Dream]])*. The lesson is plain: what arises in your mind unbidden is not yours to carry.

**A Shortcut Worth Knowing**

Understanding this is one thing; practicing it moment to moment is another. I myself found the concept too abstract to apply in each waking moment, and so I devised a prayer: *"From the last time to this time and from this time until the next, I have not been the doer of any actions nor do I take any responsibility for any of my thoughts."* This nullifies past misunderstandings and pre-empts future ones in a single breath *(→ [[stories/hingori-non-doership-shortcut|the full shortcut]])*. I drew my inspiration from Lord Ganapati, who simply circled his parents rather than circumnavigating the universe and declared both equivalent — and Lord Shankar accepted the logic. If the consciousness supreme can accept a sincere shortcut, so can we.

Spend five minutes a day reciting "I am not the doer." Make it a prayer. *People have reached there, so why not you?*
