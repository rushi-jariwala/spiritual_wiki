Over a century ago, someone whose name I do not know helped my grandfather complete his high school education. That single act — modest, unremarkable from the outside — set in motion a chain I am still tracing. My grandfather rose to become the general manager of an insurance company before it was nationalised. He helped educate every member of his family and extended kin. My father set up a free school for differently abled children in Mumbai and Delhi; several hundred have studied there since. My sister received a Rotary Club scholarship, studied in America, built a career in television, and in turn trained others who built careers of their own. A portion of the revenue of an art hotel in Bandra today goes to a trust that funds the education of village children in its vicinity — children who might otherwise remain illiterate. That unknown benefactor had no idea. He gave something small and went home.

**What Seva Actually Is**

I have thought about this story often, because it illuminates what Gurudev meant by *seva* in a way that no definition quite manages. We tend to think of seva as charity — the strong giving to the weak. But Gurudev's definition was something else entirely: *service by the spiritual self to the same spiritual self in others*. Every jivaatma — every individual soul — is a fragment of the same consciousness supreme. When you serve another, you are not descending to assist someone lesser. You are one fragment of the divine meeting another, in the costume of duality, recognising what is shared beneath it.

*(→ [[practices/seva]])*

The compounding I described in my grandfather's story is not accidental — it is structural. Seva, as I wrote in *Aatma Sutra*, "works like a multilevel marketing concept. There are benefits for those who do the seva directly, and for those who are indirect facilitators for the same." The mathematics of it is the point. My grandfather's benefactor earned karmic credit not just for the help he gave directly, but for every downstream act that gift made possible — the school, the scholarship, the trust, the village children still reading because of him a century later. A single tree bears fruit in one location; an orchard multiplies the yield without the farmer needing to be present for every harvest.

**The Rule That Must Not Be Broken**

Understanding seva's compounding power makes the Golden Rule easier to grasp: never accept any return — monetary or otherwise — for your service, or the seva's karmic accrual is nullified. Accepting payment converts the act into a transaction. Claiming mental credit does the same, in subtler form. Gurudev demonstrated this in a way I have never forgotten.

A Sikh gentleman once came to see him, hoping to be permanently relieved of a chronic headache. Gurudev cured him immediately, asking only that he abstain from non-vegetarian food and alcohol. Two weeks later, the man returned carrying a briefcase containing one lakh rupees in cash. He prostrated before Gurudev and implored him to accept it as a token of gratitude. Gurudev looked him in the eye and said, *"As soon as I touch the briefcase, your headache will return. It is my duty to do seva, not my business."* The briefcase went home unopened.

**The Direction of Gratitude**

There is one further teaching that I found genuinely surprising when Gurudev first offered it. He always reminded us to be thankful to those who let us serve them — not the reverse. Because without someone willing to receive, the act cannot happen. The recipient does the server a quiet spiritual favour by accepting. This thought, held long enough, dissolves the entire doer-receiver hierarchy.

Gurudev lived it entirely. He barely slept, spending mornings with patients before the office and evenings with whoever needed him. Four or five times a month, he refused to eat. When his devoted companion Bittu ji pressed him about neglecting his health, Gurudev sat him down and said, *"I may be physically present here in Gurgaon, but if any member of my spiritual family anywhere in the world goes to bed hungry, I do not eat that night."*

*(→ [[stories/gurudev-not-eating-hungry-disciples]])*

He summed it up in seven words: *"Seva mein he mewa hai."* The fruit is in the giving itself.

Begin today with something small. The chain you start will compound in ways you will never see.
