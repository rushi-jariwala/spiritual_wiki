Back in the days when I would meditate for hours through the night, something happened that I still find difficult to explain in ordinary language. On opening my eyes during one such session, I saw a ray of light emerge from one end of the room and travel to a point a few inches above the centre of my forehead. It hit that spot, dispersed into my head, and a thought arrived — perfectly clear, as if delivered rather than generated. I had, quite literally, seen a thought enter me from outside.

I stayed with that image for a long time afterward. Because it overturned something I had believed for most of my life: that my thoughts were mine, that they rose from within me, and that their character — the unworthy ones, the obsessive ones, the petty and occasionally shameful ones — said something damning about who I was.

**The Third Body and Its Particular Mischief**

Of the five sheaths that surround the jivaatma, the manomaya kosha is the third — and in some ways the most consequential. It is the body of the mind: the seat of thoughts, emotions, memory, attachment, identity. It stores the samskars of several lifetimes. Every decision, every craving, every creative inspiration and every jealous twinge happens here. And it has a very particular habit: it takes what passes through it and claims it as "I."

> "Unfortunately, this body keeps a person oblivious of his/her oneness with the Supreme Consciousness and creates a false identification of 'I am this' and 'I am that'. It is a kind of villain in the generation of happiness or unhappiness, both being individual perceptions."
> — Hingori, *Karma Sutra*, Ch.7, p.116

The classical term for this habit is *asmita* — the faculty of ego-identification. Picture the water of a river scooped into a glass. The water has not changed in substance — it is the same river, the same consciousness — but the glass now identifies itself as a separate entity with a particular volume, temperature, and name. Asmita is the glass. The manomaya is where that glass is maintained, polished, and mistaken — lifetime after lifetime — for the water itself.

**What Thirty Thousand Arrivals Tell Us**

The night I saw that ray of light was the most practically useful piece of spiritual education I ever received. Consider what we know: on average, around thirty thousand thoughts pass through a human mind each day. Of those, somewhere between twenty and sixty per cent are — to put it plainly — unworthy. For most of my life, I drew the obvious conclusion.

> "Out of an average of 30,000 thoughts received by a human mind per day, about 20–60 percent are unworthy. Does that make me unworthy? For most of my life, I believed in the affirmative. Today I believe otherwise."
> — Hingori, *Aatma Sutra*, Ch.III, p.23

The ray changed that conclusion entirely. If thoughts are received rather than composed, the mind is a radio, not a composer. A radio picks up whatever signals its environment is broadcasting — beautiful music, static, content it did not request and would not have chosen. It is not responsible for what arrives. It has no reason to feel proud or embarrassed about the broadcast. The manomaya's binding power rests almost entirely on our willingness to sign for the delivery — to accept a thought marked "yours" and carry it as evidence of character. We could, instead, note its arrival and set it down at the door.

**The Seesaw Within**

Beyond thoughts, the manomaya is the seat of *raga* and *dwesa* — attraction and aversion — the two forces that keep the mind perpetually oscillating. I have come to think of this as a seesaw. When you push one end down, the other rises with equal and unavoidable force. Joy and sorrow are not opposites that can be separately chosen; they are the two ends of the same plank. Pursue more joy, and the mechanism guarantees more sorrow in return — not as punishment, but as physics.

This is why even pleasant emotions, held in the manomaya's grip, are another form of bondage. They feel like profit; they function like debt. True equanimity — what the more evolved temperament aspires to — is not the high end of the seesaw but the still centre, the fulcrum, the point where neither end dominates. That centre is not joy. It is something quieter and far more stable: the bliss of the manomaya no longer driving the motion.

**The Long Work of Rebuilding**

Understanding the manomaya's mechanisms in theory is one thing. Watching them dismantle, and then reassemble, a self-image over the course of years is quite another. For a long time I carried a picture of myself that the manomaya had assembled with great efficiency: raw, selfish, complex, unenviable. A well-manicured fool, as I described it. The comparison to Gurudev — his stillness, his scope, his effortless command of any room he entered — only made the image more damning with each meeting.

Gurudev did not argue with the picture. He did not offer reassurance or comfort. He simply fed a different signal into the same receiver, again and again, across years. *"I know what you are and so will you,"* he would say. And most often: *"A horse gives birth to a horse. A son of a guru will be a guru."* I understood the words intellectually. I could not feel their truth. The manomaya had been running the old construction for too long and trusted it more than it trusted him.

But his empathy was patient and his confidence was absolute. He held a vision of what was actually stored in the jivaatma beneath the manomaya's layers — and he simply waited for those layers to become less convincing than his vision. Eventually they did. I wrote a book. In fact, more than one. The manomaya that had filed me under "fool" was the same instrument that eventually carried the gyan outward. The villain, pressed far enough, becomes the vehicle.

Watch what the mind says about you today. Note how much of it arrived uninvited, and how quickly you signed for it.
