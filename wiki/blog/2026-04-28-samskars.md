Travelling with Gurudev to one of his official camps in Himachal Pradesh, I confessed on the road that I suffered from a lot of negative thoughts and asked for help. He smiled — not the warm, reassuring kind. It was a wicked smile.

We got into the jeep and the bumpy mountain road began. What followed I cannot easily describe. Strange thoughts of Gurudev urinating, of myself behaving badly with him, thoughts profane and worse — one after the other, one worse than the other, at phenomenal speed. *It felt like a thousand years had passed by the time we reached our destination.*

I went to bed straightaway, too awkward to sit in his presence. The next morning, I woke to crispness in the air, the smell of greenery and soil. Fewer thoughts. More observations. A quiet I had not known in years. The *vitarks* had faded away — probably because of their over-exhaustion the evening before.

Gurudev had not consoled me. He had triggered the release. He knew that what I had come with was not my problem — it was my data.

**What Is Stored in the Stick**

Those thoughts were not coming from outside of me. They were not a moral failing. They were stored impressions — *samskars* — surfacing from the karmashaya, the storehouse of accumulated karma from this and previous lifetimes.

The karmashaya is a USB stick. The causal body — your third body, the *kaarna sharir* — is the USB port it plugs into. Through death and rebirth, the port changes; the stick survives. Everything you have accumulated across lifetimes is stored there: smruti (the short-term memory of this life) and samskars proper, the long-term impressions that span incarnations.

> "The karmashaya or the USB stick is attached to the USB port called the kaarna sharir or causal body. This is our third body, below the astral body. The kaarna sharir and its karmashaya pass from one incarnation to another, and become the storehouse of our conditioning, our samskars."
> — Hingori, *Karma Sutra*, Ch.8, p.130

When you think of samskars as encoded data rather than as character flaws, the whole machinery of karma becomes cleaner. Your tendencies are not who you are. They are who you were. More precisely, they are what you felt ownership over and attached an emotion to.

**The To-Do List**

Think of it this way: your list of things to do for today are your samskars. The motivation to do them is your *vasna*, and fulfilling the list is your karma.

> "Your list of things to do for today are your samskars. The motivation to do them is your vasna and fulfilling the list is your karma. A dash of emotion or a sense of doer-ship converts them back into samskars."
> — Hingori, *Karma Sutra*, Ch.8, p.129

Every samskar is specific. It is not a vague cloud of past impressions — it is an encoded task. Something that fructified once, was claimed by the "I" within, and filed back under "to revisit." The to-do list refills itself.

The formation condition is exact: emotion plus ownership. An act performed without either leaves no samskar. A judge who sentences a murderer does not accumulate karma for the execution — no emotional connect, no personal agenda. The act passes through him clean. The murderer pays; the judge does not. Same outward event, entirely different karmic consequence. The difference is not in the deed. It is in the "I" that claims it.

**The Wicked Smile**

Understanding this mechanism is one thing; watching a master wield it is another.

Giri, a devout follower from Gurgaon, came to Gurudev one day with an embarrassed confession. He had had a sensual dream. He confessed it the way a student confesses a test failure — expecting the verdict of shame. Gurudev was empathetic. He told Giri not to feel guilty, that these things would stop shortly after. And they did. *By saying so, he averted Giri's embarrassment from getting converted into a samskar.*

There was another follower who came with something even more distressing — sensual dreams about his own mother. He was emotionally traumatised. Gurudev told him not to think of it anymore, and it would never happen again. And it did not.

This is the precision of a mahaguru's intervention. He was not addressing the dream. He was addressing the *reaction* to the dream — the guilt, the ownership, the assumption that "this must be who I am." That reaction, if allowed to solidify, would have become a new samskar. The vitark had surfaced to exhaust old data; guilt would have re-encoded it as new data. Gurudev interrupted the circuit before it could complete.

**Three Destinations**

Not all stored data leads to the same place. The *Aatma Sutra* offers the simplest map of where samskars go when they fructify:

> "**Golden Rule:** The positive *samskars* stored will be converted in the future into *bhog* (pleasure and riches). The negative *samskars* into *rog* (debt, disease, and misfortune). The *samskars* related to spirituality and divinity will get converted into *yog* (spiritual evolution). Aim for *yog*!"
> — Hingori, *Aatma Sutra*, Ch.III, p.32

*Bhog* is not bad — it is the harvest of good worldly karmas. But it is expenditure, not investment. You enjoy, and the balance decreases. *Rog* is the debt that surfaces: suffering that exhausts what is owed. *Yog* is the only category that advances liberation. What you store now, in whichever form, determines where the next life opens.

Gurudev used to say: *"Everybody's Paap ka Ghada has to get filled up."* The pot of sin fills before it empties, because negative samskars fructify faster than positive ones. This is not pessimism — it is engineering. If you know the pot fills faster, you plan accordingly. You do not wait for it to overflow; you begin emptying it now.

**The Next-Time Syndrome**

There is a particular samskar-formation pathway worth watching for in yourself. When we fail at something and attach guilt or shame to the failure, the guilt itself becomes a new samskar — not the failure, but the guilt. An impression of incompleteness is filed under "pending," and it pulls the jivaatma toward another incarnation to resolve it. The failure passes; the guilt remains.

> "We must allow ourselves to fail and accept our failures gracefully otherwise it will too form samskars and add to the burden of the 'next time' syndrome."
> — Hingori, *Karma Sutra*, p.141

This is why graceful acceptance of failure is not weakness — it is efficient. The act failed. That is enough data. Adding guilt merely books the sequel.

**Exhaust More Than You Add**

The aspiration is not to empty the USB stick completely — that would be moksha, and only the most evolved arrive there. The realistic goal is to exhaust more than you add. Around 30 to 40 per cent reduction should be achievable in a single life, because 100 per cent success is a myth for anyone still living in the world. Each life lightens the stick a little. Over enough lives, spiritual ballooning becomes possible. You begin to float upward, not by effort, but by the removal of ballast.

Notice what I am saying, and what I am not saying. I am not saying stop having thoughts. I am not saying stop feeling. I am saying: when a thought arises, when a deed plays out — do not add the "I." Let it pass without the signature.

The morning after the Himachal jeep ride, the air smelled of soil and greenery. There were fewer thoughts and more observations. A quiet I had not previously earned.

That is where the stick empties: one unowned act at a time.
