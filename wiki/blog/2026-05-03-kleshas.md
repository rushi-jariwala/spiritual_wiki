I have five flatmates. They don't pay rent. They don't do the dishes. They have been living in my head — not just in this lifetime, but apparently in every single one that came before it.

I have died and been reborn. And somehow, every single time, they found the new address.

The *Yoga Sutras* of Patanjali calls them *kleshas*. I would call them five very specific types of person who have taken up permanent residence inside you, built their bedrooms, and have absolutely no intention of leaving.

We are born with them. Their patterns are pre-configured in the *karmashaya* — the storehouse of our accumulated karmic impressions from previous lives. The cosmic landlord wrote them into the tenancy before we arrived. We did not get to see the tenancy.

Let me introduce them properly.

---

**Flatmate 1: Avi**
*(Avidya — Misperception)*

At parties, Avi introduces himself as his job title, his car, and his three strongly-held opinions on things. On a difficult day, he introduces himself as his mood. He once introduced himself by mentioning his salary before his name.

Ask Avi to describe himself without any external reference — without the job, the status, the opinions, the things he owns or the things he has achieved — and he goes very quiet. He genuinely does not know. He has never been asked. He has never asked himself.

This is *avidya*: the fundamental misperception that you are the packaging rather than the contents. The confusion of the body, the personality, the roles — for the *aatma*, the actual self.

> *"Mistaking the non-aatma with the aatma leads to a chain of misconception."*
> — Hingori, *Karma Sutra*, Ch.7, p.107

He is the primary flatmate. The others would not exist without him — they build their bedrooms on his foundation. The Guru's purpose, etymologically, is to be the remover of this darkness. *Guru* means precisely that. Avi has been generating it for longer than either of us can account for.

**Flatmate 2: Asmi**
*(Asmita — Egoism)*

The meeting went well. "I had a feeling it would," says Asmi.

She once heard an idea at dinner, repeated it three days later with minor rephrasing, and by the end of that week had fully convinced herself she had come up with it. "I told you so" is not just how she responds to bad news — it is her love language. She gets quietly wounded when you recommend a film she had not discovered first. She keeps a mental record of everything she has said that turned out to be right — which, in her account, is most things.

> *"Asmita makes a person attached to material things, people and relationships. It also induces a person to claim ownership over ideas, concepts, experiences, viewpoints and beliefs. It is the glass that identifies the water of a river with a separate identity."*
> — Hingori, *Karma Sutra*, Ch.7, p.108

She is, at her core, a glass of river water that has convinced itself it is not river water.

The rest of the river has no name on it. Asmi finds this very suspicious.

**Flatmate 3: Rag**
*(Raga — Attachment/Indulgence)*

Rag says yes to everything.

The food delivery at 1am. The third watch-through of the show he has already seen twice. The party on a Tuesday when there is work at 8am on Wednesday. The extra helping. The extra *extra* helping.

But his deeper quality is not indulgence — it is attachment. Rag still listens to the album from a relationship that ended years ago. He has a jacket that has not fit him in three years — not since a summer that was particularly good — and he cannot throw it away. He is still following the social media of someone he has not spoken to in four years, just to keep track. He holds a certain old grudge with something close to warmth, because at least it is his grudge, and it is familiar.

> *"Raga leads a person to attachments, and attachments initiate different types of karmas."*
> — Hingori, *Karma Sutra*, Ch.7, p.109

He is not possessive, exactly. He just needs things to stay exactly as they are.

The karma incurred by Rag's decisions is expensive to pay. He will address that next life. He is quite sure of it.

**Flatmate 4: Dev**
*(Dvesha — Aversion)*

Dev is Rag's mirror image — which makes the flat an exceptionally tiring place.

Where Rag cannot stop clinging to things, Dev cannot stop resisting them. Mornings. That one colleague. The neighbour's dog. A specific type of weather. The way certain people hold pens. The font choice in someone's presentation. The number of times people say "actually" in meetings.

> *"My wife does not like eating Chinese food because she once fell sick after eating a Chinese meal. Her dislike is ingrained in her mind, and only she can overcome it."*
> — Hingori, *Karma Sutra*, Ch.7, p.111

Dev has many such cases. All of them feel entirely justified. None of them have been reviewed since the original incident.

The damage is not in the disliking. It is in what Dev misses while disliking. He has walked out of rooms that contained the answer. He has dismissed teachers because of their shoes. He has turned down opportunities that arrived in the wrong tone of voice.

He and Rag together ensure that the mind is permanently tilted. One tilts toward. One tilts away. They have never once met in the middle, and they never will.

> *"The two kleshas of attraction and aversion, likes and dislikes, pleasure and pain are impediments to the evolution of a person."*
> — Hingori, *Karma Sutra*, Ch.7, p.111

**Flatmate 5: Abhi**
*(Abhinivesha — Fear / Clinging to Life)*

Abhi has forty-seven insurance policies.

He has Googled his symptoms eleven times today. Current diagnoses: four separate terminal conditions and one vitamin deficiency that he has decided is the worst one. He will not board a plane. He has read every article about planes *not* crashing and found each one unconvincing.

His klesha is *abhinivesha* — clinging to life, fear of death, and all the fears that orbit those two like very anxious moons.

> *"Despite having faced death several times in the entirety of our external existence, it still remains our greatest fear. And instinctively so. This fear exists across species and in almost all human beings, even amongst the learned ones."*
> — Hingori, *Karma Sutra*, Ch.7, p.112

Abhi has died many times. In previous incarnations he has been, in various combinations, a fish, a king, a corrupt administrator, possibly a frog. He has died as all of them. The fear has survived every single one of those deaths — which is, philosophically, quite an achievement for something that was supposed to be irrational.

**The CCTV Option**

You cannot evict them. This has been attempted across civilisations and multiple philosophical traditions. It has not worked.

What I found to be the most practical method is an imaginary CCTV camera.

> *"I myself found out noticing my interactions with the kleshas from outside was the best approach. It was like putting up a CCTV camera outside me to note what kleshas were afflicting me, when and how. Whenever this imaginary camera was on, I became aware of being a victim of one or a combination of kleshas. I would laugh at myself and the strength of the klesha would fade."*
> — Hingori, *Karma Sutra*, Ch.7, p.114

Watch them. Do not engage them. When you catch Rag ordering the third delivery at 1am, simply note: *"Ah. That's Rag."* When Dev walks out of a room containing the answer, observe it. When Asmi quietly claims credit for how the evening went, let her claim it.

The klesha wanted your 'I'. You did not provide it. The strength of it fades.

You still live with them. But you have stopped feeding them.

That is where the work begins. The lease runs indefinitely. The camera, at least, is yours.
