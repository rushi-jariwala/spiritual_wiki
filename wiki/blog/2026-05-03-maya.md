The readers of the Ramayan have argued for centuries about what Lord Ram did at the battle of Kishkindha. Bali, the powerful Vanara king, carried an unusual boon: every warrior who faced him in combat would lose half their strength to him. Anyone who fought him face-to-face was already losing before the first blow landed. Ram's solution was to hide behind a tree and shoot an arrow into Bali's back.

Warriors did not do such things. It violated every norm of valour the age had ever written. Bali himself, dying on the ground, accused Ram of cowardice. And from inside the world we are all inhabiting, Bali was not wrong. *(→ [[stories/ram-kills-bali-behind-tree|Ram Kills Bali from Behind a Tree]])*

But Ram had been taught, by his teacher Guru Vashisht, that what looked like cowardice was only cowardice from inside the dream.

**What Maya Actually Is**

That encounter at Kishkindha points to a teaching Guru Vashisht had been planting in Ram for years: the doctrine of maya. The word itself is the teaching. *Karma Sutra* gives the simplest translation:

> "Maya in Sanskrit means 'that which is not'. It is a deluded state wherein each individual soul, under a sense of false identity, considers itself to be separate and distinct from the Supreme Consciousness and the rest of the creation."

This is not illusion in the thin, theatrical sense — a magician's trick we see through with a second look. It is something total and structural. Every experience we have — sight, sound, smell, thought, judgment — passes through five limited senses, is organised by a mind that classifies, and discriminated by an intellect that assigns meaning. We are working with a narrow slice of available reality and calling it the whole.

Consider the rainbow. I find it one of the clearest windows into what the ancient sages were pointing at. When we see colours arc across the sky after rain, the colours are not there. They are generated by the conical photoreceptor cells inside the human eye and interpreted by the brain. An animal without those cells sees the same sky in grey. *"When you 'see a rainbow', the colours are not in the sky, they are in your eyes and assimilated by your brain."* The most vivid thing in the sky is a co-production of the sky and whoever is looking at it.

Science has extended this further than we usually care to follow. What the naked human eye can see is less than 0.00000000005 per cent of the entire electromagnetic spectrum. The atoms composing your body are 99.9999999999999% empty space. Quantum physics has demonstrated that the observed universe is inseparable from the observer — a world independent of any observer is itself a fiction. As I wrote in *Aatma Sutra*:

> "Is science finally catching up? It will not be long before science proves that the observer, the observed, and the observation are all a part of the same consciousness."

**The Machinery of Duality**

To understand why this matters for daily life, we need to follow what maya generates. Accepting the sensory world as the whole produces a framework of duality: right and wrong, good and bad, victory and defeat, courageous and cowardly. This framework is not chosen — it arrives with the body. And within it, all karma is written. The positive and negative ledger of our actions is kept entirely in duality's ink. *(→ [[concepts/karma]])*

I use a precise image for this process of clouding. Pure water, through countless small adulterations — coffee, milk, sugar — becomes something that barely resembles its origin. Our original consciousness undergoes the same gradual obscuring: through possessions, identifications, social roles, and decades of judgments passed on ourselves and others.

> "What we perceive, we believe. For us that is reality. So is every mirage until we discover otherwise."

The remarkable teaching embedded here is that the recognition of adulteration already begins to reverse it. Noticing that you are water temporarily coloured by coffee — and not something that has permanently become coffee — is the first movement toward purity. Maya has within it the seed of its own undoing: *"Maya is thus an epiphany that has the power to liberate the soul."*

**Ram's Answer**

To see what lies on the other side of duality, I return to Lord Ram — not to Kishkindha, but to a conversation with his teacher years later. Guru Vashisht asked Ram directly: what is your inner experience now?

*"By your grace, I have attained supreme purity, as all the impurities have cleared away. I behold the world as pure, infinite, and indivisible consciousness. The world is otherwise a void, which disappears the moment illusion vanishes. I shall do whatever you wish I should do, and I shall live doing whatever I have to do, without exultation or depression, for my delusion has been dispelled."*

*(→ [[stories/ram-speech-to-guru-vashisht|Ram's Speech to Guru Vashisht]])*

This is not poetry. Ram is describing a condition. He will still rule the kingdom. He will still act, fully. But the pendulum of elation and despair that most of us experience as normal has, for him, stopped swinging. The duality that generates both guilt and pride has ceased to be real.

The same Ram, when a washerman questioned Sita's chastity after her captivity, asked his wife to leave the kingdom and live in the forest under Sage Valmiki's care. I will be honest: until I understood the Guru Vashisht teachings, I did not have much respect for this act. It looked neither wise as a king nor sensitive as a husband. But Ram did it without malice — and without guilt — because the 'I' that would have felt cruel, righteous, or vindicated was no longer running the machinery. He acted as the role required of him, and then moved on. *(→ [[stories/ram-banishes-sita|Ram Banishes Sita — Beyond Maya, Beyond Guilt]])*

**A Necessary Caution**

On reading this, there is a temptation to conclude that one may act however one pleases, attributing every convenient choice to maya. I want to address that directly: *"It is only when you reach a particular level of evolution can you practice such a philosophy."* Using maya as philosophical cover before arriving at Ram's actual state of consciousness is not liberation. It is convenience dressed as Vedanta — and an old mistake.

The path to Ram's condition is methodical. It runs through senses-management, through the daily practice of non-doership, through detachment built one action at a time, and — most reliably — through the grace of a genuine teacher. Maya is not dissolved by argument. It dissolves the way a mist dissolves at sunrise: gradually, through a quality of inner light that accumulates over time.

Notice the rainbow in your eye today. Stay with what that means.
