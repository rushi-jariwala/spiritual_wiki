Bittu ji was one of those disciples who kept the informal memory of Gurudev alive — the unrepeatable domestic moments that no formal discourse would ever preserve. One afternoon he told me a story I have never managed to retell without smiling.

Gurudev had joined a game of gully cricket with Bittu ji and a group of friends. He was at the crease when he missed a shot and the ball lodged itself inside his lungi. The bowler, confident in his LBW, appealed vehemently. Gurudev's response came without hesitation: *"Not Out!"* He refused to vacate the crease. The unfair lungi dance that followed made everyone dissolve into laughter. Gurudev played on.

*(→ [[stories/gurudev-gully-cricket-lungi|Gurudev and the Gully Cricket Lungi]])*

That story carries a teaching more concentrated than it first appears. Throughout his life, Gurudev wore many hats — guru, husband, father, son, brother, friend — *"all while acting out emotions unemotionally."* He played every role with complete investment. He argued the cricket appeal with the same presence he brought to the deepest teachings. And then, just as completely, he stepped out of the game. The role ended; the man remained.

**The Danger of Becoming the Character**

What Gurudev demonstrated so lightly on that cricket pitch has a darker counterpart: what happens when the actor cannot leave the stage.

We all inhabit roles — the attentive parent, the firm employer, the wounded friend. The question spiritual practice forces us to ask is whether we are playing the role or have, somewhere along the way, become it, and whether we could step back out if we tried.

*Aatma Sutra* names two actors to make the risk concrete. Marlon Brando played Don Corleone in *The Godfather* with such complete immersion that he needed six months of psychiatric care to find his way back. Rod Steiger played Napoleon in *Waterloo* with the same totality — and required the same intervention. Neither man could locate the door marked *exit*.

In ordinary life the same absorption happens over longer timescales. We sink into the role of the wronged spouse, the indispensable professional, the burdened parent — and over years, the role becomes the walls we live inside. What was once a function, chosen and inhabited for a season, has become the identity.

> "You can sometimes play the role that people want you to, depending on the circumstance or event. However, it is necessary to ensure you play the role with detachment, without emotion, and not believe it to be your reality."
> — *Aatma Sutra*, Ch.IX, p.108

That final instruction — *do not believe it to be your reality* — is the whole of the teaching. A role is not reality. It is a form, a function, a performance. Even when we play it sincerely, something at the interior must know the curtain can come down.

**The Cowshed Evening**

I sat with Gurudev one evening in the cowshed while he explained spiritual concepts to a small group of us. I had no idea I was about to witness the clearest demonstration of role-play I would ever see.

Billu arrived — a devotee we were all fond of, a man whose relationship with alcohol followed a pattern so reliable it had become almost routine. Gurudev would ask him to quit; Billu would promise; the promise would break; repeat. That evening Billu sauntered in smelling unmistakably of alcohol, and when he attempted to explain himself, I watched something shift in Gurudev's expression. What followed was the sharpest, most intense display of anger I had ever seen from my guru. The words were hard and direct. Both Billu and I cowered. When it was over, Billu left with his head down.

The moment he was out of sight, Gurudev turned to me with a smile and said:

*"Barkhurdar, kaisi lagi hamari acting?"* — My son, what did you think of my performance?

My jaw hit the floor.

*(→ [[stories/gurudev-billu-acting|Gurudev and Billu — "Kaisi Lagi Hamari Acting?"]])*

The anger had been absolutely real in its effect. Billu had felt it. I had felt it. But in Gurudev, not a trace of it remained the instant the purpose was served. The curtain had come down and he had walked off stage, clean. What looked from the outside like a justified fury was, from the inside, a tool picked up and set down.

That evening planted a seed I have been turning over for decades. Compassion at the highest level does not always look like tenderness. Sometimes it is a precisely measured fury deployed for the other person's benefit — by someone who remains entirely untouched. The distinction between that and cruelty is not visible in the anger itself. It lives only in the absence of personal investment underneath it.

**Acting Genuine**

To understand how Gurudev held this quality across every relationship — not in a single cowshed moment but over decades, with hundreds of people — requires understanding what was beneath the role-play. He expressed it himself in a formulation I have returned to many times:

> *"Main sab ka hoon aur sab mere hain.*
> *Lekin main kisi ka nahi hu aur mera koi nahi."*
>
> (I belong to all, and all belong to me.
> Yet I belong to no one, and no one belongs to me.)
> — Gurudev, *The Guru of Gurus*, p.108

This is not a contradiction. It is one state described from two directions. Because he belonged to no one in particular — because no one had a claim on his emotions — he could give himself entirely to everyone without running dry. His love was unlimited precisely because it was unowned.

All of us who followed him believed he was closest to us. I believed it too. We felt he cared for us more than our own parents ever did. What I came to understand much later was this: *"He loved everyone equally but without an iota of emotion. He was ready to relieve our pain, but not feel it."* He role-played affection to perfection. He manipulated us into believing he loved us more than the others — and the love was real in its effect, real in what it gave us. It simply had no emotional claim attached to it on his side.

*Aatma Sutra* points toward this as a direction we can travel:

> "Why not merely act genuine with empathy, instead of being genuine, and thereby avoid carrying the heavy baggage of involvement and emotions?"
> — *Aatma Sutra*, Ch.IX, p.109

*Acting genuine* is not dishonesty. It is sincerity without self-loss. You bring full warmth and understanding to the role — empathy ensures it is not mechanical — but you do not confuse the role with who you are. The parent who meets a child's pain with full attention, without being dissolved by it, serves the child better than the one who breaks down alongside them. The guru who performs fury and lays it down the moment it has served is more useful to Billu than one who genuinely rages and carries the weight of it for days.

Gurudev played many characters in his relationship with each of us: guru, father, mother, friend, brother — all in one, at different times. *"If you treated him like a guru, he behaved like one. If you gave him the role of a mother, he would indulge you. If you treated him like a friend, he would crack jokes and pull your leg."* He was none of these things permanently and all of them as needed. His identity remained intact beneath every hat.

When you play a role knowing it is a role, no emotion you perform leaves a mark on you. The anger burns and clears. The affection flows and releases. The duty is discharged without another layer added to who you believe yourself to be.

That is the practice: not distance, but freedom inside presence.
