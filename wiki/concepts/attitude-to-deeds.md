# Attitude to Deeds

> *"Many evolved souls give up their sense of doer-ship of deeds... Their deeds are considered non-actions and they are considered karma mukta or free of karmas."*
> — Hingori, *Karma Sutra*, Ch.7, p.104

## Definition

Hingori identifies three progressive attitudes with which a person can approach the doing of good karma. Each is valid; each places the practitioner at a different point of spiritual evolution. The sequence is not a judgement — it is a map.

## The Three Attitudes

### 1. The Self-centred Approach

Initially, people do good karma **because of the karmic benefits**. It is an investment for future gains. Several "holier-than-thou" have looked down upon this approach, but Hingori is clear: there is nothing wrong with it. If you know that good deeds beget good results, you cannot forget this when doing something beneficial for others.

This approach may not be the most evolved, but it builds a habit. The selfish attitude may benefit many, and in time the habit may outlast the motive.

### 2. Deeds for Satisfaction

When the habit of doing good forms, a **sense of satisfaction** takes over. People do not brag, but they feel good. Inspiring a smile through an act of service is itself reward enough. At a more evolved level this satisfaction matures into a sense of duty — one does good because it is simply what one does.

### 3. Deeds with Detachment

The most evolved attitude. Many evolved souls give up their sense of doer-ship entirely:

> They feel what is happening is destined. People do not feel pride or satisfaction in the good they do. Those people do not even try to qualify it as good. They simply rise above duality and stop seeing things as black or white. For them, doing someone a favour must be that person's fortune. They do not see it as their own positive karma, as they do not differentiate between the positive or negative. Their deeds are considered non-actions and they are considered *karma mukta* or free of karmas.
> — Hingori, *Karma Sutra*, Ch.7, p.104

The tough part is **not expecting gratitude and ignoring ingratitude**. For those who can rehearse their way to this achievement, giving up doer-ship becomes a distinct possibility.

## Relationship to Non-Doership

"Deeds with Detachment" is the lived practice of [[concepts/non-doership|non-doership]]. The abstract principle — take the 'I' away from deeds — becomes concrete here as the third attitude, the one that renders karma non-binding. This is what *karma mukta* means in daily practice.

## In Practice

- Stage 1 is where most people start. Do not dismiss it — let it build the habit.
- Stage 2 is where the habit becomes character.
- Stage 3 is where karma ceases to bind. It requires rehearsal — consistent practice over time, not a single act of will.
