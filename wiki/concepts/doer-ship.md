# Doer-ship and the Guilt Factor

> *The mechanism by which karma binds to the self — and why guilt is its most dangerous form.*

## Definition

**Doer-ship** is the claim (conscious or unconscious) to be the author of an action. In the Hingori framing, karma does not attach to the act itself but to the *ownership* taken of the act. Without doer-ship, no karmic entry is made on the [[concepts/jivaatma|jivaatma]]'s balance sheet.

**The guilt factor** is the book's term for the most common and damaging form of accidental doer-ship: feeling personally responsible for a negative act in a way that generates *new* negative karma on top of the old.

## Core Teaching

### The Judge Example

> A judge sentencing a murderer to the gallows has a different karma and does not pay for it, but the murderer does. Both performed a similar act — both responsible for someone's death. The difference is that the judge is doing his duty based on the rules of the land. He has no emotional connect or personal agenda against the convict. He does not take ownership for his act.
> — *Karma Sutra*, Ch.1, pp.1–2

This is the archetypal teaching on doer-ship. Two identical outward acts, two entirely different karmic consequences — because one actor takes ownership and the other does not.

### Guilt as Self-Inflicted Karma

> When we do positive deeds, we feel a sense of satisfaction, whereas negative deeds make us feel guilty and often we develop low self-esteem. **Guilt is a sure way of willing ourselves negative karmas by accepting the doer-ship of the action.** A better way to negate the guilt is to pile on a lot more good karmas to overshadow the negative ones.
> — *Karma Sutra*, Ch.1, p.8

Guilt is *not* a sign of moral progress. It is an active mechanism by which the seeker fastens themselves to the negative act and keeps its karmic weight alive on the ledger. Where an unclaimed negative act might have passed through as an unaccounted or minor entry, a *felt guilty* act becomes firmly owned — and compounds.

### Better Than Guilt: Accept, Forgive, Out-Act

> It will be wiser to accept your inadequacy and forgive yourself with a resolve not to disappoint again. This would be a far more positive way of correcting yourself rather than feeling guilty and holding a grudge against yourself.
> — *Karma Sutra*, Ch.1, p.8

The recommended response to moral failure is three-part:
1. **Accept inadequacy** — do not deny the act
2. **Forgive yourself** — do not fasten to it
3. **Resolve not to disappoint again** — redirect forward

Then: pile on positive karma in quantity.

## Nuances

### The Ego Trap of Good Deeds

Doer-ship is not only a hazard with negative acts. Positive acts, done with pride, also bind:

> Even doing good deeds can give a person pride in himself/herself. This too can fortify the sense of 'I' principle. The ego gets glorified and hence the gap widens.
> — *Karma Sutra*, Ch.1, p.12

This is the subtle form of the trap: the seeker who identifies as "good" or "spiritual" still owns the act, and so still accumulates. See [[concepts/maya]], [[concepts/moksha]].

### Hingori's Personal Practice

> I may not claim doer-ship. Whenever I have too much enjoyment or pleasure, the pain is not far behind. In such a case I go through a balancing act... I have opted to not allow joy to be a dominating emotion in my mind and thus bought an insurance against the emotion of sorrow.
> — *Karma Sutra*, Ch.1, p.25

Hingori describes a personal discipline of *withholding identification from joy itself*, not out of pessimism, but because the felt ownership of pleasure inevitably calls up a balancing pain. This is doer-ship as a moment-by-moment practice.

### Duty, Intent, and Emotional Distance

The judge is not exempt because the act is legal — he is exempt because (a) he is acting from duty, (b) he has no emotional connect, (c) he has no personal agenda. These three form the working definition of "without doer-ship" in practical terms.

## In Practice

- **Watch for the feeling of "I did this."** Both in triumph and in failure, that feeling is the moment the karma binds.
- **Refuse guilt.** Regret without identification is fine; guilt that fastens is karmic self-harm.
- **Do not collect virtue.** Let good acts pass through you without building a "good person" around them.
- **Withhold from pleasure, not to suffer, but to avoid the binding ownership of it.** Hingori's practice, not a requirement, but a striking illustration.

## Open Questions

> [!question] Can doer-ship be fully released?
> Is a fully non-doer state actually achievable for an embodied person, or is doer-ship something to be continuously reduced? The judge example suggests pockets of non-doership are possible, but the book's preface implies that total non-doership is the aspiration of the evolved and "almost impossible to achieve."
