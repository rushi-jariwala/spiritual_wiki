# Guru Dakshina

## Definition

*Guru dakshina* is the offering made by a student to a teacher in repayment of the karmic debt incurred through receiving teaching. It is the formal acknowledgment that knowledge received creates an obligation — and that the obligation must be honoured.

> Tann, mann, dhann or the body, mind and wealth are an offering the student makes to his preceptor as guru dakshina or repayment to guru.
> — Hingori, *Karma Sutra*, Ch.2, p.39

## The Three Forms

| Sanskrit | Meaning | Nature of Offering |
|---|---|---|
| **Tann** | Body | Physical service, presence |
| **Mann** | Mind | Attention, devotion, learning, carrying the teaching |
| **Dhann** | Wealth | Material offerings, financial support |

All three together constitute the fullest repayment. Each alone is partial.

## Why the Teacher's Debt is Special

Among the six categories of [[concepts/karmic-debt|karmic debt]], the guru's is the heaviest in one specific sense:

> The spiritual teacher gets the cream of the credit because he/she helps you first unlearn all the delusions and cleanses your slate before he/she can rewrite on it. The understanding of karma theory and practicing it at best is the result of his/her performance. It is an invaluable debt and cannot be measured.
> — *Karma Sutra*, Ch.2, p.39

The debt to a spiritual guru is therefore *qualitatively* different from the debt to a school teacher or a parent — it is the debt for being able to perceive reality correctly at all.

## Traditional and Extreme Examples

Hingori cites these not as recommendations but as illustrations of the *weight* of the concept:

- **Eklavya** — Guru Dronacharya asked him to repay by cutting off his right thumb. Eklavya complied. The thumb was his greatest skill — the sacrifice matched the debt.
- **Ravan** — Offered his head to his guru, Lord Shiva, as guru dakshina (whether literally or symbolically is left open).

> These are not recommendations but only bizarre and unique examples to stress a point.
> — *Karma Sutra*, Ch.2, p.39

Conventional forms of dakshina include: coconut offerings; 11 sets of clothes; utilities like bed and umbrella.

## The Onward Transmission

In the Hingori Sutras framework, the most complete form of dakshina is not a material offering but **passing the teaching forward**:

> The only way to neutralise that debt is to work hard towards being a good student to the satisfaction of your guru. Thereafter, spreading wisdom down the line and crediting that karma to your guru is an act of repayment.
> — *Karma Sutra*, Ch.2, p.39

This is precisely the model Hingori himself follows — [[entities/hingori|Hingori]] transmits Gurudev's teachings and abilities to a new generation, and credits the karma to [[entities/mahaguru|Gurudev]].
