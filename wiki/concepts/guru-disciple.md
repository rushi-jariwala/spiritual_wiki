# The Guru-Disciple Relationship

> *"Guru means remover of darkness."*
> — *Karma Sutra*, Ch.1, p.19

## Definition

A **guru** is one who takes upon themselves the task of reducing the *ignorance* (avidya) of their disciples. "Ignorance" here is not a lack of information but a specific kind of spiritual occlusion — one of the five [[concepts/kleshas|kleshas]] that distort human consciousness.

*Guru Sutra* gives the sharpest definition: a guru is the **dispeller of avidya (ignorance) and the unveiler of maya (perceived reality)**. Avidya is not merely a knowledge gap — it is the condition of receiving reality through biological senses that cap perception at roughly 5% of what actually is. Maya is not hallucination but a perceptual frame: our senses deliver a narrow, filtered slice and we mistake it for the whole.

> "In a spiritual context, a guru is the dispeller of avidya (ignorance) and unveiler of maya (perceived reality)."
> — Hingori, *Guru Sutra*, Ch.1, p.5

> "Our biological senses limit our assessment of the absolute reality to about 5%!"
> — Hingori, *Guru Sutra*, Ch.1, p.5

Whether the guru appears as healer, sage, prophet, or guide, the function is the same: to catalyse the unmasking of the divinity within the disciple by displacing ignorance with light. A **siddh guru** is the qualified form — one who has personally attained and can therefore transmit.

A **sadguru** ("right teacher") goes further: they take responsibility for the *enlightenment* of the disciple, passing on all their knowledge and abilities.

> Getting a sadguru is like winning a lottery.
> — *Karma Sutra*, Ch.1, p.19

## Core Teaching

The guru-disciple relationship, in the Hingori framing, is not primarily instructional. It is **transactional at the level of karma**. A sadguru can:

- **Activate positive prarabdh early**, granting relief the disciple could not otherwise access
- **Re-balance the equation** by helping the disciple accumulate positive karma through seva and tapasya
- **Release sanchit benefits into the current life** — tapping the warehouse ahead of schedule
- **Provide an "umbrella"** of energy that neutralises negative planetary effects

> By adding a substantive number of positive karma, strictly avoiding the negative, and by committing to a lifestyle that will keep the karmic assets increasing and liabilities reducing, the guru can even give the disciple benefit of his sanchit karmas in this life, thus re-balancing the equation in his/her favour.
> — *Karma Sutra*, Ch.1, p.21

## Nuances

### The Guru's Umbrella

The umbrella metaphor is central and deserves its own treatment:

> The blessings of the guru may not avert the rain of bad luck, but it could provide an umbrella, by neutralising the negativity of the planetary effects. By getting attached to the aura of the disciple, his ability to withstand the negative rays gets enhanced.
> — *Karma Sutra*, Ch.1, p.21

The guru does not usually *cancel* the disciple's destiny. He **changes how the disciple experiences it**. The difficulty still arrives, but the will-power, perception, and reaction of the disciple are altered such that the negativity rounds off. Two vivid examples are filed as [[stories/gurus-umbrella-wife-delivery]] and [[stories/scooter-accident-vision]].

### Changing the Experience, Not the Script

> A guru can make his disciple die in his/her dream-state. He can give his disciple a new lease of life and extend his/her present life span by several years wherein the situation may remain the same, but the experience may be different.
> — *Karma Sutra*, Ch.1, p.22

The critical distinction: the outer events may still occur, but inner response, consciousness, and capacity are transformed. A financial squeeze is still endured, but with enhanced will-power and higher consciousness.

### Evolution Without a Guru

Not all seekers have a human guru. Hingori acknowledges:

> Many people also evolve without the help of a guru or spiritual guide. They read religious or spiritual books, practice philanthropy, chant mantras or prayers, and do seva by working with NGOs... Such people are well informed about the spiritual world and its dynamics. Their intellect helps them understand the laws of karma... It is a do-it-yourself process, but sometimes it works.
> — *Karma Sutra*, Ch.1, p.20

The do-it-yourself route is real but implicitly harder, with fewer guarantees. **Guru Nanak himself**, Hingori notes, did not have a guru in human form — he worshipped a devi, and through her received the attainment of OM on his palm and his spiritual powers. See [[stories/guru-nanak-river-bain]].

### Posthumous Guidance

A guru's relationship with disciples does not end at death. Hingori says his own guru continues to meet disciples in the dream state. Sai Baba has appeared in dreams and granted darshan to followers. Similar accounts exist for Jesus, Moses, and countless saints. See [[concepts/jivaatma]] on the persistence of beings after bodily death.

### The Guru Across Traditions

Hingori names renowned gurus across cultures and eras — **Guru Vashisht** (to Lord Ram), **Jesus Christ** (with his eleven disciples), **Guru Nanak**, **Gautam Buddha**, **Ramakrishna Paramhansa**, and beyond India: **Aristotle**, **Plato**, **Lao Tzu**, **Prophet Mohammed**, **Khalil Gibran**. His view:

> Every religious teacher has said almost the same thing in different languages — all have advocated performing good deeds... The truth is one and presentations many.
> — *Karma Sutra*, Ch.1, p.19

## In Practice

For the disciple:
- **Recognise the lottery.** A sadguru is not a given; receive it as the fortune it is.
- **Seva and tapasya are the currencies of the relationship.** The guru's ability to manipulate karma on your behalf is activated through your own effort in service and practice.
- **Expect changed experience, not changed events.** Don't pray for the difficulty to disappear; pray for the capacity to pass through it transformed.
- **Wait-as-tapasya.** Waiting in queue for the guru, or enduring delay before relief, is itself part of the currency. See [[stories/bada-guruvar-queue]].

## *The Guru of Gurus* — Precision of the Guru's Watch

The Entrepreneur Extraordinaire chapter adds a dimension of **disciplinary accountability** to the relationship. Gurudev maintained a constant vigilance over disciples' conduct, and correction was swift:

> When his disciple, Malhotra ji, delayed a train to suit his convenience, the mahaguru threatened to withdraw his powers. Malhotra ji later rose to become one of the mahaguru's most accomplished disciples. The mentor kept a tight vigil and a lofty attitude.
> — Hingori, *The Guru of Gurus*, p.128

Powers are granted conditionally; they can be withdrawn. The same Malhotra ji who could delay a train was threatened with losing the ability to do so — not as cruelty, but because the powers belong to the guru's trust, not the disciple's ego. The severity of the consequence matched the gravity of misuse: Malhotra ji's power was not a personal possession but a tool placed in his keeping for seva.

This also illustrates why the relationship is not sentimentally indulgent despite its warmth. The "lofty attitude" Hingori names is the guru's refusal to normalise misuse of spiritual gifts.

## *Guru Sutra* Ch.2 — The Guru Decoded

### Who Is (and Is Not) a Guru

Hingori begins by dividing life into three segments — physical/social, mental/intellectual, and spiritual — and locating the guru squarely in the third. A teacher of the first two is a teacher; a guru works at the level of the spirit, transforming the conditioning of multiple lifetimes.

Who is **not** necessarily a guru:
- One versed in scripture (Bible, Koran, Gita, etc.)
- One who "has long grey hair, sports a glorious beard, has a disarming smile, wears saffron robes and acts the part"
- A demagogue who wins hearts through oratory
- One who performs arduous asanas or sits in prolonged meditation

> "The concept of a guru is far more exalted and profound, because he is a role model in almost every aspect of a disciple's life. He takes on the disciple's karmic debt and leads him out of it to whatever extent possible, not just in one life, but possibly in future lives as well."
> — Hingori, *Guru Sutra*, Ch.2, p.10

The responsibility is stated explicitly: greater than that of playing the role of mother or father. Pretenders to the title who seek the grandeur rather than the responsibility "tarnish this ancient and sacred institution of Guruism."

### Guru as Concept, Not Person

A crucial reframe: in principle, a guru is not a person but **a concept that embodies infinite wisdom and universal knowledge**. The disciple emulates not the person but the *gunas* (qualities). This is evidenced by **Eklavya**, the archer from *The Mahabharat*, who was refused instruction by Dronacharya (reserved for the Kuru princes) but built a clay statue of him and trained before it as his guru — acquiring mastery by worshipping a concept, without ever receiving direct instruction. Hingori's own ongoing inspiration by Gurudev's memory, and Krishanmohanji of Durgapur who "lives his life as if he is being continuously watched and guided by Gurudev," are contemporary examples of the same principle.

### The Need for a Guru

Hingori documents the spiritual lineage of major figures to show that virtually no spiritual evolution has occurred without a guru:

- **Adi Shankaracharya** — born enlightened, yet sought a guru and wooed him for diksha (see [[entities/adi-shankaracharya]])
- **Ramakrishna Paramahansa** — would have been "nothing more than a sadhu in a trance" without his guru Totapuri
- **Vivekananda** — the spiritual creation of Ramakrishna
- **Yogananda Parmahansa** — created by Yukteshwar Giri, who was created by Lahiri Mahasaya, who was created by Mahaavatar Babaji
- **Ram** — needed Guru Vashisht to lead a life of non-duality and become an iconic king
- **Krishna** — needed Guru Sandipani Muni for "fine-tuning"
- **The Buddha** — had many gurus contributing to the infrastructure of his final emancipation
- **Gurudev (the Mahaguru)** — even a destined spiritual giant had teachers before his power awakened: **Sitaramji of Dasua** was his early spiritual advisor (Sitaramji himself had a guru, Sitaramji of Benaras); Gurudev consulted him frequently until the mahaguru's own power emerged. See [[entities/mahaguru]].

> "In his early years, before the power of the Mahaguru awakened in him, Gurudev had a spiritual advisor by the name of Sitaramji of Dasua, whom he often consulted."
> — Hingori, *Guru Sutra*, Ch.2, p.12

Hingori identifies a "spiritually volcanic period" between the 8th and 3rd century BC — the **Axial Age** — when Confucius, Lao-Tse, Buddha, Zarathustra, Mahavir, and others profoundly shaped future philosophies.

> "History clearly reveals that almost no spiritual evolution has ever been possible without the teachings of a guru."
> — Hingori, *Guru Sutra*, Ch.2, p.14

The Thursday fast on Jupiter (the planet of the Guru) is the traditional method for those seeking a guru but not yet finding one: a channelising of intent rather than a search through media reports.

> "It is said that the guru finds his disciple and not the other way around. However, this may not apply to devotees and admirers. Furthermore, destiny is another key factor at play. Unless destined, neither a guru would be a guru nor a disciple, a disciple!"
> — Hingori, *Guru Sutra*, Ch.2, p.14

### Guna-Based Taxonomy of Gurus

Ch.2 introduces a taxonomy of gurus by guna level — directly extending the three-guna framework (see [[concepts/gunas]]):

**Tamasic gurus** offer bali (sacrifices), teach negative tantra vidya (black magic), casting spells, blocking fortune. Their services can be bought; they teach disciples to do the same.

**Rajasic gurus** teach: art of warfare and self-protection, scriptures and epics, politics and kingship, philanthropy, mantras of rajasic deities (Laxmi), yoga, rituals, havans, animal sacrifices. They are demonstrative and prone to "pomp and show," comfortable with donations, palatial ashrams, and a lavish life. When their positive karmic balance runs out: bad luck in this life, or karmic debt to be paid later.

**Sattvic gurus** teach disciples to *unlearn*, to practise detachment (vairagya), to understand maya, to control the senses and go beyond mind and intellect. The objective is mukti or moksh.

> Golden Principle: "It is not 'getting what you want' but 'wanting what you get'."
> — Hingori, *Guru Sutra*, Ch.2, p.16

**The Siddh Guru and the Guna Mix:** A siddh guru utilises all three gunas — tamas, rajas, and sattva — balancing them for the disciple's benefit. As evolution advances, the sattva percentage increases; "very little and only that which is useful is left of rajas and tamas." Even figures like Krishna (Mahabharat), Guru Gobind Singh, and Prophet Muhammad called on rajasic qualities to achieve sattvic goals.

See also [[concepts/tantra]] — clarified here as a spiritual science, inherently neither positive nor negative; only its application determines the polarity.

### The Guru Introduces You to Yourself

The ultimate function of the guru is not instruction but introduction:

> "Nobody can introduce you to yourself better than your guru. A siddh guru who has the intuitive knowledge to recognise you beyond your physical self knows you at the 3 distinct levels of the body, mind, and spirit, whereas you only know what you perceive of yourself."
> — Hingori, *Guru Sutra*, Ch.2, p.19

The guru introduces the disciple to their destiny and gunas, enabling genuine self-acceptance on an "as-is-where-is basis." He sees the disciple's future potential where the disciple sees only limitations. When Hingori told Gurudev he felt like a fool knowing nothing about spiritualism, Gurudev "scoffed and said that he knew how much gyan I really had."

> "Only when a disciple understands the totality of who and what he is, can he play music to the words 'Tat Twam Asi' or 'That you are' — which means, at the core, you are a part of the consciousness supreme."
> — Hingori, *Guru Sutra*, Ch.2, p.20

The chapter closes with the second Golden Principle:

> Golden Principle: "In the final analysis, the guru introduces the disciple to the disciple's inner being, and within that being lies the ultimate guru."
> — Hingori, *Guru Sutra*, Ch.2, p.20

And Aristotle's closing quote: *"Knowing yourself is the beginning of all wisdom."*

### A Distinct Viewpoint: Swami Rama's Boat Analogy

**Swami Rama**, a spiritual teacher quoted in *Guru Sutra* Ch.2, represents a counterpoint to deep guru-veneration:

> "Guru is not the goal. Anyone who establishes himself as a guru to be worshipped is not a guru. Guru is like a boat for crossing the river. It is important to have a good boat and it is very dangerous to have a boat that is leaking. The boat brings you across the river. When the river is crossed, the boat is no longer necessary. You don't hang onto the boat after completing the journey, and you certainly don't worship the boat."
> — Swami Rama, quoted in Hingori, *Guru Sutra*, Ch.2, p.11

Hingori includes this as "a distinct viewpoint" but flags it as "stretched to an extreme view, harsher than warranted." In the Hingori tradition the guru is venerated deeply — the boat analogy, while pointing to a real truth (the guru is a means, not the end), overstates the case by implying that post-liberation the guru should be discarded. See also [[wiki/analogies]] for the boat analogy entry.

> [!warning] Swami Rama's boat analogy creates a mild tension with the tradition of deep, ongoing devotion to the guru. Hingori acknowledges this directly. See [[sources/guru-sutra]] Contradictions section.

This connects directly to [[concepts/self-acceptance]] and ultimately to [[concepts/self-worship]].

## *Guru Sutra* — The Spectrum from Admirer to Disciple

*Guru Sutra* Ch.1 lays out a four-stage progression. The stages are not merely descriptive — each has a distinct mechanism of connection and a distinct quality of surrender.

### Admirer
An admirer appreciates certain qualities in another. They need not be a guru to be admired. As long as admiration remains inspirational it leads to self-improvement. Crucially, admiration is not passive:

> "Since energy flows to and from where attention goes, what you consistently admire, you attract towards yourself. This is how the human magnet attracts attributes and qualities from other sources."
> — Hingori, *Guru Sutra*, Ch.1, p.6

Not every admirer becomes a follower, devotee, or disciple.

### Follower
When admiration becomes sustained and the person begins to emulate — observing and analysing the guru's words, deeds, and actions — they are a follower. The distinction from admiration: the follower *acts on* what they observe rather than merely appreciating it.

### Devotee
A follower evolves into a devotee once a faith-based connection develops. Worship begins; the philosophical system within which the guru operates becomes unshakeable. At this point the devotee is a few steps from complete surrender to the guru's will.

### Disciple
The critical teaching: **a devotee becomes a disciple not when they consider themselves one, but only when the guru accepts them as such.** Until then, however advanced the devotee's practice, the rank is not conferred.

> "While for some this wait can extend across lifetimes, for some others, destiny allows a fast-track provided the being is preconditioned and has the requisite gunas (qualities)."
> — Hingori, *Guru Sutra*, Ch.1, p.6–7

Complete surrender means surrendering ego, body, mind, and spirit. Once the guru accepts and a homogeneous link is created, the disciple is elevated. What follows:

- The disciple realises the guru and disciple are the same — part of the same consciousness supreme, at distinct stages of spiritual journey. They accept both on an **"as-is-where-is basis"**.
- Desirelessness arrives. Tasks are performed as duty, not desire.
- The mind becomes quiet and more attuned to the spirit body than the physical.
- The guru's grace and prescribed practices hasten the empowerment of the disciple's *suksham sharir* (spirit body).

> "The Indian scriptures suggest, 'Guru bina gati nahin', implying there can be no spiritual evolution without a guru."
> — Hingori, *Guru Sutra*, Ch.1, p.7

### Hingori's Personal Testimony (Introduction)

Hingori's own arc illustrates the spectrum:

> Not knowing how to even spell the word 'spiritualism', mine was an experience of sailing from semi-debauchery to a divinity I had not aspired for. I laughed at and ridiculed the 'spiritually inclined'. But the tables turned, as they had to, and the joke was on me. Astrologically speaking, I bought a ticket to Venus but inexplicably, crash-landed on Jupiter (the ruling planet of gurus). Most of my co-passengers thought this a temporary halt. The joke was on them too. We all became Jovians, though not by choice or chance. Jupiter was our home intended. We just did not know it then.
> — Hingori, *Guru Sutra*, Introduction

> Being misfits worked to our advantage. We learned to play Shakespeare, not in robes but in jeans and shirts. We neither looked spiritual, nor walked or talked spiritual... Some of my guru bhais (spiritual associates) were bankers, government servants, and salaried employees, while others were traders and businessmen. Some even bus drivers!
> — Hingori, *Guru Sutra*, Introduction

The group's training had no textbooks, no scriptures, no theory — only seva (service). Every aspect of seva was an opportunity to serve with humility: arranging shoes at the shoe-stand, offering tea and khichdi to thousands on a *bada guruvar* (the Thursday following a new moon). The operative word was always seva.

## *Guru Sutra* Ch.3 — The Gurukul System

A person's destiny may be programmed to make them a spiritual guru, yet there is a protocol to be followed for their evolution. The gurukul system is that protocol — a structured path with three main routes.

### 1. Scriptures

The ancient gurukul (school for spiritualism) divided education into theory, practice, and mentorship. Scriptures were memorised so they could rest within the subconscious and be recollected during **tark-vitark** (spiritual debates), and applied in the student's life journey.

### 2. Mantra Vidya

Students were taught higher-order mantras specifically created by sages of ancient India. Alongside mantra recitation, the curriculum included meditation techniques, yogic postures, and **mind control** — without which pupils could not focus on spiritual practices.

> People in ancient times used mind control for various purposes. They used their energies to propel weapons, lift and move objects and so forth. I have witnessed this in a vision, in the early hours of the morning, in a separate reality. The Lakshman Rekha from the Ramayan is a famous example of the use of energy directed by the power of the mind. Even today, some of us have learnt how to create a keel (a circle of spiritual protection), using energy that cannot be penetrated easily by spirits or negative forces.
> — Hingori, *Guru Sutra*, Ch.3, p.23

A keel is a circle of spiritual protection created through directed mental energy — a surviving remnant of the more extensive mind-over-matter capacities that existed in ancient times. As Hingori notes, that broader capacity has since waned: "in our day and age, even very powerful mantras work only at the level of the aura and do not have the same effect as they once did." See [[concepts/tantra]] for the full treatment of mantra vidya within the gurukul curriculum.

### 3. Mentorship

After years of scripture and mantra training, students were sent to a senior spiritual mentor who examined their grasp of the subject and their gunas. The mentor:

- Filled knowledge gaps and tested attitudes
- Taught higher levels of tantra, **yantra** (occult mystical diagrams), and advanced mantras
- Imparted several types of meditation including techniques for **samadhi** (highest level of consciousness)
- Fine-tuned understanding of philosophy, duality, and maya
- Taught the elements of **brahmavidya** (incomprehensible knowledge of the universe) — higher spiritual concepts that led to overcoming delusion
- Taught students to earn, harness, and use spiritual energy

Students who excelled were promoted and given special attention; some were chosen as successors of the mentor at the institution.

The curriculum also required waking at specific times, fasting, and performing designated duties. Service to others — humans, animals, plants — was central because it shed karmic baggage:

> "Service to others, be they humans, animals or plants helped the students shed some karmic baggage."
> — Hingori, *Guru Sutra*, Ch.3, p.24

This universal principle of seva runs across Hinduism, Sikhism, Islam, and Zoroastrianism alike. See [[practices/seva]].

### Gurudev's Modern Reinvention

The gurukul system is followed by many cults and religions. In Gurudev's world, the system had to be reinvented due to limitations of funds:

> Our homes became our ashrams and a cabinet on the wall became our temple. Being householders we had no formal attires or uniforms and could blend in.
> Our methods were casual but our results were not.
> — Hingori, *Guru Sutra*, Ch.3, p.25

The closing verse of Ch.3 frames this entire journey — from the first stirring of devotion through the full gurukul arc — as a single movement toward self-realisation. See [[stories/guru-sutra-ch3-verse]].

## *Guru Sutra* Ch.4 — Admirer to Disciple (The Lived Mechanics of Each Stage)

Ch.4 expands the four-stage spectrum into lived experience, tracing each stage's internal mechanics, emotional quality, and pivot point.

### The Arc: Devotion → Acceptance

The chapter opens with a governing distinction:

> "Even though the journey may start with devotion, it ends with acceptance."
> — Hingori, *Guru Sutra*, Ch.4, p.27

**Devotion** (shraddha → bhakti) draws its power from heightened emotion. **Acceptance** is a centred approach that does not need emotion as its fuel. The final stage is not worshipping the guru but worshipping the guru *within* — which requires acceptance of the self.

### Stage 1 — Admirer (Detailed)

The admirer examines the guru by analysing demeanour and actions, is susceptible to others' opinions, and discusses the guru with others. If a celebrity guru is chosen (known only through media), the relationship is one-way: the guru is oblivious to the admirer's existence. Hingori's own experience confirms that while there are historical cases of benefit from such "hypothetical relationships," personal interaction far outweighs remote connection.

The admirer enjoys reflected glory: "the reflected glory of the guru acquires a higher market cap." A guru motivates admirers toward his philosophy; he selects those ready to advance. Many admirers are content to remain admirers.

### Stage 2 — Follower (Detailed)

The follower is emotionally bonded. Every interaction is stored in a "designated mental folder." The follower tries to emulate the guru; emotions oscillate with the guru's approval or reprimand — like a pendulum, like a child's relationship with their teacher. Spiritual maturity has not yet surfaced.

An anecdote from Hingori: a successful architect flies from Delhi to Mumbai to seek clarity on the Hingori Sutras. Hingori finds her spiritually potent but observes she is "at the threshold of faith" — mystified, but not yet willing to take the plunge from follower to devotee.

### Stage 3 — Devotee (Detailed)

Sustained admiration becomes devotion; the throat gets heavy, eyes wet. The devotee worships the guru's image, recites mantras and prayers before it, requests favours, and receives answers — "which may come from his own inner mind."

Hingori's personal devotee phase: a home temple with Gurudev's photograph, incense, diyas, flowers. He talked to the temple, received responses he could not explain, and believed his devotion was of a high order.

Short anecdotes from the sthan: Pooja saw Gurudev's head move in his photograph during meditation; she and Vipan spotted an Om in the flame of a diya on several occasions. See full story: [[stories/guru-sutra-ch4-devraj-jal]] — Devraj receives a reply from Gurudev through his home temple photograph on Mahashivratri; the jal bottle disappears; Gurudev's concluding teaching: "our ultimate goal should be to evolve to a level where every drop of water we consume becomes jal in our body."

### Stage 4 — Disciple: As-Is-Where-Is Acceptance

Stage 4 arrives not through effort but through a shift of ground. One night, Hingori heard a voice from within his own ear asking whether he would still accept his guru if he were not as great as believed — or even dishonest. The eventual answer was unconditional:

> "I would accept him as my guru irrespective of how great he was. Even if he were not completely honest with me, I would surrender to him and accept him on an as-is-where-is basis."
> — Hingori, *Guru Sutra*, Ch.4, p.32

This is identified as a **Golden Principle**: fate assigns a disciple to a guru; the disciple does nothing to change it even if they could. The voice was recognised as Hingori's own spirit — not an external source. With that recognition, his relationship with Gurudev shifted from physical to metaphysical. The emotion in his home temple practice receded; worship became matter-of-fact. He began relating to Gurudev as a being who had existed since eternity and would persist beyond his physical frame.

The practical marker of Stage 4: Hingori was no longer desperate to be in Gurudev's physical presence. He could offer salutations at the door, even without Gurudev knowing he was there. He had "acknowledged the omnipresent status of his being."

When informed of Gurudev's death, Hingori laughed — recognising it as another in a long series of astral departures, this one perhaps longer or permanent.

> "Unemotional reverence for your guru is sattvic and one step prior to the ultimate relationship, which is, finding no difference between oneself and the guru."
> — Hingori, *Guru Sutra*, Ch.4, p.33

Full testimony: [[stories/guru-sutra-ch4-inner-voice]].

> [!tip] The "as-is-where-is" principle appears earlier in the *Guru Sutra* (Ch.1, describing what happens when a disciple is finally accepted). Ch.4 gives the origin story: it was not a concept Hingori received from outside — it arose from within, from the voice of his own spirit. The outer teaching and the inner realisation are the same event at different points in the timeline.

### Stage 4 is not comparable to any other

> "At all stages, continue to assess your relationship with your guru. And then, let it evolve, as it does, when it does."
> — Hingori, *Guru Sutra*, Ch.4, p.33

Ch.4 closes with Rumi:

> "One moment spent / In the company of saints / Is far better / Than praying for a hundred years."
> — Rumi, epigraph closing *Guru Sutra*, Ch.4, p.34

## *Guru Sutra* Ch.5 — Disciple: Lesser or Greater (pp.35–42)

### The Novice Entry Point

Most disciples arrive not as seekers of liberation but as seekers of relief — cures for physical illness, financial trouble, mental ailment. Hingori is frank about this in his own case:

> When I first visited Gurudev in 1977, it was only because I had rheumatoid arthritis and was told he could cure me.
> — Hingori, *Guru Sutra*, Ch.5, p.35

Waiting in line, Hingori witnessed the man ahead of him — who had been unable to keep down solid food for six months — eat aloo parathas served by Gurudev, and be cured on the spot. When Gurudev's turn came with Hingori, he deliberately cured only 95% of the pain. His reaction: "I felt this was a one-time transaction." He had no intention of returning. Destiny disagreed.

### Seeing the Line, Not the Cross-Section

Five years later, arthritis returned and Hingori had to go back. From that second meeting grew the discipleship. Reflecting on the gap between who he was in 1977 and what he became, Hingori offers the chapter's central philosophical image:

> The distance I believe I have travelled over the last few decades; from dummkopf to divine, is not a real distance. It is merely the fulfilment of a process that was to happen. I made a judgment about myself at a different cross section of time and observed myself as a point on the line rather than the line itself.
> — Hingori, *Guru Sutra*, Ch.5, p.37

The instruction: see yourself not as a point on the line but as the line — extending across past, present, and future. The disciple who judges themselves by today's cross-section will invariably evaluate too lowly. Hingori's recommendation: "have more faith in the rest of the line."

> [!tip] This "point on the line vs. the line itself" framing mirrors *Aatma Sutra* Ch.XII's teaching on not judging yourself by the present cross-section (see [[stories/rajeev-alcoholic-to-saint]] and [[concepts/self-acceptance]]). Both books arrive at the same principle from different angles: destiny/evolution is a trajectory, not a position.

Gurudev — who could see Hingori's future — told Mrs. Jolly, one of his devotees: *"This boy will be able to attract many people towards our subject."* Hingori notes: "Thankfully, the boy did not hear that until 30 years later, for he may have never believed it then."

He also told Hingori directly: "I can only see from the level of my eyes but I will carry you on my shoulders so you can see from much higher." And when Hingori confessed his ignorance of spiritualism: "You are a guru. You are my son. Gurus create gurus."

### The Siddh Guru Sees the Seed

A siddh guru can read potential where no evidence of it yet exists:

> A siddh guru can judge a seed and foresee the potential tree.
> — Hingori, *Guru Sutra*, Ch.5, p.38

Examples from Hingori's own practice: when Rajeev was brought to him as an alcoholic, he saw the saint Rajeev would become (see [[stories/rajeev-alcoholic-to-saint]]). When Vipan's father complained that his son was not commercially inclined, Hingori told him the boy would serve many people and earn his father's goodwill. Both came to pass.

The *Gospel of John* (18:13–27) is cited: Jesus told Peter, "Before the rooster crows today, you will disown me three times" — knowing both the immediate failure and the eventual foundation-stone. Seeing the future whole, not just the present cross-section.

### Disciples Can Evolve Beyond Their Guru

Ch.5 introduces a teaching absent from earlier chapters: the disciple evolving beyond the teacher is not only possible but is a mark of the teacher's greatness. Hingori's examples:

- **Alara Kalama** was the Buddha's teacher from his father's kingdom. Much later, after the Buddha attained enlightenment, Alara Kalama became his student.
- **Guru Gorakshanath**, **Adi Shankaracharya** (see [[entities/adi-shankaracharya]]), and **Ramakrishna Paramahansa** each evolved to perceivably higher levels than their teachers.

> The evolution of a disciple to a higher status is definitely a compliment to the guru who went beyond himself. It shows the generosity of the teacher and the fact that he did not think competitively, only gracefully!
> — Hingori, *Guru Sutra*, Ch.5, p.38

### Spiritual Hierarchy vs. Chronological Hierarchy

A disciple's standing within a spiritual order is often determined by when they arrived — who met the guru first. Hingori contests this:

> Generally, the hierarchy that exists in many spiritual orders is based purely on chronological grounds and not on spiritual ones. However, just because a person met his guru on a particular date does not mean he is spiritually more advanced than someone who might meet his guru later.
> — Hingori, *Guru Sutra*, Ch.5, p.40

A younger person with the right spiritual antecedent — one who may have already reached a particular stage in a previous life, or who was born at the right mahurat — could easily exceed someone with more years on the path in this life.

> [!warning] The hierarchy malady: many religious orders suffer from defaulting to chronological seniority. "The right man does not always get the right job and often, the most spiritual person of that order does not get the lead because he came later or is comparatively younger. Thus, the spiritual order is forced to suffer the leadership of a less worthy person." — *Guru Sutra*, Ch.5, p.41. See [[sources/guru-sutra]] Contradictions section.

**The Thuksey Rinpoche case** is the sharpest illustration: Thuksey Rinpoche founded a school in Leh in his previous life. His disciple Gyalwang Dhukpa later mentored him — the reincarnated Rinpoche — in this life. The former master is now a student of his own former student. Courts have even legally transferred the school's land to Thuksey Rinpoche in this life, acknowledging the reincarnation. See [[stories/thuksey-rinpoche-reincarnation]].

> Golden Principle: "In spiritualism, one should compete only with oneself and not with others."
> — Dr. Shankar Narayan to Hingori, *Guru Sutra*, Ch.5, p.40

This principle was given to Hingori so often by Dr. Shankarnarayan that it became part of his psyche. Shankarnarayan ji — who was Gurudev's senior at work — would bow at Gurudev's feet regardless of colleagues' reactions. When Gurudev asked his first disciple Malhotraji to give Shankarnarayan the first mantra, Shankarnarayan bowed to and touched the feet of a man who was younger and less senior than him in every conventional domain. See [[entities/dr-shankarnarayan]].

> A disciple's success is measured not by his seniority but by the amalgam of his qualities.
> — Hingori, *Guru Sutra*, Ch.5, p.41

Jesus in Matthew 20:16: "So the last will be first, and the first will be last."

The chapter closes with Guru Nanak Dev's verse: see [[stories/guru-sutra-ch5-verse]].

## *Guru Sutra* Ch.6 — Journey Towards Disciplehood (pp.43–62)

### Choosing Those Who Do Not Want to Be Chosen

The chapter opens with Hingori's own origin story — atheist, non-vegetarian, drinker, mocker of spiritual teachers — and immediately flags a central paradox:

> Gurudev taught me how to choose disciples and the first rule is to choose those who do not want to be chosen. I find religious, or for that matter even spiritually pre-conditioned people have a lot already embossed on their minds. It takes a long time to erase their preconceived notions of spiritualism.
> — Hingori, *Guru Sutra*, Ch.6, p.44

The logic: a religiously pre-conditioned person brings a full hard drive of dogmas, whereas the uninterested person — Hingori, [[stories/surenderji-murder-plan|Surenderji]], Darius (rugby and club nights), Vipan (barely looks spiritual) — is a blank cassette. The siddh guru reads the spirit's potential, not the person's resume.

> A siddh guru will always know the strength of a potential disciple's spirit. He will also try to sense the connection between the person's destiny and his spiritual growth.
> — Hingori, *Guru Sutra*, Ch.6, p.45

### Seven Qualities of a Good Disciple

**1. Sense of Awareness**

Awareness is both inward and outward. Inwardly, the disciple learns to observe thoughts, reactions, and feelings — the prerequisite to self-assessment and then self-improvement. Outwardly, a spiritualist must be aware of negative energies and potential attacks:

> Moreover, the magnetic pull of a spiritualist's lower chakras keeps his consciousness hovering at a lower level, leading to the indulgence in baser instincts, thereby being susceptible to temptations of the senses. Awareness of this aspect not only safeguards a spiritualist against such pitfalls but also helps to push him to higher levels.
> — Hingori, *Guru Sutra*, Ch.6, p.45

**2. Flexibility and Non-dogmatism**

The guru may want the disciple to react differently from how they think they should. That requires flexibility. Dogmatism — the product of stringent religious rules about how to pray, what to wear, which direction to face — is another name for a calcified preconception. A siddh guru makes a disciple *unlearn* all of this, taking them "to an understanding beyond duality."

**3. Sense of Sacrifice**

Sacrifice runs through every dimension: comfort for the guru and others, time, money, opportunity, physical effort. Accepting the dislikable is a sacrifice in itself. "A sense of sacrifice is a must for someone who aspires to spiritually evolve."

**4. Sheer Guts**

Safety and spiritualism do not always go together. Spiritual service includes protecting people from spirits, black magic, and energies linked to religious institutions. The healer absorbs negative energy. Hingori was physically pushed backward by an invisible force in a Venice church; a disciple lacking courage may quit the path.

**5. Initiative**

Philosophy is easy; acting on it is not. The example is [[stories/dr-vachhani-persistence|Dr. Vachhani]], who waited outside Hingori's office every day for months until Hingori relented. Thirty years later, Vachhani sends patients from allopathy to the sthan and sometimes wills them to improve:

> Most gurus are not looking for yes-men. They would prefer disciples who have the capacity to grasp, understand, and act on their intuitions rather than being governed by dogmatic rules and regulations.
> — Hingori, *Guru Sutra*, Ch.6, p.48

**6. Obedience**

> Disobedience and obedience are the snakes and ladders in the game of Guruism.
> — Hingori, *Guru Sutra*, Ch.6, p.49

Gurudev's instructions were often asymmetric — different rules for different disciples — which produced confusion, debate, and occasional refusal. Tonyji's refusal to open a Kashmir sthan meant "his sthan is where his boots are" — a missed lifetime opportunity. Hingori's own refusal to start a sthan led to a three-year forced sabbatical (see [[stories/hingori-sthan-sabbatical]]). Obedience is grounded in an epistemic claim:

> You form opinions based on your experience of this physical life and the conditioning you carry with you, whereas your guru bases his opinions on his knowledge of your past, present and future lives. He also knows how to get you through the troughs and crests, not only of your destiny but also your attitudes and beliefs.
> — Hingori, *Guru Sutra*, Ch.6, p.50

**7. Loyalty**

Loyalty to the guru and his mission is the most significant qualification in Hingori's personal experience — but it must be paired with maturity and discretion: "A loyal but foolish disciple has often been a liability to the institution."

### Six Obstacles to Disciplehood

**1. Desire for Self-importance**

Almost everyone wants to be known or considered important. Seeking attention and significance keeps a disciple's consciousness fixed on the social self rather than the aatma. The chapter's poem (see [[stories/guru-sutra-ch6-poem1]]) puts it plainly: befriending the chicanery of self-importance and praise sets the soul up for a mighty fall.

**2. Spiritual Vanity**

Spiritual vanity — impressing others with spiritualism, being dramatic in discourses, wearing the "right garb," pursuing a media-friendly spiritual persona — makes a practitioner egoistic and defeats the purpose of attainment. Hingori connects this directly to Maharishi Patanjali's concept of **asmita** (the klesha of I-ness):

> Spiritual vanity keeps you fixated on your physical self and does not let you identify with your inner being. Your spiritual journey is supposed to reach the destination of 'I am That' (the consciousness supreme). But, the 'I-ness' gets a person stuck at 'I am X socially, or Y commercially, or Z status wise!'
> — Hingori, *Guru Sutra*, Ch.6, pp.46–47

Between *"I am"* and *"I am That"*, the difference is one word in print and a trillion in invisible ink. See also [[concepts/kleshas]] — asmita as the second klesha.

**3. Spiritual Greed**

Material ambition bleeds into spiritual life: competing on meditation hours, mantra volume, number of followers, speed of attainment. This is the same greed, in a different currency:

> Greed has no role to play in the process of spiritual living and evolution.
> — Hingori, *Guru Sutra*, Ch.6, p.53

The distinction drawn: selfishness is greed, sacrifice is service. An old person wanting to accumulate more virtue before death is not greed — it is seva. The car rally analogy applies: the one who arrives later may have had a far richer journey than the one who finishes first (see [[wiki/analogies]]).

**4. Envy**

Competitiveness among disciples — wanting to be praised, noticed, preferred — erodes self-worth and slows the climb to self-realisation:

> Spiritual envy at a conscious level makes the unconscious mind of a disciple look down upon itself.
> — Hingori, *Guru Sutra*, Ch.6, p.54

Kapill of Lonavala (see [[entities/kapill]]) gained Hingori's respect precisely by promoting others before himself — and in doing so unwittingly superseded peers who came before him.

**5. Anger**

Anger burns the aura, depletes spiritual energy, adds negative karma, and creates a sense of failure at an unconscious level. Ancient shraaps (curses) were delivered in moments of anger — harming both recipient and giver. Durvasa, Vishvamitra, and Parshuram are cited as spiritual greats known for anger; Jesus, Guru Vashisht, The Buddha, and Gurudev are held up as the counterpoint.

**6. Emotional Attachments**

Showing emotional attachment is not harmful; *feeling* it is. The power to heal, when used for family and friends out of attachment rather than discretion, becomes something "the power itself would consider unworthy." Disciples who fast-track their relatives' access to the guru, or who sacrifice a patient's seva to fulfil a social obligation, fall in the guru's eyes.

One anecdote: a younger disciple of Gurudev left his unconscious daughter (who had fallen off the stairs) to attend to a cancer patient. Gurudev later cured the daughter instantly. The precedence of service over personal attachment is the teaching.

> Golden Principle: Service to others before self, is the path to spiritual progress.
> — Hingori, *Guru Sutra*, Ch.6, p.55

Hingori quotes Lao Tzu's *Tao Te Ching* in this context:

> "If you realize that all things change, there is nothing you will try to hold on to."
> — Lao Tzu, *Tao Te Ching*, quoted in *Guru Sutra*, Ch.6, p.56

The only way to achieve emotional neutrality is: "convincingly play the role of being emotional without actually being so" — a direct call back to [[concepts/role-play]]. See also the note on Hingori's inability to fully play this role with his own son, whose spirit he recognised before birth (see [[stories/hingori-unborn-son-spirit-vision]]).

### Judging Oneself

Ch.6 closes with a substantial treatment of spiritual self-assessment. The instruction:

> One must not judge oneself on chronology or status. Growth is a process, and therefore, dynamic.
> — Hingori, *Guru Sutra*, Ch.6, p.57

The mango tree analogy: fruit follows flowering, which follows establishment. A disciple who has not had visions, or who has fewer experiences than associates, or who struggles with basic practices despite knowing advanced mantras, is not necessarily behind. Hingori confesses his own inability to fully control his sense of taste despite decades of attainment — "I have learnt to accept my self on an as-is-where-is basis."

No criterion is a perfect measure of advancement:

> - Your experiences?
> - The ability to perform supernatural acts?
> - Knowledge of scriptures?
> - Practice of meditation?
> - Recognition by others?
> - Would spiritual designations be a criterion?
>
> None of the above criteria is a perfect measure of spiritual advancement.
> — Hingori, *Guru Sutra*, Ch.6, p.59–60

The preferred measure is **aatmic equality** — whether one has felt it:

> Unlike Karl Marx, the father of communism, who believed in equality at a physical, economic and social level, I am a spiritual communist who believes in aatmic equality. If you have been able to feel that, then you have gone quite far in my esteem.
> — Hingori, *Guru Sutra*, Ch.6, p.59

And in Gurudev's assessment: having amply served other life forms is the winning hand in the game of life. The instruction to judge oneself not against others, not against past or present self, but *with the self on the eve of one's departure* — because the line is virtually infinite and ends only at moksha. The Buddha's example is given: his evolution was a series of points on a line, from prince to student to emancipated, across lifetimes. His last words to disciples (see [[quotes/guru]]) direct them to assess themselves by adherence to his teachings rather than by any external criterion.

## Open Questions

> [!tip] What qualifies a siddh guru?
> *Guru Sutra* Ch.1 names the **siddh guru** as the qualified type: one who has personally attained and can catalyse the unmasking of divinity in others. *Guru Sutra* Ch.2 onwards develops this further. The distinction sadguru / siddh guru / mahaguru will be clarified on continued ingestion of [[sources/guru-sutra]].

> [!question] Contracts of the relationship
> If the guru can release sanchit benefits into a disciple's current life, does the disciple incur any obligation in return — karmically, energetically, or otherwise? Or is the guru's giving free? Worth tracking through later sources.
