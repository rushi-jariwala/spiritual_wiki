# Hygiene (Social, Commercial, Mental, Spiritual)

> *His methods, like spiritual mutual funds, provided unheard of returns on investment. When we adopted his practices as our habits, they aided our spiritual transcendence.*
> — Hingori, *The Guru of Gurus*, p.197

## Core Teaching

The quality of your existence must supersede your physical life. Karmas — actions and thoughts — influence the quality of existence in this world (Bhu-loka) and qualify you for other lokas. For this reason, aims and aspirations must extend beyond the present incarnation to multiple lifetimes.

Gurudev identified four domains of conduct — social, commercial, mental, and spiritual — each requiring sustained, habit-grade practices. These are the "hygiene" of the spiritually conscious life: maintenance routines that, when internalised, amplify one's spiritual efforts and become the scaffolding of transformation.

The goal: each lifetime must stack up toward higher lokas, extended bliss, mukti, and ultimately reunion with the param-aatma. As a benchmark: the jivaatma evaluates itself objectively at the end of each life term based on *attitudes changed, attributes gained, and altitude attained* — a performance review that determines the quality of the next life. See also [[analogies|Analogies — The Corporate Performance Review]].

## Social Hygiene

### Gratitude and Benevolence

Gurudev's social hygiene was grounded in a specific inversion of the ordinary service relationship: he was the server, not the served. He taught his disciples to be grateful not merely for receiving seva but for *being allowed to give it*.

> The mahaguru always reminded us to be thankful to those who let us serve them.
> — Hingori, *The Guru of Gurus*, p.200

This concept — that the *recipient* of service does the server a favour by accepting — bridges the mental divide between "I" and "You," creating a sense of unity. Seva is no longer charity from the strong to the weak but a mutual act of spiritual exchange.

**Benevolence as a habit:** Gurudev's benevolence extended to the animal world with the same unconditional reach as it did to people. When the monkey who regularly visited his farm lost her infant, he went on paath and granted the dead infant a human birth in his next life. The snake-reborn-as-baby incident (see [[stories/snake-reborn-as-baby]]) was similarly an act of response to a being who sought his help.

**Gratitude in miniature:** His gratitude to the fakir of the dargah from his childhood — where he and Subbhash ji picked jujubes — appeared to have seeded his admiration for the mystical. On significant days like Mahashivratri and Guru Purnima, he refused to wear more garlands than his guru: "I don't want more garlands on my neck than my guru."

**The server's privilege:** Among his disciples, this posture was consistent regardless of station. Bittu ji recalls being woken at 3am in the wintry mornings of Renuka with a freshly brewed cup of chai. Raji ji remembers Gurudev helping him unload gunny sacks of fabric from his car. Pratap Singh ji's most treasured memory is of Gurudev personally cooking breakfast for him at camps. A visit to Sonipat was used not to spend time with family but to visit his farm caretaker's aged parents — a request made in undertones, honoured in overtones.

**Connection with others:** His connection manifested differently across relationships. With children he was playfully naughty — evening gully cricket in his lungi, cheating at the game but arriving with sweets to forestall complaints. With elders he was unfailingly respectful: Dr. Shankarnarayan recalls accompanying the mahaguru to his in-laws' house in Bilga in 1990, where Gurudev touched his mother-in-law's feet in the Hindu custom. *"The mahaguru, whose feet millions waited to touch, was touching the feet of an ordinary woman!"*

### Socio-centric Beliefs

Respecting the religious and cultural conditioning of others was another facet of social hygiene. Gurudev understood that people perceive the world according to their upbringing, and his conduct shifted to meet them there.

- He stubbed his cigarette when he saw Sikh devotees approaching, knowing their religious aversion to smoking.
- He allowed devotees from West Bengal to eat fish occasionally as it was their staple food.
- He advised abstinence from alcohol but did not abandon devotees like Billu ji who genuinely struggled to give it up. However, inebriated visitors were not permitted inside the sthan.
- He opposed dowry and viewed *kanyadaan* — giving a daughter in marriage — among the highest forms of seva. He regularly contributed to bridal trousseaux to protect a bride's father from financial ruin.
- He saw women as independent equals at a time when Indian women were profiled only as daughters, wives, and mothers; he did not insist on women marrying young.

> Although the mahaguru was flexible in his ways, he was principled in his thinking.
> — Hingori, *The Guru of Gurus*, p.201

## Commercial Hygiene

Gurudev lived debt-free and taught that money is a tool of spirituality, not a measure of materiality. He did not accept anything he could not afford and was wary of taking or consuming salt or cereals paid for by another.

### Intent Over Obligation

The single most important commercial principle was honouring *intent* above obligation. When Kasturi's family woke at 3am to prepare a large breakfast for the group at the gas station en route from Himachal Pradesh, Gurudev enjoyed it graciously. His disciples refused, not wanting to be obligated. He reprimanded them: they had neither honoured devotion nor valued intention over obligation. See [[stories/kasturi-gas-station-breakfast]].

A related principle: repayment for petty theft came in the loss of seva points, not public shaming. When he noticed bills missing from his wallet and quietly named the likely person, he explained: "It wasn't required since that man would lose the benefit of some of his seva."

### Money Earned and Money Spent

Gurudev always told his disciples that money earned in proper ways would find an outlet for good use, while money earned by improper means would be squandered in some form.

- He distributed a significant portion of his meagre salary on payday to those in need.
- When Santlal ji brought lottery tickets hoping for a blessing and entry into the Himgiri Trust, Gurudev tore them: *"I think of my disciples as my lottery. Whatever seva happens via the Trust will be funded by their hard-earned wealth as I have enabled them to earn their seva."*
- He reprimanded Bittu ji for buying an expensive tray: *"It is not in our disposition to be ostentatious and given to luxury. We must value simplicity and not show off."*
- During Diwali, when most people bet on money, he taught respect for it: disrespecting money was akin to disobliging Laxmi Devi.
- He neither accepted gifts at the sthans nor allowed his disciples to accept them.

**The briefcase that came back:** A Sikh gentleman cured of a chronic headache returned with a briefcase containing a lakh of rupees. Gurudev told him: *"As soon as I touch the briefcase, your headache will return. It is my duty to do seva, not my business."* See [[stories/sikh-headache-briefcase]].

**The karmic logic of spending:** In Gurudev's framework, money spent on oneself or accepted from others creates a debt; money spent in service of others credits the giver with the benefit of the act. Money can supplement spiritual intent but cannot substitute it. His recommendation: deploy 10–20% of monthly earnings on seva.

**Correcting greed:** A senior disciple's greed for property was quietly corrected without public humiliation. Gurudev told him: *"Don't crave for money because I will give you so much spiritual wealth that you will never run out."*

## Mental Hygiene

Decluttering the mind is essential because mental health is a crucial determinant of the quality of existence. The primary sources of mental clutter are thoughts and emotions.

Consider the mind a warehouse: getting rid of unwanted inventory is part of reducing clutter. When clutter is regularly decanted, mental energy is preserved, psychosomatic disorders are avoided, and room is made for self-observation and [[concepts/constant-awareness|constant awareness]].

### "Vichaar Vishay Se Aata Hai"

This *ek vakya* was Gurudev's compressed teaching on the origin of thought. Hingori unpacks it across four observations:

1. **Thoughts emerge from sensory interaction.** Control your reactions to sensory inputs and you control the type of thoughts you attract.
2. **Your guna mix determines the type of thoughts you attract.** By voluntarily changing your guna mix, you change the nature of your thoughts.
3. **The mind has a magnetic nature.** Similar discoveries happen in different parts of the world simultaneously because many minds attract the same thought from the atmosphere. Thoughts in the atmosphere are dynamic and continuously change.
4. **Thoughts arise from samskars surfacing.** The mind plays the role of self-reflector, understanding the samskars via thoughts.

Only when Hingori had a direct encounter with a thought — a ray of light travelling from a corner of his room to a point just above the centre of his forehead, dissipating into a crystal-clear observation — did the full import of Gurudev's words sink in. Before that experience, he enjoyed positive thoughts and suffered negative ones. After seeing a thought, he understood: *thoughts were neither his choice nor his creation.* The collateral benefit was freedom from guilt and joy alike. See [[stories/hingori-sees-thought]].

FC Sharma ji, Gurudev's senior disciple, recalls Gurudev's view: *"While you are in physical form, you cannot stop thoughts from coming to you. So you must let them come as they please and not react hastily. Instead, you should evaluate them objectively to select those to be acted upon or dismissed."*

### Objectivity and Emotion

Objectivity is integral to thought management. Bias intensifies when harboured thoughts churn into feelings, which then devolve into emotions. Emotions further bias actions and reactions.

> "Everybody has emotions, and they cannot be avoided. But don't nurture them or make them the basis of your conduct. They do not benefit anyone. Instead, they confuse and often lead to wrong decisions."
> — FC Sharma ji (recalling Gurudev's sentiments), *The Guru of Gurus*, p.210

Through associations and relationships, emotions bind you to objects of physical reality and become deterrents on the spiritual path. Your relationship with yourself, however, is inherently unemotional. Those in the quest for self-discovery must weigh the worth of their emotions.

Gurudev listened to people vent their emotions, letting them feel less burdened. When asked how he managed to hear so many traumatic stories daily without being bogged down:

> "Beyond that moment, I offer everything to Mallik and do not retain even the memory within."
> — Gurudev, *The Guru of Gurus*, p.210

(Mallik was Gurudev's name for the param-aatma.)

Whenever an emotion is associated with an experience, it creates a memory. These memories are stored and carried into future lifetimes as samskars — which will resurface as thoughts and circumstances in this life or the next. The relationship between emotions, samskars, and thoughts will cyclically continue beyond the current lifetime. See [[concepts/samskars]].

Gurudev's own emotional control was necessitated by his constant awareness: he carried the burden of knowing the past, present, and future of everyone he met. Without mastering emotional control, this cosmic intuitiveness could have wreaked havoc in his mind. The one thing that became an emotional irritant for him was *inefficiency in performing seva*. Virender ji echoes this teaching: *"Seva is key to demolishing one's ego."*

**The summary:** *"Clearer the mirror, clearer is your self-realisation and greater your spiritual awareness."*

## Spiritual Hygiene

The spirit is formed of ether and has a subtle vibration. The physical body is far more effective than the spirit at magnetising subtle vibrations — and can help its spiritual counterpart by rapidly multiplying and magnifying its energy. The purpose of human existence is to amplify the energy required by the spirit in its non-physical incarnation following death.

The spirit's energy manifests as an electromagnetic field, or [[concepts/aura|aura]], surrounding the physical body. The larger the aura, the more powerful the spirit. Spiritual hygiene concerns specific practices that support the maintenance and magnification of the aura.

Gurudev did not provide reasoning when he advised on these practices. They were transmitted as guidelines for enriching spiritual energy, not as doctrine.

### Copper Bracelet (Kada)

Almost all of Gurudev's devotees wear a copper *kada* blessed by a guru. In blessing the kada, the guru willingly infuses some of his energy into it. Copper is a good conductor of electricity and minimises energy leaks from the body, protecting the aura. When worn, the guru's aura circulates within the wearer's aura, connecting the wearer to a higher source of consciousness.

When a kada is available and the situation calls for it, offering one's kada can be a matter of life and death. See [[stories/srinagar-dehradun-kada-mission]].

### Food, Liquids, and Clothes

- **Food:** Food prepared by others can be affected by the cook's thoughts, mental disposition, and the aura transmitted through the eyes. A negative aura infects food and disturbs the consumer. White-coloured foods are most susceptible: milk can be tinted with turmeric, yoghurt flavoured with cinnamon, to change the colour before consuming.
- **Prasad:** At sthans, kitchen management is assigned to those with higher levels of thinking, precisely to keep the food's aura clean.
- **Sipped water and used plates:** When Gurudev wanted to share his energy with others, he gave them his sipped water to drink or asked them to eat from his used plate. Saliva is a carrier of aura; this liberty transfers some of the saintly person's aura to the recipient. Conversely, eating from the used plate of someone under the influence of negative energies can infect you with that negativity.
- **Used clothes:** Gurudev transferred some of his aura by giving disciples his used clothes to wear, specifying the number of days to wear them.

### Planetary Colours — Days of the Week

Gurudev wore specific colours on certain days of the week to maximise the effect of planetary rays on his aura:

| Day | Colour |
|---|---|
| Monday | White |
| Tuesday | Browns |
| Wednesday | Blues |
| Thursday | Yellow |
| Friday | Greens |
| Saturday | Black and white |
| Sunday | Red |

He typically wore half-sleeved shirts and open sandals — keeping arms and feet bare to sense ethereal energies, including spirits.

### Thursday Rules

Every Thursday (*Guruvar* — Jupiter's day), a set of restrictions are observed by devotees and disciples. Since Jupiter's planetary rays are most dominant on this weekday, practices that disrupt or contaminate the aura are avoided:

- No head massage or shampooing
- No cutting hair or clipping nails
- No washing clothes
- No alcohol
- No non-vegetarian food
- No sexual indulgences

There is also an auric implication to disposing of body parts: *there is some auric loss whenever we cut or discard any part of our body.* Physical hygiene necessitates trimming hair and nails; spiritual hygiene necessitates containing the resulting auric loss. Cut hair and nails should be offered to a running stream of water rather than swept into a trash can — as aura contributed to the energy of water counts as a repayment of one's debt to nature.

### Sexual Connections and Diet

- Be mindful of who you sexually connect with — allowing someone into your space, physically or mentally, opens you to being influenced by their energy imprints.
- Opt for vegetarianism: eliminating meat keeps the aura free from lower-order animal energies, and meat-eating is a tamasic practice that contradicts the sattvic qualities of Jupiter's rays.
- Alcohol is a downer: after an initial rajasic high, tamasic effects (sloppiness, inertia, drowsiness, irritability) take over, and sattva reduces.

### Sthan Energy Storage

Gurudev could channel and root his energy wherever he desired. When he opened sthans, he stored some of his energies there. Over time these energies multiplied due to the collective energy deposits of the sevadaars and the sthan's visitors. He also carried his own photograph on critical assignments — a gesture Hingori understood as taking the nirman kaya along. See [[concepts/astral-travel]].

### Stay Low-Key

Gurudev taught his disciples to stay low-key: do what has to be done without seeking recognition. He avoided media exposure and did not allow his photograph to be taken or published. Those who defied this and took photographs discreetly found the camera's roll blank when developed.

### Final Status

Technically, your aura is your cosmic currency. It is the measure of your final status in this life and the determinant of the quality of your existence in the afterlife.

The spirit needs its energy to exist, travel, and protect itself. It can attain this energy through deeds done in its name — either by itself (in physical form) or by others. This is why we perform seva while alive, or do charity in the name of ancestors. Gurudev suggested observing the period of shraddh to serve ancestral spirits.

> [!warning] Practitioners of black magic can take your belongings — hair, nails, clothes, shoes, handwritten notes — and use these to cast spells on you. So be very mindful of spiritual hygiene and careful in handling and dispensing your aura, knowingly or inadvertently.
> — Hingori, *The Guru of Gurus*, p.217
