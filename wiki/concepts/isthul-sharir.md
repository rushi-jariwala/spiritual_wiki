# Isthul Sharir

## Definition

The **isthul sharir** is the physical, gross, or material body — the body assigned for a single lifetime. It consists of the physical self, partial mind, and intellect.

> The *isthul sharir* may be understood as the physical, gross or material body, which consists of your physical self, partial mind, and intellect.
> — Hingori, *Aatma Sutra*, Ch.II, p.13

It is the outermost and most visible layer of the five-body model: Isthul Sharir → [[concepts/sukshma-sharir|Sukshma Sharir]] → [[concepts/kaarna-sharir|Kaarna Sharir]] → [[concepts/jivaatma|Jivaatma]] → [[concepts/aatma|Aatma]].

## Goal

The conventional goal of the physical body is economic, social, and intellectual accomplishment — limited to the current lifetime. But Hingori identifies a deeper, more important aim:

> It may be the goal of the physical body to work towards economic, social and intellectual accomplishments. However, one of its more important aims is to shed its adulterations of multiple bodies and indulge in self-purification.
> — Hingori, *Aatma Sutra*, Ch.II, p.13

This self-purification is the work of chipping away what is extraneous — an idea Hingori frames through Michelangelo:

> "Every block of stone has a statue inside it and it is the task of the sculptor to discover it." And about the sculpture he said, "I saw the angel in the marble and carved until I set him free."
> — Michelangelo, quoted in *Aatma Sutra*, Ch.II, p.13

The purified self is already inside the physical body; the lifetime's work is to carve it free.

## Purification Through Gunas

The primary instrument of self-purification is the management of one's [[concepts/gunas|guna]] mix. The three gunas — *tamas* (inaction, procrastination, confusion), *rajas* (action, movement, dynamism, desire), *sattva* (contentment, equanimity, duty, detachment) — are present in different proportions in every person.

> To achieve this self-purification, one of the aims of the physical body should be to change its guna mix to more *sattvic*, by reducing the other two gunas (*rajasic* and *tamasic*) substantially.
> — Hingori, *Aatma Sutra*, Ch.II, p.13

For the physical body to be purified, it needs to surmount attachments, fears, passions, likes and dislikes — and work toward contentment and bliss.

## Bliss vs Happiness — "Happylessness"

Hingori draws a sharp distinction between ordinary happiness and bliss, using the see-saw image:

> Usually, happiness is considered synonymous with joy. Unfortunately, it is a state of emotion that is always counter balanced by sorrow. It is like sitting on a see-saw with joy on one side, and sorrow on the other. Bliss on the other hand, is the fulcrum, the spot at the centre of the see-saw, where emotion is almost non-existent.
> — Hingori, *Aatma Sutra*, Ch.II, p.14

He coins the term **"happylessness"** for this state — bliss as the absence of both joy and sorrow, the still centre of the see-saw. The goal of the Isthul Sharir's purification is not more joy; it is the stable, emotion-free fulcrum.

> Maybe in 2070, the Oxford English Dictionary will include it, describing the word as, a word that means "Bliss, which is neither joy nor sorrow"!
> — Hingori, *Aatma Sutra*, Ch.II, p.14
