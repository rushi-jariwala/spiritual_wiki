# Karmic Debt

## Definition

The obligations a jivaatma accumulates simply by inhabiting a body and living a life. Chapter 2 of *Karma Sutra* frames these as the **expenditure side of the karmic profit and loss account** — debts incurred before a single voluntary action is taken.

> [!tip] Core reframe
> "It is a matter of strategy and not the goodness of heart."
> — Hingori, *Karma Sutra*, Ch.2, p.29
>
> Karmic debt is not a moral or sentimental category. It is an accounting category. Whether you love your parents or resent them is beside the point — the debt exists, must be tracked, and must be repaid.

## Core Teaching

Just by being born, acquiring a body, eating, drinking, being sheltered, and receiving teaching, the jivaatma enters into a series of **transactions with six categories of creditors**. These debts accumulate in [[concepts/sanchit-karma|sanchit]] and must be balanced — either through deliberate positive action in this life, or across future lives.

Failure to repay does not erase the debt. It carries forward.

## The Six Creditors

### 1. Parents

The mother provides a womb, nurtures the body for months before birth and years thereafter. The father provides shelter, food, clothing, teaching, and sustenance. This is a **foundational debt** — the spirit could not have acquired a body to work out its karma at all without them.

**Repayment:** Looking after parents in old age; paying for their needs, medical treatment, funeral arrangements; dedicating good deeds to them after death.

**Debt erosion (reverse):** If parents insult, abuse, wrongfully scold, or deny inheritance, their negative acts reduce the child's obligation proportionally. The accounting is automatic — love or hate does not alter the ledger.

### 2. The Five Elements

The body is constituted from *pancha bhuta* — ether, air, fire, water, earth. Humans routinely misuse these: cutting trees, wasting and contaminating water, polluting air, degrading earth. Each misuse is a debit.

The elements are also **mediums between forms of consciousness**: fire carries offerings to enlightened souls; water can be energised for healing; earth is a mother; air is purified through incense. Most Hindu rituals invoke all five as witnesses to spiritual commitments.

**Repayment:** Planting trees; conserving water; lighting diyas; participating in havans; worshipping elemental personifications.

### 3. The Cow

A human being consumes the lifetime production of approximately **12 cows** in milk and milk products. The debt is substantial — hence the Indian tradition of cow worship, which Hingori frames not as sentiment but as **acknowledgment of debt**.

> Cow daan (charity) is one of the greatest charities, especially when given to brahmins or priests, in return for the prayers and religious rituals performed by them.
> — *Karma Sutra*, Ch.2, p.33

**Repayment:** Feeding cows; providing shelter; being a benefactor. The obligation extends to all providers of milk.

**For those outside India (practical alternatives):**
- *Option 1:* Visualise buying fodder for a cow; donate the equivalent money to a place of worship or charity on a regular basis.
- *Option 2:* Keep a sculpture or photograph of a cow; visualise feeding it; donate the equivalent cost.

### 4. The Green Kingdom

A human lifetime consumes approximately **35 truckloads of vegetables**, plus tons of rice, wheat, cereals, herbs, and flowers.

**Repayment:** Greening spaces; cultivating land and donating produce; digging water resources for arid zones; donating trees to schools, colleges, hospitals, or farm houses; engaging NGOs or corporate tree-plantation schemes on one's behalf.

> [!tip]
> Even though plants are the lowest form of life consumed, it is still a debt — though a lesser debt than animal life.

### 5. The Animal Kingdom

Meat-eating generates **graduated karmic debit** based on the evolutionary level of the animal. Higher evolution = greater debt.

See [[concepts/non-vegetarianism]] for the full treatment with quantified costs.

### 6. Teachers and the Guru

Mother is the first guru; father the second; teachers of education the third; and finally the spiritual guru, who helps attain eternal knowledge (*gyan*).

> The spiritual teacher gets the cream of the credit because he/she helps you first unlearn all the delusions and cleanses your slate before he/she can rewrite on it.
> — *Karma Sutra*, Ch.2, p.39

This debt **cannot be measured** — it is the debt for being able to perceive reality at all.

**Repayment:** Being a good student; spreading wisdom down the line and crediting the karma to the guru; offering *tann, mann, dhann* (body, mind, wealth). See [[concepts/guru-dakshina]].

## Additional Debt Categories

### Acts of Kindness

Any favour received — a glass of water when thirsty, a laugh, a helpful gesture — creates obligation. The debt can be small or large depending on the nature of the favour.

**Critical rule:** Repay with a counter-gift, however small. And when *you* do a good deed, **do not accept any counter-gift in return** — accepting it cashes out the positive karma you earned. See [[stories/sadhu-blanket-clove]].

### Accepting Hospitality

Food, shelter, clothing, gifts, business items — all are karmic debts. Salt and cereal carry particular weight; in ancient times people avoided eating others' food for exactly this reason.

**Repayment:** Counter-gifting (a token coin, silver); paying for food; offering equivalent value.

## In Practice

- Track what you consume and receive as a *running ledger*, not a moral catalogue.
- Repayment does not require the exact same form — creative equivalents count (e.g., visualising cow-feeding and donating the equivalent money).
- Corporate and collective karma is valid — contributing to an NGO's tree plantation counts as personal karmic repayment.
- The debt to the guru cannot be "paid off" in one gesture; it requires a lifetime of dedicated studentship and onward transmission.
