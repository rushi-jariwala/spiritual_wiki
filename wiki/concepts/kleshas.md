# Kleshas

> *"They act as speed-breakers in attaining clarity of mind. They cloud our intellect and obstruct the evolution of our minds."*
> â€” Hingori, *Karma Sutra*, Ch.7, p.107

## Definition

The *klesha* does not have a perfect translation in English. Afflictions, hindrances, and obstacles of the mind are some of the words that explain it. We are born with kleshas as part of our conditioning from past lives. Our karmas are often influenced by these kleshas. The Patanjali *Yoga Sutras* lists five such kleshas â€” and Hingori uses this classical set.

## The Five Kleshas

### 1. Avidya (Misperception)

The primary klesha. Avidya is belief in a delusionary reality â€” misconception. It is our individual circle of [[concepts/maya|maya]].

> Crossing the boundary of maya leads to vidya or enlightenment. Avidya is ignorance and delusion. Naturally, this gives us a jaundiced view of reality. It brings the level of our pure consciousness down to a level of body consciousness where the triggers are the senses and the victim our mind.
> â€” *Karma Sutra*, Ch.7, p.107

While identifying with our bodies we fail to identify with our *aatmas* or the real self. Eternal knowledge and real joy can only be found in unison with the *aatma*. Mistaking the non-*aatma* with the *aatma* leads to a chain of misconception.

Even at a mundane level: not knowing which road to take next, not knowing how to use an automatic dispenser, not knowing French in Paris â€” these become a klesha. *Avidya leads to other kleshas.*

### 2. Asmita (Egoism)

As an adulteration of the Supreme Consciousness, we develop a sense of individuality. "Me, mine and I" become the root cause of our sense of duality.

> Asmita makes a person attached to material things, people and relationships. It also induces a person to claim ownership over ideas, concepts, experiences, viewpoints and beliefs. It is the glass that identifies the water of a river with a separate identity.
> â€” *Karma Sutra*, Ch.7, p.108

Over many lives the ego acquires a sense of uniqueness, taking asmita to greater levels and making its undoing more complicated. Getting rid of this conditioning is a prerequisite to spiritual advancement and freedom.

### 3. Raga (Attachment/Indulgence)

Raga leads a person to attachments, and attachments initiate different types of karmas. If one is fond of sensual pleasures, raga may lead to actions one later regrets â€” forcing someone into an extra-marital relationship and thereby accumulating expensive-to-pay karma. Many Indian kings were so lost in sensual indulgences that they allowed their kingdoms to be mismanaged, sometimes losing them entirely. Excessive entertainment, drinking, shopping â€” these are examples of raga.

### 4. Dwesa (Aversion)

The mirror image of raga. Dwesa can make a person miss out on many opportunities through aversion to them.

> My wife does not like eating Chinese food because she once fell sick after eating a Chinese meal. Her dislike is ingrained in her mind, and only she can overcome it.
> â€” Hingori, *Karma Sutra*, Ch.7, p.111

A bad experience with a frivolous sadhu could make one hate the spiritual subject entirely, blocking future enrichment. *The two kleshas of attraction and aversion, likes and dislikes, pleasure and pain are impediments to the evolution of a person.*

It is only at the centre of a seesaw that emotions are at equilibrium. There is no joy at the centre, but then there is also no sadness. If you want to reduce sorrow, you must first reduce joy. Bring your state of mind to the point of equanimity.

### 5. Abhinivesha (Fear/Insecurity)

Abhinivesha literally means clinging on to something â€” clinging onto life, fear of death, and fears of other kinds. The desire for self-preservation can lead to desperation and unstable thinking.

> Despite having faced death several times in the entirety of our external existence, it still remains our greatest fear. And instinctively so. This fear exists across species and in almost all human beings, even amongst the learned ones.
> â€” Hingori, *Karma Sutra*, Ch.7, p.112

Hingori notes that even with several out-of-body experiences, he can only claim 60 per cent conviction that he is an exception to this fear. The honest admission is instructive.

## Overcoming the Kleshas

### Avidya â€” counter-acted by gyan

Intuitive knowledge is the best form, but even reading and understanding the scriptures can make a difference. The father of knowledge is ignorance: as you realise your ignorance, it begins to fade. The teachings of an accomplished guru can lead from *avidya* to *vidya*, from darkness to light. Guru means remover of darkness.

### Asmita â€” understanding and acceptance

Comprehending that ego is part of the circle of maya is the starting point. The awareness of *asmita* helps one realise its isolation and hence its longing for association with the whole.

The supreme ego is different from Supreme Consciousness, though they share the word "supreme." When consciousness manifests into creation it acquires an identity â€” the Hindu concept of Shiv and Shakti, Father who art in Heaven, Allah, Bhagwan â€” and that is the supreme ego, not the individual one.

### Raga and Dwesa â€” yams and niyams

*Pratyahar* is the yogic science of withdrawing the mind from the objects of sense experience. The five *yams* are non-violence, truth, honesty, sensual abstinence, and non-possessiveness. The five *niyams* are cleanliness, contentment, austerity, self-study, and resignation to the Supreme. Self-discipline is the direct solution.

### Abhinivesha â€” stapled down with knowledge

When the fear of the unknown becomes a fear of the lesser known, it has already lost its power. Books, real-life experiences, and teachings can be relied upon to understand our fears and face them.

## The CCTV Method

> I myself found out noticing my interactions with the kleshas from outside was the best approach. It was like putting up a CCTV camera outside me to note what kleshas were afflicting me, when and how. Whenever this imaginary camera was on, I became aware of being a victim of one or a combination of kleshas. I would laugh at myself and the strength of the klesha would fade.
> â€” Hingori, *Karma Sutra*, Ch.7, p.114

This is the same witnessing-from-outside principle Hingori deploys in the [[practices/karmic-worksheet|karmic worksheet]]: becoming a conscious observer of one's own mind rather than its subject.

## The Klesha Cocktail â€” Asisa

In Ch.8, Hingori introduces [[concepts/asisa|asisa]] â€” the will of the jivaatma to exist eternally â€” as a composite force assembled from three kleshas working together:

- **Asmita** fuels the desire to preserve the 'I'-sense and individuality into perpetuity
- **Abhinivesha** fuels the fear of extinction and loss of individual identity across lives
- **Avidya** provides the underlying delusion that makes both seem real

Together they generate the force that keeps the jivaatma cycling through birth after birth, adding to its [[concepts/samskars|samskars]]. Recognising asisa as a *mechanism* rather than a truth is part of the gyan that can weaken it.

## Kleshas in the Karmashaya (Ch. III)

*Aatma Sutra* Ch.III reveals that klesha *trends* â€” the dominant stress patterns active in a given lifetime â€” are one of the three data sets configured within the karmashaya:

> "This container holds the software that organises the data expulsion of our *samskars*, the configuration of the *gunas*, and the trends of our *kleshas*."
> â€” Hingori, *Aatma Sutra*, Ch.III, p.25

This means the specific kleshas that dominate a person's life are not random â€” they are pre-configured in the causal body as part of the karmic schedule for that incarnation. See [[concepts/kaarna-sharir]].
