# Maya

> *"That which is not." The power of delusion through which each individual soul perceives itself as separate from the Supreme Consciousness.*

## Definition

> The power of delusion is called maya.
> — *Karma Sutra*, Ch.1, p.9

> Maya in Sanskrit means "that which is not". It is a deluded state wherein each individual soul, under a sense of false identity, considers itself to be separate and distinct from the Supreme Consciousness and the rest of the creation.
> — *Karma Sutra*, Ch.1, p.11

Maya is the field within which all ordinary experience occurs — the world as constructed by the five senses, organised by the mind, discriminated by the intellect, and believed as real.

## Core Teaching

> What we perceive, we believe. For us that is reality. So is every mirage until we discover otherwise.
> — *Karma Sutra*, Ch.1, p.9

Ordinary perception is partial and species-specific: turtles see shades of red, some animals see only black and white, humans perceive seven colours — which themselves are only 1/7 of the light spectrum. Our reality is built from a slim slice of the possible. Even the three states of waking, dreaming, and sleeping are, in the ancient view, part of the same delusionary field.

**Within maya, we live in the acceptance of duality** — right/wrong, left/right, good/bad. The mind projects these oppositions, and we mistake them for the world. This is why karma, experienced through duality, has its positive and negative valences. Transcend the duality and the law of karma loosens its grip.

## Nuances

### The Limits of Sense, Mind, and Intellect

Three faculties sustain maya:
1. **The five senses** — gather partial data
2. **The mind** — analyses it
3. **The intellect** — discriminates between perceptions and thoughts

The ancient teaching holds that the eternal truth lies **beyond all three** — reality can only be known by transcending sense, mind, and intellect together, at which point one discovers the non-dual oneness of the cosmos.

### Maya as Adulteration

Hingori offers a parallel: pure water that, over time, is mixed with additives and adulterations until it becomes beer or cola. Our original consciousness undergoes the same fragmentation through futile possessions and identifications — name, religion, status, social bonds.

> Realisation of adulteration leads to its eradication resulting in pure water. Likewise, realisation of futile possessions within oneself and around us results in finding one's true consciousness.
> — *Karma Sutra*, Ch.1, p.12

This reframes maya not merely as a trap but as **the mechanism of its own undoing** — the epiphany that liberates the soul is born within maya itself.

### The Catch of Good Deeds

Even positive karmas can fortify maya if performed with ego:

> Even doing good deeds can give a person pride in himself/herself. This too can fortify the sense of 'I' principle. The ego gets glorified and hence the gap widens.
> — *Karma Sutra*, Ch.1, p.12

This is the subtlest trap of maya: the spiritual seeker who does good but builds a self around the doing. See [[concepts/doer-ship]].

## In Practice

- **Do not trust perception as final.** Treat the sensed world as a working model, not the thing itself.
- **Notice identification.** Each time "I am my job / family / reputation / views" arises, recognise it as an adulteration, not a truth.
- **Do good without fortifying the doer.** Good deeds are useful; the pride in doing them is not.
- **Read the dream state as instructive.** Dreaming is not outside maya — it is another dimension of it. The Hingori Sutras treat dreams as spiritually charged territory in their own right (see [[sources/dream-sutra]] when ingested).

## The Advait Teaching — World as Brahman's Dream

*Aatma Sutra* Ch.VII takes the maya teaching into the non-dualist (*Advait*) school of Vedanta, drawing on [[entities/guru-vashisht]]'s teachings to Lord Ram as recorded in *Yog Vasishta, the Supreme Yoga* (tr. Swami Venkateshananda):

> "Whatever is seen, either in a dream or in the waking state, has space alone as support. It is born of space and it is of the nature of space (void). This space is none other than the supreme, infinite consciousness. Nothing, not even this body, has ever been created; hence nothing exists. The infinite consciousness experiences the existence as if in a dream. The diversity that arises in consciousness, on account of the limitlessness of its potentiality, appears to give rise to diversity of creatures."
> — Guru Vashisht, *Yog Vasishta* (quoted in *Aatma Sutra*, Ch.VII, p.73)

> "All things in this world are forever unreal, but they are also real because of the consciousness that is the sole reality and their content. Whatever the consciousness decides 'This is such and such' that it becomes, whether it is real or unreal. Such is the nature of consciousness."
> — Guru Vashisht, *Yog Vasishta* (quoted in *Aatma Sutra*, Ch.VII, p.74)

> [!tip] The Advait subtlety: the world is *not* simply "fake." It is unreal as something independent of Brahman; it is real *as* Brahman. Maya is not a mistake to be corrected but a mode of Brahman's self-experience. This is why the Yog Vasishta also says: "It is consciousness alone, which conceives of 'the other world' and experiences it as such."

## The Supreme Thought

Hingori translates the Advait cosmology into accessible terms. The capacity for creative visualisation — an artist imagining a "goat-man," a yogi creating a *nirman kaya*, a healer curing from across the world through visualisation — all point to the same underlying faculty. The consciousness supreme operates that faculty at a scale that dwarfs all human examples:

> "So, let us apply this concept at the level of the consciousness supreme. The visualisation by the consciousness supreme uses a zillion times more energy than humans. Being the supreme form of existence, the thought forms manifest as planets, beings, and other life forms."
> — Hingori, *Aatma Sutra*, Ch.VII, p.76

In this frame, Brahma (the creator, the *viraat* or cosmic person) is himself of pure consciousness — and the world is the dream-city Brahma has conceived. The analogy connects directly to [[concepts/astral-travel]]: just as a yogi's *nirman kaya* is a thought-form with an independent consciousness, the entire universe is the consciousness supreme's *nirman kaya* operating at the largest possible scale.

## Limitations of Perception — The Scientific Case

Hingori supplements the Vedantic argument with empirical facts, citing the narrowness of human sense-perception as modern corroboration of ancient maya teaching:

- **Sight**: less than 0.00000000005% of the electromagnetic spectrum is visible to the naked human eye.
- **Hearing**: less than 1% of all sounds on Earth are within human auditory range.
- **Motion**: you are travelling at 220 km/second across the Milky Way — imperceptible to every sense.
- **Matter**: atoms in the body are 99.9999999999999999% empty space. The "solid body" is almost entirely void.
- **Colour**: a rainbow's colours are not in the sky; they are generated by the eye's conical photoreceptor cells and interpreted by the brain. Animals without those cells see only grey.
- **Quantum physics**: the famous double-slit experiment proved that the "observed universe" is a function of the observer — a universe "out there" independent of an observer is itself an illusion.
- **Quantum entanglement**: particles communicate faster than the speed of light, puncturing Einstein's ceiling and opening a scientific gateway to telepathy.

> "Is science finally catching up? It will not be long before science proves that the observer, the observed, and the observation are all a part of the same consciousness."
> — Hingori, *Aatma Sutra*, Ch.VII, p.78

> [!tip] Hingori's method: ancient Vedantic insight + empirical scientific data as parallel tracks converging on the same conclusion. This dual-track approach (wisdom tradition → modern science) recurs across *Aatma Sutra* and is part of its rhetorical strategy for reaching readers who need empirical grounding before they can accept metaphysical claims.

## Maya Applied — Ram Beyond Guilt (Ch.XI)

*Aatma Sutra* Ch.XI deploys the maya teaching in a specific applied context: being beyond maya means being beyond the duality of right/wrong, and therefore beyond guilt. Hingori uses Lord Ram — taught by [[entities/guru-vashisht|Guru Vashisht]] that the world is pure maya — as the clearest available example.

Ram killed Bali from behind a tree (violating all norms of valour) and banished his wife Sita to the forest (acting against the expected norms of a husband). Both acts, viewed within maya's dualistic framework, generate guilt. Viewed from beyond maya, they are simply actions in a void:

> "Ram was taught by his teacher, Guru Vashisht, that the world was nothing but *maya* — a delusion of the mind. Nothing was real. Everything was a figment of the cosmic imagination, making duality unworthy of merit. Ram was thus beyond guilt. Surmounting duality took him beyond doership, leaving no justifications for right or wrong."
> — Hingori, *Aatma Sutra*, Ch.XI, p.138

Ram's own description of this state: *"I behold the world as pure, infinite, and indivisible consciousness. The world is otherwise a void, which disappears the moment illusion vanishes."* See [[stories/ram-speech-to-guru-vashisht]] and [[stories/ram-kills-bali-behind-tree]].

> [!warning] Hingori adds the necessary caveat: "It is only when you reach a particular level of evolution can you practice such a philosophy." Using maya as philosophical cover before reaching that level is the "path of convenience" — not the path of acceptance.

## Open Questions

> [!question] Is maya neutral or obstructive?
> Hingori calls maya both a deluded state *and* an epiphany with the power to liberate. The Yog Vasishta deepens this: the world is "unreal" as independent of Brahman but "real" as Brahman. Is maya best understood as an adversary to overcome, or as the very medium through which liberation becomes possible?

> [!question] Science and Vedanta convergence
> Hingori predicts science will prove that "the observer, the observed, and the observation are all a part of the same consciousness." The double-slit experiment points in this direction but does not yet reach the full non-dualist conclusion. How complete is the convergence, and does it matter to the practice?
