# Maya

> *"That which is not." The power of delusion through which each individual soul perceives itself as separate from the Supreme Consciousness.*

## Definition

> The power of delusion is called maya.
> — *Karma Sutra*, Ch.1, p.9

> Maya in Sanskrit means "that which is not". It is a deluded state wherein each individual soul, under a sense of false identity, considers itself to be separate and distinct from the Supreme Consciousness and the rest of the creation.
> — *Karma Sutra*, Ch.1, p.11

Maya is the field within which all ordinary experience occurs — the world as constructed by the five senses, organised by the mind, discriminated by the intellect, and believed as real.

## Core Teaching

> What we perceive, we believe. For us that is reality. So is every mirage until we discover otherwise.
> — *Karma Sutra*, Ch.1, p.9

Ordinary perception is partial and species-specific: turtles see shades of red, some animals see only black and white, humans perceive seven colours — which themselves are only 1/7 of the light spectrum. Our reality is built from a slim slice of the possible. Even the three states of waking, dreaming, and sleeping are, in the ancient view, part of the same delusionary field.

**Within maya, we live in the acceptance of duality** — right/wrong, left/right, good/bad. The mind projects these oppositions, and we mistake them for the world. This is why karma, experienced through duality, has its positive and negative valences. Transcend the duality and the law of karma loosens its grip.

## Nuances

### The Limits of Sense, Mind, and Intellect

Three faculties sustain maya:
1. **The five senses** — gather partial data
2. **The mind** — analyses it
3. **The intellect** — discriminates between perceptions and thoughts

The ancient teaching holds that the eternal truth lies **beyond all three** — reality can only be known by transcending sense, mind, and intellect together, at which point one discovers the non-dual oneness of the cosmos.

### Maya as Adulteration

Hingori offers a parallel: pure water that, over time, is mixed with additives and adulterations until it becomes beer or cola. Our original consciousness undergoes the same fragmentation through futile possessions and identifications — name, religion, status, social bonds.

> Realisation of adulteration leads to its eradication resulting in pure water. Likewise, realisation of futile possessions within oneself and around us results in finding one's true consciousness.
> — *Karma Sutra*, Ch.1, p.12

This reframes maya not merely as a trap but as **the mechanism of its own undoing** — the epiphany that liberates the soul is born within maya itself.

### The Catch of Good Deeds

Even positive karmas can fortify maya if performed with ego:

> Even doing good deeds can give a person pride in himself/herself. This too can fortify the sense of 'I' principle. The ego gets glorified and hence the gap widens.
> — *Karma Sutra*, Ch.1, p.12

This is the subtlest trap of maya: the spiritual seeker who does good but builds a self around the doing. See [[concepts/doer-ship]].

## In Practice

- **Do not trust perception as final.** Treat the sensed world as a working model, not the thing itself.
- **Notice identification.** Each time "I am my job / family / reputation / views" arises, recognise it as an adulteration, not a truth.
- **Do good without fortifying the doer.** Good deeds are useful; the pride in doing them is not.
- **Read the dream state as instructive.** Dreaming is not outside maya — it is another dimension of it. The Hingori Sutras treat dreams as spiritually charged territory in their own right (see [[sources/dream-sutra]] when ingested).
