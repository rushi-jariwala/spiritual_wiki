# Moksha (and Mukti)

> *The final liberation — the merging of the individual fragment back into the Supreme Consciousness.*

## Definition

Two related but distinct terms:

- **[[concepts/mukti|Mukti]]** — the intermediate state; karmashaya substantially reduced; long intervals in higher realms (1,000–2,000 years); the spirit still exists as an individual
- **Moksha** — the final dissolution; complete deletion of karmashaya data; the jivaatma merges back into the [[concepts/aatma|aatma]] or consciousness supreme, losing all individual identification

> From one to the multiple and from the multiple back to one is called moksha (ek se anek aur anek se ek).
> — Hingori, *Karma Sutra*, Ch.1, p.11

*Aatma Sutra* sharpens the distinction further, defining moksh as "disintegration of the self into the consciousness supreme" — not just freedom from rebirth but the erasure of the self as a separate entity:

> It is only when the *karmashaya* has managed to delete or cleanse its stored data, can a person get freedom from life and death and attain *moksh*, the ultimate level of attainment.
> — Hingori, *Aatma Sutra*, Ch.I, p.11

## Core Teaching

Mukti is the *precondition*; moksha is the *completion*. The body is the [[concepts/karma|karma kshetra]] — the field where karmas are worked out, exhausted, and squared up:

> Only when a body is free of karma can it attain mukti, i.e., freedom from birth and death, and finally attain moksha, or becoming one with the Supreme. In order to achieve this, the individual has to ensure that fresh karmas stop getting generated.
> — *Karma Sutra*, Ch.1, p.2

This is the whole spiritual project in one sentence: **work out old karma, generate no new karma, return to source.**

## Nuances

### Why It's Hard

> Intellectually, one can read the last sentence and understand it, but practically it's almost impossible to achieve! From the time we wake up to the time we sleep, we are engaged in different types of karmas.
> — *Karma Sutra*, Ch.1, p.2

Moksha is not mysterious in principle. It is difficult in practice because ordinary waking life continuously generates karma. This is why the Sutras emphasise practical strategies: converting karma to [[concepts/kriyaman-karma|kriyaman]], avoiding [[concepts/doer-ship|doer-ship]], and leaning on the guru.

### The Catch of the Good Seeker

Even the pursuit of moksha can backfire:

> Individual entities often widen the gap and the karma philosophy backfires rather than becoming a solution. Fame, self-importance, worldly significance, attachments, etc., make us believe that we are special, different from others and not one of the whole. Even doing good deeds can give a person pride in himself/herself. This too can fortify the sense of 'I' principle. The ego gets glorified and hence the gap widens.
> — *Karma Sutra*, Ch.1, p.12

The subtlest obstacle to moksha is the spiritual ego — the self that is "advanced." This is the trap the seeker must most carefully watch for.

### The Kosha Ladder

The route from jivaatma to moksha is structural: one must climb through the [[concepts/koshas|five koshas]] — the sheaths that surround the individual self. This is not a shortcut; it is a graded traversal.

## In Practice

- **Exhaust without accumulating.** Balance old karma but do not create new.
- **Avoid identification.** Each "I am" that forms around success, virtue, or even spiritual progress adds another layer to the gap.
- **Watch for pride in good deeds.** Do the good. Do not collect it.
- **Take refuge in the guru.** A sadguru can materially alter what would otherwise be impossible alone — see [[concepts/guru-disciple]].
