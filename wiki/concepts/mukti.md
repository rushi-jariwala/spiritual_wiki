# Mukti

## Definition

**Mukti** is the intermediate state of liberation in which the [[concepts/kaarna-sharir|karmashaya]] has been substantially reduced — not yet blank, but light enough that the spirit is freed from the compulsion of frequent rebirth. In this state, the spirit body can remain in higher realms for **a thousand to even two thousand years** before its next incarnation.

> When the karmashaya has reduced baggage and data, there are fewer negative thoughts that would come to the fore on a regular basis. There would be less lives to lead, and longer intervals where the spirit body could live in higher realms than the Earth, for a thousand to even two thousand years. This state is called *mukti*.
> — Hingori, *Aatma Sutra*, Ch.I, p.10

Mukti is distinct from [[concepts/moksha|moksh]]: moksh is the complete deletion of karmashaya data and the full dissolution of self into consciousness supreme. Mukti is the earned breathing room between the two.

## The Nirman Kaya

Even while still alive in physical form, an evolved spiritualist can create a **nirman kaya** — an additional or duplicate spirit body — out of their own spiritual energy. This gives the spiritualist, even in physical incarnation, two significant capacities:

1. **Seva** independent of the physical body — service performed through the nirman kaya while the physical body is at rest or occupied elsewhere
2. **Accelerated karma exhaustion** — the nirman kaya allows the fructification of stored karmas in the karmashaya without requiring new physical embodiment for each one

> Even while alive, there are opportunities for the spirit body to do things independent of its physical body. This is done through an additional or duplicate spirit body called the *nirman kaya*. An evolved spiritualist can create his *nirman kaya*, while in physical form, out of his own spiritual energy. Thus, the *nirman kaya* gives the spiritualist, even while alive, opportunities to do things (like *seva*) independent of his physical body. More importantly, the *nirman kaya* gives the evolved spiritualist, even while alive, a chance for the fructification of his stored *karmas* in the *karmashaya*, in order to exhaust his *karmas* quickly.
> — Hingori, *Aatma Sutra*, Ch.I, pp.10–11

Hingori acknowledges this sounds like science fiction — "maybe the *yogic* version of Matrix Reloaded" — but states he has experienced it directly.

Chapter VI provides the clearest live demonstrations:
- **Ashish at Lemon Tree Resort, Goa**: his spiritual guru appeared across from him at lunch (physically in another city) and told him not to eat non-veg on Thursday; "his second body seemed to have a will of its own."
- **Virenderji (retired judge, Haryana)**: three friends arrived saying Virenderji had invited them to the park that morning — but he had overslept and missed his walk. "A clear example of how another version of you can exist whilst on a path of spiritual evolution." See [[stories/virenderji-uninvited-guests]].
- **Gurudev at two places**: seen simultaneously at two locations by different people. (*Aatma Sutra*, Ch.VI, p.71)

## Erasing Samskars Through Memory

One practical pointer:

> By erasing your long-term memories, you can erase the *samskars* and reduce your *karmas*, or "stock in trade".
> — Hingori, *Aatma Sutra*, Ch.I, p.11

Short-term memory (smruti) eventually merges into samskars. Working to dissolve identification with long-term memories is one path toward reducing karmashaya data.

## Mukti vs Moksh

| | Mukti | Moksh |
|---|---|---|
| **Karmashaya state** | Substantially reduced | Fully deleted / blank |
| **Rebirth** | Still possible; long intervals | No more rebirth |
| **Realms** | Higher realms for 1,000–2,000 years | Dissolution into consciousness supreme |
| **Self** | Still individual | Self disintegrates into the whole |

> [!tip] Siddhi renunciation as threshold to higher attainment
> A cross-source pattern across [[sources/guru-of-gurus]] and the *Aatma Sutra* biographical introduction: Gurudev acquired *siddhis* under his first mentor Sitaram ji of Dasua in his youth, then renounced all of them before his ascent as a mahaguru (described in Ch.1 as renouncing them "before his ascent as a mahaguru"; in *Aatma Sutra* as surrendering them in the Ganges at Haridwar at age 35). In both accounts, the first phase of spiritual attainment — siddhi acquisition — must be deliberately surrendered as a condition for the second phase. The siddhis are described as "mediocre compared to the powers he attained as a mahaguru." This inversion (gaining to give away) is structurally consistent with the non-doership / mukti framework: the capacities that feed ego-ownership must be released before subtler powers — those that cannot be claimed as "mine" — can arise.
