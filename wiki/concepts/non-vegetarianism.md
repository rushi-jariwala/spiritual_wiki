# Non-Vegetarianism

## Core Teaching

Eating meat and fish is a **karmic debit**, not a moral prohibition. Hingori does not frame vegetarianism as a spiritual virtue or religious duty — he frames non-vegetarianism as an **expensive transaction** that depletes the karmic balance sheet.

> Eating meat and fish may be a delicious experience but an expensive one. You pay for it in two ways; money in the physical form and debit in the spiritual.
> — Hingori, *Karma Sutra*, Ch.2, p.37

The debt is graduated: the higher the animal's evolutionary level, the greater the karmic cost.

## The Karmic Hierarchy of Meat

| Animal | Relative Cost | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| Chicken / Pork | Lowest | Still a significant lifetime accumulation |
| Goat | Higher than chicken/pork | 1 goat = feed 300 people to balance |
| Beef | Beyond all others | Cow is itself a major creditor (see [[concepts/karmic-debt]]) |

## Quantified Costs

Hingori provides concrete numbers to make the math inescapable:

**Goat:**
> One has to provide food for at least 300 people to pay for the life of a goat consumed by a person.
> — *Karma Sutra*, Ch.2, p.37

**Chicken — a lifetime calculation:**
> If a person eats only half-a-chicken a day, he then consumes 175 full chickens in a year, 1,820 in a decade and 5,000 in less than 30 years. If he/she consumes meat for 50 years, he/she has to pay back the karma for 5,000 chickens and more!
> — *Karma Sutra*, Ch.2, p.37

> If consumption of one chicken's negative life karma can be balanced by feeding say 50 people only, you still need to feed 4 lakh people. Is that a doable number?
> — *Karma Sutra*, Ch.2, p.37

**Hence:**

> Saints call for vegetarianism.
> — *Karma Sutra*, Ch.2, p.37

## Vegetarianism as Karmic Strategy

The argument is not *don't eat meat because it is cruel or religiously prohibited.* The argument is *don't eat meat because the repayment obligation is arithmetically unmanageable in a single lifetime.* Plants carry the lowest debt; animals the highest.

> Even though plants are the lowest form of life we consume, it is still a debt, though a lesser debt than animal life.
> — *Karma Sutra*, Ch.2, p.35

This reframes vegetarianism as **rational karmic accounting**, not piety.

> [!tip]
> The positive balance of karma gets depleted with negative karmic acquisitions from meat. To overcome this debit, one has to balance it with a lot of good deeds — but the numbers make that a nearly impossible task for a lifelong non-vegetarian.

## Nuances

- **Intent is still the primary karmic variable** (see [[concepts/karma]]). However, in the case of dietary karma, the *act itself* — consuming animal life — generates debt regardless of intent, because a life was taken.
- The debt is not about *killing* the animal personally. It accrues to the consumer.
- The higher the animal's evolution, the more valuable the life consumed, and the greater the repayment required.
