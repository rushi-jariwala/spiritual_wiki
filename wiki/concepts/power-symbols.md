# Power Symbols

## Definition

**Power symbols** are spiritual certifications that appear on the body of an evolved spiritualist, indicating that their aura is fortified with the abilities the symbols represent — and that they are qualified to use them. They are not acquired through deliberate practice; they manifest as a consequence of the level of consciousness attained.

> Many of Gurudev's powers manifested as symbols on his body, indicating that his aura was fortified with the abilities represented by these symbols. Their presence also implied that he was qualified to use them.
> — Hingori, *The Guru of Gurus*, p.236

## The Shiv-Parivaar on Gurudev's Hands

The mahaguru carried the entire Shiv-parivaar on his hands. On his right hand: OM, Nandi, gileri, and the shivling with a snake twined around it. On his left hand: the symbols of OM with a trishul passing through it, Ganpati, and the jyot. These symbols were seen by many disciples. Some also noted:

- Three pindis on Gurudev's right hand (Santlal ji believed he acquired them from the Vaishno Devi shrine)
- Trishuls on his fingernails (Santlal ji)
- A Sudarshan Chakra on one hand (Mataji)
- The OM occasionally radiating so much light that it seemed encased in a silver ring

The symbols glowed from both palms and became even more distinct when he sprinkled water on them.

## Each Symbol and Its Meaning

### OM

The OM is a certification standard of divinity that positions the bearer in the league of the divine. It denotes a certain level of consciousness — enhanced connectivity with all living forms. It implies association with and influence over all three loka groups: the Netherworlds, the Earth, and the higher worlds.

Gurudev had the OM on his hands, chest, back, and forehead. He told Surinder Kaushal ji: *"OM is the sound that created everything in the universe. OM is the guru. When you chant OM, you chant my name, and when you think of OM, you think of me."*

### Trishul

The trishul is a weapon of self-protection and preservation of self and others. It represents an increase in Shakti (creative energies) and the ability to use it for healing.

Before Gurudev attained the trishul, he and his disciples would internalise negativity while healing others. After acquiring the trishul, he distributed it to his disciples:

> "You don't need to absorb the disease because you now have the Shakti to deal with it."
> — Gurudev, *The Guru of Gurus*, p.237

### Shivling with Gileri (and the Snake)

The shivling with the gileri represents the masculine and feminine energy principles. Their symbol on the hand denotes triumph over duality — not only at the conscious and subconscious levels, but also at the unconscious level. In lay terms, it signifies the attainment of the *Ardhanarishvara* form. The snake twined around the shivling complements the ability to use Shakti to help, protect, and defend.

### Ganpati

Ganpati is the astral force activated when the masculine and feminine energy principles merge. Ganpati rules the Mooladhara chakra, which is home to Bhu-loka (the earth plane). Its benevolence helps overcome obstacles that prevent the kundalini from rising to higher chakras, thereby reducing ignorance at both microcosmic and macrocosmic levels.

Many disciples saw a quarter-inch Ganpati graphic superimposed like a sculpture on Gurudev's and some of their own hands.

### Nandi

Nandi is Shiv's vahaan (vehicle). Though Shiv can manifest in anyone or anything and requires no transportation, the Nandi symbol signifies Shiv's ascent due to the following:

- It represents the power to protect or destroy; denotes constant awareness and alertness; serves as a sentinel to Shiv's power
- Seated facing the Shivling in most temples, Nandi exemplifies patience, inaction, and unwavering devotion
- Nandi is an animal form of great gunas — strength, loyalty, and surrender. The spirit within Nandi can humanise itself after becoming the object of worship

### Jyot

The jyot connotes the light of manifested and unmanifested fires. Like the sun, the gross fire is visible light; the subtle fires are mostly invisible. The jivaatma is also a light — though often invisible to naked eyes, its radiation can sometimes be seen or felt.

The jyot appears as a transparent but permanent skin blister on the left hand. Gurudev is believed to have attained this power from Jwalaji, a temple of dynamic fires with no known source.

**Transfer of the jyot** — a visible flame entered the sthan at Khar through a window, travelled across to where VP Sharma ji (one of Gurudev's disciples) was seated, and lodged itself in his left hand in full public view. He received it without knowing — it was only when his guru later hinted at it that he became aware of the bestowal.

## Transfer to Disciples

Gurudev transferred power symbols in several ways: placing his hand on people's heads, giving them his sipped water to drink, or simply willing the transfer from thousands of miles away. He did not ascribe to any standard procedure.

Many disciples received the jyot or other symbols unknowingly — remaining unaware of the bestowal until their guru hinted or casually mentioned it. The symbol qualifies the recipient but not always at a conscious level, and unlike Gurudev, many disciples did not know how to assess their attainments consciously.

> [!tip] The distinction between power symbols and siddhis: siddhis are acquired, exercised, and can be renounced (as the mahaguru demonstrated at Har ki Pauri). Power symbols are certifications of *what a being is* — they cannot be renounced because they are not possessions. They are the aura's own signature.
