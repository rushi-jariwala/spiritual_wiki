# Pratipakshbhavana (Superimposing Thoughts)

## Definition

*Pratipakshbhavana* is a yogic mind practice that teaches you to cultivate a positive thought every time a negative one enters the mind. Literally, it is "opposite attitude" (*pratipaksha* = opposite side; *bhavana* = attitude/feeling). It is a device for creating an opposite or corrective orientation toward thoughts and feelings that disturb the mind.

The fourth of the five key formulas of evolution in *Aatma Sutra* Ch.IX, it is especially practical when [[concepts/vairagya|vairagya]] and [[concepts/empathy|empathy]] feel out of reach.

> "*Pratipakshbhavana* is one of the yogic mind practices that teaches you to cultivate a positive thought every time a negative one enters the mind. In other words, it is a device for creating an opposite attitude towards thoughts or feelings that cause a disturbance in your mind."
> â€” Hingori, *Aatma Sutra*, Ch.IX, p.110

## Core Teaching

When a negative thought arises â€” animosity toward someone who was rude, frustration at a person's behaviour â€” *pratipakshbhavana* does not suppress it. Instead, it superimposes a replacement thought that defuses the negative charge:

> "If someone is rude to you then instead of naturally developing animosity towards them, *pratipakshbhavana* teaches you to look at them in the opposite manner. You can convince yourself that maybe the person was going through stress and did not realise how they were speaking or that perhaps, it was your destiny to face a rude reaction from someone, or perhaps something that you said might have initiated this reaction."
> â€” Hingori, *Aatma Sutra*, Ch.IX, p.110

This is not self-deception. The substitute thought engages the *same* intellect and applies the conceptual frameworks (destiny, karma, causation) already accepted as true. The result is that the negative thought loses its grip before it can encode itself as a new [[concepts/samskars|samskar]].

## The Golden Rule

> **Golden Rule:** Instead of looking at the rate of success, look at the rate of decreasing failure.
> â€” Hingori, *Aatma Sutra*, Ch.IX, p.111

Progress in *pratipakshbhavana* is not measured by how often you generate the positive thought perfectly. It is measured by how often the negative thought fails to take root. The baseline improves incrementally.

## Patanjali on Anger-Samskars

> "Expose the mind to constant thoughts of anger and resentment, and you will find these anger-waves build up *anger-samskars*, which will predispose you to find occasions for anger permeating your daily life. A man with well-developed *anger-samskars* is said to have a bad temper."
> â€” Maharishi Patanjali, quoted in *Aatma Sutra*, Ch.IX, p.111

This is the mechanism *pratipakshbhavana* is designed to interrupt. Each negative thought that goes uncountered deepens the samskar channel. Each substituted thought is a small act of erosion on that channel.

## Gurudev's Variation â€” Visualisation

Hingori recounts a direct teaching from Gurudev on handling unwanted attraction:

> "I once went to Gurudev and said, 'What should I do if I get sensual thoughts while treating women?' He smiled as if he had been asked that question a hundred times before. He said, 'While treating a woman, visualise her as an infant and then an old lady.'"
> â€” Hingori, *Aatma Sutra*, Ch.IX, p.111

This is *pratipakshbhavana* applied not through an opposite emotion but through a sequence of visualisations that dissolves the original charge. Hingori developed his own variant: visualising the skeleton of the person, "eliminating any chance of attraction."

## The Cricket Analogy

Knowing the principle does not deliver the reflex:

> "*Pratipakshabhavana* is easier explained than practised. I urge you to think about a game of cricket. Technically, it is almost impossible to see a ball at 100 miles an hour and then decide how to play it. Few batsmen can do that. So how do they hit a ball that moves faster than you can think? It is called practice. It also leads to muscle memory."
> â€” Hingori, *Aatma Sutra*, Ch.IX, p.111

The analogy is exact: negative thought-waves arrive faster than conscious decision-making. The substitute thought must become automatic â€” a trained reflex, not a deliberate calculation. This requires going "to the nets" and practising until the response is instinctive.

## Application to Self-Acceptance

*Pratipakshbhavana* is also pointed inward:

> "When you find a fault with yourself, you can look at the flip side and say, 'These days, I dislike myself less.' And then say, 'I hardly dislike myself', which will eventually lead to a feeling wherein you will not dislike yourself at all. From here on, you can start accepting yourself unquestioningly."
> â€” Hingori, *Aatma Sutra*, Ch.IX, pp.111â€“112

This application connects the concept directly to the arc of Chapters XIâ€“XII (*Accepting Yourself*, *Falling in Love with Yourself*): *pratipakshbhavana* is how the internal narrative is systematically revised, one thought at a time.

## Nuances

### Not the Same as Positive Thinking

*Pratipakshbhavana* does not instruct you to think happy thoughts. It instructs you to generate a thought that is *contextually accurate within the framework* you already believe in â€” one grounded in karma, destiny, empathy, and the understanding of duality. The counter-thought is not arbitrary; it is a *correction* based on what is actually true.

### Relationship to Vitarks and Vichars

*Pratipakshbhavana* operates on the *vichar* level â€” it is a deliberate rational response to the involuntary *vitark* (anti-yam thought wave). See [[concepts/vitarks-vichars]]. The practice does not prevent *vitarks* from arising; it prevents them from converting into samskars by substituting a more accurate *vichar*.
