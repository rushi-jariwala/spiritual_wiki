# Self-Acceptance

> *"Who are you to blame me? You are my painting. I am the artist. By blaming yourself, you are blaming me."*
> — Divine voice to Hingori, *Aatma Sutra*, Ch.XI, p.136

## Definition

Self-acceptance is the foundational practice of Part C of *Aatma Sutra* — the critical first step toward self-love and eventual self-worship. It is the capacity to hold oneself "on an as-is-where-is basis with all our flaws and faults, and all our thoughts — good and bad," including acceptance of one's destiny.

> "By the end of this chapter, it is my hope that, you will learn to assimilate the concept of self-acceptance, which is critical to reaching your spiritual goals. However, the obstacles are substantial. Circumvention is key."
> — Hingori, *Aatma Sutra*, Ch.XI, p.128

The image Hingori uses at the chapter's close: pure water that "feels guilty" of losing its purity when adulterated by coffee, milk, and sugar. The water needs to love itself as pure water and recognise the adulterations as removable through *shuddhi*. The self — however obscured by circumstance, guilt, or societal evaluation — is always restorable to its essential purity.

## Obstacles to Self-Acceptance

### Societal Norms of Evaluation

Hingori catalogs the societal gauntlet through which self-opinion passes across a lifetime:

- **Age**: at no stage is a person deemed to possess the right balance of maturity, experience, and relevance. The young are naive; the middle-aged are preoccupied; the older are no longer relevant.
- **Profession**: every rung of the career ladder carries its own stress and diminishment — juniors curb their intelligence to survive; seniors carry accountability with fierce competition.
- **Physical appearance**: most people are dissatisfied with their looks. "Look at the inner qualities and not the outer beauty" (*seerat ko dekho, surat ko nahi*) — Hingori's grandfather's instruction, easily given but rarely absorbed.
- **Social background**: caste, community, region, education, religion — in India especially, these categories invite societal disapproval and outright insult. Each is a potential wound to self-opinion.
- **Negative feedback**: from parents who reprimand instead of counsel, teachers who damage self-esteem instead of building it, friends quick to criticise. "Thus, as we grow older, we have received so much negative feedback that we stop believing in ourselves."

### Destiny as Obstacle

Destiny does not always allow hopes and aspirations to be fulfilled. Despite sincere effort, outcomes disappoint. Even successful people feel let down when fate intervenes. The adage holds: *"Nanak dukhiya sab sansaar"* — the world is always in a state of unhappiness. And when tides turn in one's favour, the resulting confidence often tips into pride — another distortion of self-image.

### Self-Blame

Self-blame is the internal amplification of external obstacles. Girls who remain unmarried blame themselves for lacking something. Couples experiencing relationship breakdown blame themselves. Those unable to conceive blame themselves. The pattern is pervasive, and the chapter's argument is that it is philosophically misplaced: karmas are already lined up for fructification, so personal blame — whether for late marriage, divorce, or any apparent failure — is a misdirection of cause.

> *Karmas* are already lined up for fructification, so you have no choice but to accept them.
> — Hingori, *Aatma Sutra*, Ch.XI, p.134

See [[stories/rabia-of-basra-sufi-saint]] — sold as a slave, forced into prostitution, she refused to blame herself. She prayed herself into *dhyaan*, and her master freed her when he saw the ethereal lantern over her head. She became a great Sufi saint. Hingori's point: self-blame would have foreclosed all of that.

## Guilt — The Double-Edged Sword

Guilt is presented as the deepest and most complex obstacle to self-acceptance:

> "Guilt is a double-edged sword. On the one hand, it is a downer for self-esteem, preventing us from seeing our true nature, which is beyond earthly perceptions of right and wrong. On the other hand, without such moral compasses, a person would feel free to harm others, to indulge in negative behaviour without restraint or repercussions."
> — Hingori, *Aatma Sutra*, Ch.XI, p.135

Guilt is a moral scaffolding instilled by parents and society. It teaches consequences. But at the spiritual level, it operates as an obstacle: guilt re-encodes the act as a *samskar*, programs a self-punishment into the unconscious, and prevents the seeker from recognising their inherent worth. It must be surmounted — not by abandoning moral responsibility, but by releasing the *ownership* of the act that generated it.

> "Guilt at a conscious level can be an insurmountable obstacle to self-acceptance."
> — Hingori, *Aatma Sutra*, Ch.XI, p.142

## Surmounting Guilt

### The Mechanism: Non-Doership + Destiny Acceptance

The chapter's synthesis: guilt arises when we take doer-ship over destined events. The act was already encoded in the karmic programme. Acceptance of destiny as the "sole creative force for our actions" removes the grounds for guilt:

> "I accept that my destiny created my fit of rage, which resulted in me slapping the domestic worker. Acceptance of destiny means acceptance of the fact that destiny is the sole creative force for our actions. Therefore, we ought not to take ownership or doership of our actions."
> — Hingori, *Aatma Sutra*, Ch.XI, p.136

There is also the destiny of the other person: the domestic worker may have been fated to go through the misery of being ill-treated. The wrongdoer is the pawn; guilt enters only through the crevice of ownership.

See [[stories/hingori-meat-chopper-guilt]] for the full account. See also [[synthesis/destiny-and-doership]] for the larger framework.

> [!tip] The advocacy here is not a license to act wrongly — Hingori pre-empts this explicitly. Acceptance is not convenience. It is the recognition that guilt compounds error by manufacturing a second karmic event: the self-punishment *samskar*.

### Two Guilts Become None

A striking mechanism: when the conditions that generate guilt themselves cancel each other, the guilt dissolves. Hingori lied to his cousin Neena; felt guilty. She then revealed that Gurudev had already confirmed Hingori's agreement in Gurgaon — which meant the lie had not been a lie at all in the deeper sense. The two guilts cancelled each other:

> "I now realise that truth can neither be limited to, nor interpreted by the five senses. Being pronounced guilty by reference to the senses alone lacks perfection."
> — Hingori, *Aatma Sutra*, Ch.XI, p.142

See [[stories/hingori-neena-two-guilts]].

### Ram: Beyond Guilt (The Advanced Example)

For the spiritually evolved, the resolution goes further: being beyond *maya* means being beyond the duality of right and wrong, which means being beyond guilt entirely.

Ram is Hingori's example. Taught by [[entities/guru-vashisht|Guru Vashisht]] that the world is pure *maya* — a figment of the cosmic imagination, with duality unworthy of merit — Ram acted from beyond the framework of right and wrong altogether:

- He killed Bali from behind a tree, violating every norm of valour. He was beyond guilt for it. See [[stories/ram-kills-bali-behind-tree]].
- He banished Sita to the forest. He had no malice for Ravan and no guilt for the banishment. See [[stories/ram-banishes-sita]].

Ram's own account of his state, in response to Guru Vashisht: [[stories/ram-speech-to-guru-vashisht]].

> "I behold the world as pure, infinite, and indivisible consciousness. The world is otherwise a void, which disappears the moment illusion vanishes... I am at peace. My vision is clear... I am free from hopes and desires."
> — Lord Ram, *Supreme Yoga* (Venkateshananda), quoted in *Aatma Sutra*, Ch.XI, pp.138–139

> [!warning] Hingori is careful: this is only achievable at a particular level of evolution. Acting as Ram did *before* reaching that level — and using it as philosophical cover — is precisely the path of convenience he warns against.

## In Practice

The chapter's practical formula for self-acceptance:
1. **Recognise the societal mirror for what it is**: others have no right to define your worth. Their evaluations are projections through their own limitations.
2. **Reframe self-blame through destiny**: planetary configurations are known to produce late marriages, divorces, and apparent failures. You are not the cause.
3. **Dissolve guilt through non-doership**: refuse to accept ownership of the act. The act was destined; the guilt is the gratuitous addition.
4. **Accept potential across all phases**: self-acceptance is not only of the present cross-section. "If you have embarked on the voyage of self-discovery, then give yourself a fair chance." Ch.XII deepens this into the **Phase A-to-Z** teaching — Valmiki (bandit to *maharishi*), Vishvamitra (arrogant king to *brahmarishi*), Rajeev (alcoholic to healer) — the latent saint is a standard feature. See [[concepts/self-love]].
5. **Watch yourself do good**: new positive impressions are superimposed on negative *samskars* through repeated self-observation of worthy deeds. Repeated actions build self-respect, which builds self-love.

## The Three-Stage Arc

Self-acceptance is Stage 1 of *Aatma Sutra*'s Part C:

**Self-acceptance → Self-love → Self-worship**

Self-acceptance generates self-like; self-like generates self-love; self-love that permeates all layers of consciousness becomes *bhakti* (self-worship). See [[concepts/self-love]] and [[concepts/self-worship]].

> "Why judge yourself at the cross-section today? If you have embarked on the voyage of self-discovery, then give yourself a fair chance."
> — Hingori, *Aatma Sutra*, Ch.XI, p.154
