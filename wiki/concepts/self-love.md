# Self-Love

> *"Love, to most people, is an enhanced version of like. They need to like themselves before they can consider the evolved concept of self-love."*
> â€” Hingori, *Aatma Sutra*, Ch.XII, p.149

## Definition

Self-love is the second stage of the Part C arc in *Aatma Sutra*, positioned between self-acceptance and self-worship. It is not emotional, not possessive, and not narcissistic. Hingori defines it as an **unemotional, agenda-free connectivity** to all forms of the consciousness supreme â€” within and without. You love the *aatman* in yourself by learning to love the *aatman* in everything outside you.

> "I believe love is devoid of emotion. Love is not a quid pro quo, nor is it something you direct towards a specific person or even a set of people. It is an attitude with an underlying theme, wherein you love all forms of the consciousness supreme outside of you, and in doing so, you can love the same spark of the consciousness supreme within you."
> â€” Hingori, *Aatma Sutra*, Ch.XII, p.145

The hierarchy is explicit: **self-acceptance â†’ self-like â†’ self-respect â†’ self-love â†’ bhakti (self-worship)**. Once self-acceptance permeates from one layer of consciousness to the next, the divinity within becomes accessible. Continuous indulgence in the subject attracts the desire and understanding of *bhakti*.

## Love as Patanjali Saw It

Hingori invokes Patanjali to ground the concept philosophically:

> "Human love is the highest emotion most of us know. It frees us to some extent from our egotism in our relation to one or more individuals. But human love is still possessive and exclusive. Love for the *aatman* is neither. What people 'really are' is the *aatman*, nothing less. To love the *aatman* in ourselves is to love it everywhere. And, consequently, to love the *aatman* everywhere is to go to the reality beyond any manifestation of nature. Such a love is too vast to be understood by ordinary minds, and yet, it is simply an infinite deepening and expansion of the little limited love we all experience."
> â€” Patanjali, quoted by Hingori, *Aatma Sutra*, Ch.XII, p.147

## What Self-Love Is Not

### Not Romantic or Familial Love

The chapter opens by distinguishing self-love from the dominant cultural definitions â€” romantic love (Shah Jahan and Mumtaz), familial love ("your loved ones"), and cult-love (pride in being Indian, Punjabi, Khanna). These forms are agenda-laden: love for one's own fraternity within concentric circles. They are not love "for the sake of love itself" but for the sake of the group.

### Not Attention-Seeking

> "Film stars spend half their lives trying to get into the public eye, and the other half wearing dark glasses to hide from it."
> â€” quoted by Hingori, *Aatma Sutra*, Ch.XII, p.150

Hingori observes that many famous people become victims of constant attention â€” positive or negative. Once the attention wanes, depression follows. His psychologist's advice: "give positive attention to your wife, because if you did not, she would rather receive negative attention than none at all." Self-love that depends on external recognition is its opposite in disguise. True self-love seeks *vairagya* and solitude â€” not applause.

> "Once you see the spark of divinity within you, you will not want attention from anyone else. You will look for *vairagya* and that solitude will give you contentment."
> â€” Hingori, *Aatma Sutra*, Ch.XII, p.131 *(approx.)*

### Not Narcissism or Vanity

> "Let us not, even for a moment, imagine, that 'falling in love with yourself' is a case of vanity or narcissism. Neither is it about how impressive you are, nor how popular."
> â€” Hingori, *Aatma Sutra*, Ch.XII, p.148

Self-love as Hingori defines it encompasses all the *selves* â€” body, *citt* (mind), and spirit â€” accepted on an as-is-where-is basis, including all flaws, faults, thoughts good and bad, and destiny. Vanity privileges the outer body; self-love includes and transcends it.

## Gurudev's Teaching Lived

Hingori practised non-attachment on Gurudev's instruction, wondering if it made him a cold father, a distant husband. What he found instead was an underlying connectivity â€” not emotional, but unemotional caring for all life forms, known and unknown, rich and poor, good and even bad.

> "I found an underlying connectivity to most human beings, known or unknown, rich or poor, good and even bad. The kind of love I entertained earlier had an emotional connect, which was replaced by an unemotional caring for all life forms."
> â€” Hingori, *Aatma Sutra*, Ch.XII, p.147

This is Hingori's personalised enactment of self-love: role-play without emotional entrapment; duty without attachment; connectivity without concentric circles.

## Watch Yourself Do Good

Self-love is not only a philosophical stance; it is built through direct self-observation:

> "When you see yourself doing things that you find likable or lovable, your self-worth goes up."
> â€” Hingori, *Aatma Sutra*, Ch.XII, p.151

The mechanism: new positive impressions are superimposed on existing negative *samskars*, gradually changing the *karmashaya* blueprint. Repeated positive action â†’ repeated self-observation of that action â†’ accumulated self-respect â†’ self-love.

**Summarised practical steps:**
- Our actions are likable.
- Our attitude is lovable.
- Our deeds are worthy.

When ownership of a deed is not taken (non-doership), one becomes free of positive *and* negative emotion â€” the resulting equanimity becomes a clear lens for self-impression.

## Judge Yourself from Phase A to Z

The chapter's most important corrective for low self-love: you cannot assess yourself by your current cross-section alone.

> "Why judge yourself at the cross-section today? If you have embarked on the voyage of self-discovery, then give yourself a fair chance."
> â€” Hingori, *Aatma Sutra*, Ch.XII, p.154

Hingori's examples:
- **Valmiki** â€” bandit and murderer at one stage of life; later wrote the Ramayan and is revered as a *maharishi*. [[entities/valmiki]]
- **Vishvamitra** â€” arrogant king who sought revenge on Guru Vashisht; failed many times; eventually reached the title of *brahmarishi* (most advanced in spiritual practice). [[entities/vishvamitra]]
- **Rajeev** â€” alcoholic and drug addict after his family's wealth collapsed; Hingori told his father-in-law "your son-in-law is a saint"; years later Rajeev became a respected spiritual healer. [[stories/rajeev-alcoholic-to-saint]]

The latent saint is a recurrent motif: the unawakened giant, the philanthropist in the making. Hingori did not know what he was until someone taught him how to sense his own reflection without looking at it directly.

## The Ms. Water Analogy

Why should Ms. Water love herself *only* in pure form? If water can love itself as part of a river, lake, or ocean, it should also love itself as the base ingredient of coffee or tea. Self-love must extend to the consciousness supreme *in product differentiation* â€” in whatever form the self is currently taking, even when adulterated. ## Adi Shankaracharya's Formula

> "I have no separation from my true self, no doubt about my existence, nor have I discrimination based on birth. I have no father or mother, nor did I have a birth. I am not the relative, nor the friend, nor the guru, nor the disciple, I am indeed, that eternal knowing and bliss, Shiva, love, and pure consciousness."
> â€” Adi Shankaracharya, quoted by Hingori, *Aatma Sutra*, Ch.XII, p.148

This is offered as the endpoint that self-love, once fully realised, leads to.
