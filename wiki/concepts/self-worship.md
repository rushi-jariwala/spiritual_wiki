# Self-Worship

> *"Remember those magic words in Sanskrit: Tat Twam Asi (That You Are)."*
> — Hingori, *Aatma Sutra*, Ch.XIV, p.175

## Definition

Self-worship (*aatmic bhakti*) is the third and final stage of the Part C arc in *Aatma Sutra*, following self-acceptance and self-love. It is the **reverence of the *jivaatma*** — the inner light — as a part of the *parmaatma* (consciousness supreme). It is not worship of the physical body, not narcissism, not glorification of social identity. It is the recognition that the same divinity present in any deity or saint is present in every being.

> "The 'self' in self-worship we are referring to, is not the self of the gate but the self of the light within. Therefore self-worship is justifiable even for the most wretched, the most evil or the most perceivably unqualified."
> — Hingori, *Aatma Sutra*, Ch.XIV, p.172

## The Two Gates Metaphor

Hingori uses the image of a light surrounded by two gates:

- **Outer gate** = the physical body (isthul sharir): may be imperfect in appearance, social status, achievements, self-perception
- **Inner gate** = the spirit body (sukshma sharir): may be ignorant, weak in guna mix, low in shakti
- **The light within** = the jivaatma, the divine spark

The gates are "ever changing and dynamic." They can be bright or dull, admired or scorned. But they will not diminish the quality of the light. Self-worship uses the outer doorway as a passage to the light — not as the object of worship itself.

> "Your physical self is the doorway that leads to the 'you' that can be worshipped. So why not use this doorway instead of some other? There is no vanity involved, so do not fear it. There are nine billion doorways (the number of people alive) to the supreme, yours is one of them. So, use it."
> — Hingori, *Aatma Sutra*, Ch.XIV, p.173

## The Philosophical Ground

### Jivaatma as Part of Parmaatma

> "There is merit in loving yourself for who you are... the *jivaatma* is a part of the *parmaatma* or the supreme, much like a glass of water is a smaller version of the ocean."
> — Hingori, *Aatma Sutra*, Ch.XIII, p.155

From Genesis 1:27: *"God created man in his own image."* From Adi Shankaracharya: *"I am not the mind, nor intellect, nor ego, nor the reflections of inner self (citt). I am not the five senses. I am beyond that."* Hingori presents these as converging on the same truth: the core of each being is divine.

### Why the Mind Cannot Perceive It

> "The biggest challenge is that you are trying to perceive a reality that lies beyond the mind and intellect. Spirituality is definitely not a subject for the five senses to fathom."
> — Hingori, *Aatma Sutra*, Ch.XIII, p.155

The mind and intellect — the only tools of normal perception — cannot themselves apprehend what lies beyond them. Hingori's response: have faith. Spiritual experiences, visions, and dreams serve as faith-builders that progressively open the seeker to self-worship. See [[concepts/self-worship#Dreams and Visions as Faith-Builders]].

### Sant Tukaram's Formula

> "I made an earthen image of Shiva, but the Earth is not Shiva; My worship reaches Shiva, the Earth remains the Earth it was..."
> — Sant Tukaram, quoted in *Aatma Sutra*, Ch.XIV, p.173

See [[stories/sant-tukaram-worship-poem]] for the full verse. The image — whether made of earth, stone, or pewter — is a **conduit pipe**. Similarly, the self-photograph of Exercise 1 is a conduit: the physical appearance in the photo is not what is worshipped; the divine at the other end is. Hingori says: "You have outlined a path between the physical appearance in your photograph and the consciousness supreme at the other end."

## Self-Worship and Non-Doership

These might appear contradictory: if "I am not the doer," who is it that worships, and who is worshipped?

Hingori addresses this directly:

> "No, they are not [contradictory]. The result of non-doership is a consistent reduction of the karmic weight through the exhaustion of *samskars* thereby, helping you to inch towards circumventing the cycle of life and death and towards *mukti*. The fact that you learn to love and worship yourself means you have transcended and would not wish to live endlessly, life after life and death after death."
> — Hingori, *Aatma Sutra*, Ch.XIV, p.177

Non-doership operates on the karmic level — reducing the weight of ownership and guilt. Self-worship operates on the level of identity — re-rooting the self in its divine essence rather than its physical and mental noise. They are complementary movements toward *mukti*, not contradictory ones.

> [!tip] The affirmation "I am not the doer" must be repeated several thousand times in a hypnotic way, seeping from conscious to unconscious. Self-worship deepens the same shift from a different angle: instead of removing ownership of acts, it installs a sense of divine identity that the acts cannot touch.

## Dreams and Visions as Faith-Builders

Spiritual experiences of any kind help fortify belief in spiritualism. Hingori presents four categories of dream/vision:

1. **Perception of competence** — dreaming of meeting saints or gods means the unconscious considers itself qualified to be in their company; it changes the mirror's reflection.
2. **Steam release** — insignificant, scattered dreams are the mind expelling stored *samskars*; they are not meant to mean anything.
3. **Out-of-body experiences** — the spirit body physically exits; the physical body is kept in suspended animation by the energy at the *sahasrara*; travel to other states of existence directly proves the multi-body configuration.
4. **Mantra/teaching transmission** — *mantras* received in dream state from deities or teachers; include 3D "operational manuals" for their use; some disciples have passed mantras to their own disciples in the same way.

Visions of the self being blessed by saints or gods shift self-perception permanently. See:
- [[stories/rajee-shiv-darshan]] — Rajee receives milky water from Shankar: "mrityu, mrityu, mrityu"
- [[stories/kapill-shiv-in-himself]] — Kapill sees Shankar in meditation and realises he is looking at himself
- [[stories/hingori-badrinath-brother]] — Gurudev asks Hingori to call Lord Badrinath "my dear elder brother"
- [[stories/hingori-vision-of-ram]] — Hingori offers good wishes to Ram, Sita, Lakshman instead of asking for a boon

## Animal Miracles as Faith-Builders

Animals can also serve as instruments of spiritual communication, raising consciousness beyond their normal level:

> "If the heightening of awareness can happen for a crow or a dog, then why not us?"
> — Hingori, *Aatma Sutra*, Ch.XIII, p.168

- [[stories/hingori-crow-morning-alarm]] — a crow knocks at a window daily at 5:45am to wake a mother for her premature baby
- [[stories/hingori-dog-ghosheini-temple]] — a lazy dog becomes animated, leads Hingori to a hilltop temple; the temple asks to collaborate; two years later the exact land is offered

## Multiple Levels of Mental Existence

> "I realised that I could not hold a certain level of introspective thinking or remain in a particular state of self-evaluation, all the time."
> — Hingori, *Aatma Sutra*, Ch.XIII, p.156

A key teaching for practitioners: you cannot always remain at your highest level. You oscillate from 5th grade to college to PhD and back. Knowing this is itself an achievement — it keeps you flexible enough to oscillate in and out of *maya*'s circle without losing yourself.

> "Knowing that your so-called normal life is one of *maya* (delusion) is a great awareness. Therefore, living with the realisation of *maya* keeps you flexible enough to oscillate in and out of its circle."
> — Hingori, *Aatma Sutra*, Ch.XIII, p.156

## The Three Exercises

Hingori prescribes three concrete practices:

### Exercise 1 — Photograph Worship
Take a photograph of yourself and treat it as you would an image of a saint or deity. Bow daily; offer flowers, a candle, or incense. Sit before it and meditate. Tell the character in the photograph you know it is a godly character and that you love it unconditionally — on an as-is-where-is basis, irrespective of the waxing and waning of the physical body's fortunes. The photograph connects the physical appearance, the spirit body, and the *kaarna sharir* — all observed by the *jivaatma* as witness.

### Exercise 2 — Mirror and the Half-Moon
Sit cross-legged in front of a mirror; stare at the forehead. Over weeks, you will see an imaginary straight line extending from above the nose to the centre of the forehead, and a half-moon or u-shape forming above the eyebrows toward the hairline. Continue staring at this for several minutes daily, for years. Recite a mantra, or simply chant Om / Allah / Hail Mary.

### Exercise 3 — Inner Visualization
Imagine yourself sitting within yourself in a cross-legged pose, located between the heart and forehead chakra. Look at your present form, then slowly look back at yourself in youth, then childhood, until you see yourself as a child sitting in the same pose within you. May take weeks or months. "Keep trying and keep failing, until you fail at failing."

## Gurudev as Exemplar

Gurudev enacted self-worship without naming it so:

- He stood outside every temple he visited and never entered; sent disciples in while he remained outside — mirroring Guru Vashisht's teaching to Ram: *"There is no god, and this world's appearance is generated by the collective mind."*
- During difficult healing missions, he carried a photograph of himself in his shirt pocket: *"I am carrying this photograph so that I can take myself along for this important mission. I want myself to come and help me complete it."* — aligning all components of his being to work in consonance.
- He accepted *guru bhakti* unconditionally on an as-is-where-is basis — Hingori's dream asked him: "Are you willing to accept him even if he is not the great saint you think he is?" His confirmed "yes" shifted the relationship from physical to metaphysical.

> "I realised that my guru was not the 5-feet-8-inch man, well-spoken, incredible exemplar. He was more than human — a ball of energy. I knew that I should stop worshipping the man and worship the being."
> — Hingori, *Aatma Sutra*, Ch.XIV, p.180

## Self-Healing by an Atheist

> "It is a case of an atheist worshipping himself and benefitting from his own divinity."
> — Hingori, *Aatma Sutra*, Ch.XIV, p.182

John, a boxing promoter in New York with blood cancer (3–6 months to live), refused god and temples. Hingori's prescription: light a candle and pray to yourself for 5 minutes twice daily. Twenty years later, he was still alive. See [[stories/john-atheist-self-healing]].

## From Tree to Orchard

Gurudev's life embodied self-worship-through-service at scale: Ministry of Agriculture job by day; farm work; 50–100 daily visitors; 30,000 on *Bada Guruvar*; over 100 *sthans* worldwide; *paath* (samadhi-travel) at night to work on distant cases. He trained 100+ disciples to carry his mission — growing from a tree to an orchard. After his death, three generations of disciples continue.

Guru Vashisht declined *moksh* to remain and help others climb the same path — the same principle at cosmic scale.

## Cosmic Currency Revisited

Self-worship and seva build *cosmic currency* — the aura/shakti that the spirit body carries into the afterlife. Charity done in the name of ancestors also adds to their cosmic currency. The self-worship exercises therefore serve a dual function: building self-identity *and* building the soul's post-death wealth. See [[concepts/aura]].
