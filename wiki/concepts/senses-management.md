# Senses Management

## Definition

Senses management is the practice of governing the five senses — sight, hearing, touch, taste, smell — not by suppression but by *observation*, so that the practitioner becomes an observer of the sensory play rather than the actor who indulges in it. This detachment allows the senses to be used as tools rather than rulers.

> Gurudev learned to manage his senses not by suppressing them but by observing his reactions as they played out. In this manner, he became an observer of the sensory play rather than the actor who indulged in them. By being an observer, the mahaguru immediately created a template for controlling and manipulating his senses as per his will.
> — *The Guru of Gurus*, p.172

This is distinct from asceticism or mortification. It is a shift in *position* — from actor to observer — that allows full engagement with the world while remaining unbound by it. See [[concepts/role-play]] for how this same posture shapes Gurudev's emotional functioning.

## Why the Senses Bind

The brain has no direct interface with the outside world. It interprets electrochemical signals from the sensory organs. As a result, *what we see is an internal model of our personal world, not what is out there.* Our perceived reality is a more personalised view of reality and not the cosmic reality. To align with the cosmic perspective, you must overcome the limitations of a personalised worldview — which begins with managing the senses.

> Multiple senses working together give rise to a series of emotions leading to a range of actions.
> — *The Guru of Gurus*, p.172

## Gurudev's Methods, Sense by Sense

### Sight

The mahaguru's formula for dealing with the opposite gender: imagine every attractive woman simultaneously in her *gudiya* (child) and *budiya* (aged) forms to accurately evaluate attraction. Hingori went further, visualising every woman he met as a skeleton — insurance against untoward desire that also revealed *"beauty was literally in the mind of the beholder."*

Gurudev stated that when evolved spiritualists die, their spirits arrive at a crossway — one path leads to lovely-looking lower lokas, the other to plainer-looking higher lokas. The pursuit of beauty can land a spirit in a lower realm.

### Smell

Taking a leaf from Aghor vidya: *develop an indifference to stench if you want to cultivate an indifference to incense.* Hingori practised tolerance and nonchalance to unpleasant smells; over time he also gave up colognes and perfumes. The sense of smell as a teacher in both directions.

### Taste

Gurudev's own food journey: foodie in youth → sattvic by the time of his ministry. Lassi (buttermilk) for lunch on most days; nimbu pani (lemonade) for breakfast. On Mahashivratri, he did not eat until the last person in the queue had been met — which could take two days.

> "He who learns to feed others before feeding himself transcends the desire for food."
> — Gurudev to his daughter, *The Guru of Gurus*, p.171

He also recommended fasting once a week as a detox that reduces bodily secretions and frees the mind from activity and stress.

An anecdote illustrates sensory management through taste: Gurudev once visited an associate's home with disciples. The associate and wife were absent; their eight-year-old daughter offered to make tea and brewed it on a kerosene stove. Upon leaving, the mahaguru gave his disciples a sharp mouthful for barely sipping the kerosene-smelling tea: *"Is it possible they lost a lot because they could not bear a little?"*

### Hearing

The sense of hearing connects us to others in ways no other sense can. Gurudev discouraged idle talk and gossip. His discipline: develop discernment — intuitively hear the unsaid, and be selective in responding to what is heard. *"Do not believe anything we hadn't seen or heard for ourselves."*

A celebrated anecdote on discernment: when a disciple denied a mistake in front of Gurudev, the mahaguru had Bittu ji fetch a sealed set of blank cassettes from a bedside cabinet and play the third one. Those seated heard the verbatim first-person conversation of the disciple bragging about the same 'mistake' he had vehemently denied. See [[stories/blank-cassette-disciple]].

Gurudev liked Mohammed Rafi and radio in the car; over time his interest in music waned to the point where he could appreciate it without being drawn into it or drowning in its melody.

### Touch / Anger

High temper in powerful spiritualists is attributable to the uprisen fire element owing to intense tapasya. Gurudev's method in early years at IARI: he sat in the corridor outside his office abusing passersby. When they retaliated, he smiled back — deliberately inciting abuse to test his resilience. He erased a strong emotion by inducing it until it lost its grip. *Lesser the emotion, more the stability of the mind.*

### Managing Sight — The Young Devotee's Tea

Gurudev once blessed the tea offered by an eight-year-old hostess and handed the cups to his disciples. Some barely sipped and left the rest because it smelled of kerosene. The mahaguru's rebuke was direct: could they not endure a small discomfort for the sake of what had been offered and blessed?

## Neuroplasticity

When the brain is denied input in one sensory modality, it can rewire itself to support and augment other senses. The areas of the brain dedicated to the lost sense are remapped to process other senses, sometimes including the birth of new neurons.

Gurudev instructed Hingori to open a school for the deaf in Dehradun, which Hingori eventually did. Working with the deaf revealed their exceptional manual dexterity and high concentration — seldom distracted by sound, their sense of touch is dramatically enhanced.

> The mahaguru suggested we let one vice remain till we manage to control or offset the others. Ultimately, we must give up even the remaining vice.
> — *The Guru of Gurus*, p.173

Hidden in this simple suggestion were *the secrets of neuroplasticity* — progressive sensory control, with the last vice serving as an anchor while the others are progressively relinquished. Hingori identifies his remaining challenge as the sense of taste.

## The Mind

The brain is a multisensory organ blending information from various senses. The mind — distinct from the brain — is the spirit and causal-body counterpart. It provides extrasensory information to the brain, granting access to psychic abilities: intuition, telepathy, remote sensing, clairvoyance, clairaudience, psychokinesis.

The physical body is not solid matter but vibrating strings of energy — seven octillion atoms made of energy vortices, each with a distinct energy impression. As a result, the universe is nothing more than vibrating strings of energy, consistent with quantum physics' findings.

To change the state of your consciousness, you must change the level of your mind. The higher the level of the mind, the lesser its movement and the greater your awareness. When the mind is stiller, it reflects the param-aatma to a greater degree.

## In a Nutshell

> The form exists in the seeing. The sound exists in the hearing. The feel exists in the touching. The taste exists in the tasting. The smell exists in the smelling. If a person can diminish the experience of seeing, hearing, touching, tasting, and smelling, then there isn't much to experience. At this stage, the experiencer begins to experience himself. In that experience of himself, he grasps his limitlessness and realises, as is he, so is the experience.
> — *The Guru of Gurus*, p.175

Sensory management is ultimately the method by which the jivaatma strips away the personalised worldview and begins to perceive its own nature.
