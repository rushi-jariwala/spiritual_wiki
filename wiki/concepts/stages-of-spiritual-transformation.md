# Stages of Spiritual Transformation

## Definition

Gurudev's teaching on the stages of spiritual transformation is his most systematic framework for the jivaatma's journey from ordinary embodied life toward moksha. While some schools of Indian philosophy describe five stages, the mahaguru's teaching translates to **six stages**.

> The transformation from aadmi to aatma is facilitated by an accomplished spiritual teacher, a siddh guru. The teacher has two primary responsibilities. First, he brings his practical experience and wisdom to bear upon his mentorship. Second, he becomes the external representation of the jivaatma, which every student must acknowledge within themselves to realise the param-aatma subsequently.
> — *The Guru of Gurus*, p.145

## The Six Stages

| # | Stage | Description |
|---|---|---|
| 1 | **Shraddha** | Belief in a spiritual existence, something greater than oneself |
| 2 | **Vishwas** | Faith in the existence of a jivaatma |
| 3 | **Seva** | A jivaatma's efforts towards the betterment of other jivaatmas |
| 4 | **Gyan** | The awareness that one's jivaatma is a part of the param-aatma |
| 5 | **Bhakti** | The ability to worship the supreme in oneself and others; the strong desire to merge with the param-aatma and lose one's identity |
| 6 | **Divya Gyan** | Enlightenment; realisation of the param-aatma |

> [!tip] In Gurudev's school of metaphysics, gyan is both the passport for skyrocketing and the runway for final landing. Bhakti (self-worship) bridges the gap between gyan (awareness) and divya gyan (enlightenment).

## Each Stage in Detail

### Shraddha

Shraddha is the seed. Even before a person meets a guru, shraddha draws them to him — people travelled long distances and forfeited daily wages, enduring queues and physical discomfort, to meet Gurudev. He recognised their shraddha and gave immediate relief in their first meeting, *building the case for faith*.

> "Many of those who come to meet me in Delhi are more focused on examining my powers."
> — Gurudev to Sushila ji, *The Guru of Gurus*, p.146

Those in Delhi examining his powers received less benefit than those at camps who came in pure shraddha. Reward of shraddha activates vishwas.

### Vishwas

Faith is described as *"the most significant investment a person can make since it delivers the highest returns."* Faith in a guru binds him in a web of spiritual responsibility and creates a field of influence. As the disciple's receptivity grows, the guru's efforts halve but the results multiply.

Hingori's own journey illustrates the cost of delayed vishwas:

> "A doctor can prescribe the medication, but until the patient has faith in the doctor, the medicines will not be as effective."
> — Gurudev, *The Guru of Gurus*, p.146

Hingori first met Gurudev in 1977, received partial relief from rheumatoid arthritis, then dismissed the Thursday practices Gurudev prescribed. Arthritis returned ferociously five years later. When he finally returned and submitted to seva at the Gurgaon sthan, the 1982 visit became his actual spiritual entry — five years squandered because his intellect had blocked the faith his spirit had already formed in 1977. See [[stories/hingori-faith-development]].

> "You will get a lot more than you can imagine."
> — Gurudev to Hingori, *The Guru of Gurus*, p.148

Lucky are those who develop faith instantly — they fast-track their transformation, bypassing shraddha.

### Seva

[[practices/seva|Seva]] is the practical hinge — the stage that converts belief into transformation. The mahaguru spent endless hours meeting people, healing them, teaching. He empowered disciples to assist him, and trained over a hundred spiritual healers across the world.

Pradeep ji's daughter Pragya fell from stairs and lapsed into semi-consciousness; doctors said the situation was critical. Before seeking Gurudev's help, Pradeep ji first served a young cancer patient whose father had dreamed of him curing his son. Only then did he take Pragya to Gurudev — whose touch triggered her recovery.

> When you serve another jivaatma, you serve the jivaatma within yourself. Therefore, what you choose to transform, transforms you.
> — *The Guru of Gurus*, p.149

Even a dog (Kalu, at the Gurgaon sthan) could heal wounds by licking them. *Seva is a type of practical spirituality practised by all forms of life.*

### Gyan

Gyan emerges from various external and internal sources — things seen, heard, read, felt; dreams; intuition; conversations. Reflections flash at random. Intuition grows and what you say frequently comes true. Supernature begins to show its hues, though dimly.

> [!warning] Arrogance, pride, doership, and vanity can become massive stumbling blocks in the transition from gyan to enlightenment. Most people are highly impressed with themselves at the gyan stage, not knowing that what they see or sense is only a trailer of divya gyan.

Gurudev to Bittu ji: *"All my disciples have gyan within them, even if they may not be consciously aware of it. As and when the need arises, gyan will emerge on its own."* Hingori validated this when, after opening a Mumbai sthan, a djinn attacked him at night — and he instinctively knew how to repel it with the Mahagayatri mantra.

### Bhakti

Gurudev defined bhakti not as emotional devotion to an external god, but as **unemotional self-worship**. For him, there was only one relationship — the 'I' within with the 'I' without. Until the two 'I's fuse into one, transformation is incomplete.

> "To conquer happiness, you must conquer sorrow."
> — Gurudev to Hingori, *The Guru of Gurus*, p.151

This set off Hingori's four-year bhakti tryst. He went from being a flamboyant, popular, gift-of-the-gab personality to a social recluse — reciting mantras for six hours daily, powers of the Shiv-parivaar appearing as symbols on his hands. At the end, Gurudev gave him the responsibility of opening and running the Mumbai sthan. See [[stories/hingori-bhakti-journey]].

### Divya Gyan

Intense bhakti can open the **Ajna chakra** (third eye). At the stage of divya gyan, a person's consciousness oscillates at will. When Gurudev's consciousness shifted to his Shiv roop, the colour of his eyes lightened, his expressions changed, his voice became heavy as if from deep within. Disciples felt an odd sense of awe and apprehension.

Hingori had his Ajna opened by Gurudev; Gurudev then lowered his kundalini to prevent him from getting stuck at that level before reaching Sahasrara. Years later, while meditating, Hingori experienced transcendence to the seventh chakra — "something like a thin paper tore at the back of my head… I could suddenly see what was behind me" — what he calls the opening of the **fourth eye**, for a couple of minutes, most likely Gurudev's way of giving him a preview of higher consciousness.

> "Whenever you see an image of me, remind yourself that you have discovered god."
> — Gurudev to Suresh Kohli ji, *The Guru of Gurus*, p.153

A person in divya gyan can be in constant awareness, awaken clair senses, create energy bodies, and bilocate. The mahaguru could do all of this at will.

The dissolution of the jivaatma's identity into the absolution of the param-aatma is moksha — *"technically achievable, but a nearly impossible feat."* Several lifetimes may be required, not just a few years.

## Non-Linear Progression

> [!tip] The six stages are neither linear nor parallel. They co-mingle and piggyback on each other. Shraddha and vishwas are the foundation and pivot. Seva, gyan, and bhakti co-mingle. Bhakti internalises the energies of a power; seva enhances and intensifies; gyan becomes reality when this energy growth raises the kundalini to the Ajna chakra. Divya gyan emerges when the remaining stages have matured into saturation.
