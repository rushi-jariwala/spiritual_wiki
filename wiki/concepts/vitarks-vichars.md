# Vitarks and Vichars

Two specific categories of *vritti* (disturbance in the *citt*) described by Hingori in *Aatma Sutra* Ch.III, drawing on Maharishi Patanjali's *Yogasutras*.

## Vitarks

> "*Vitarks*, with their negative notions, mental chatter and reverse inclinations to our positive intent, act like a hindrance to our attempt to evolve. They act as an impediment for people who want to meditate or maintain a calm state of mind."
> — Hingori, *Aatma Sutra*, Ch.III, p.31

*Vitarks* are considered *anti-yams*. According to Patanjali, the five *yams* are the ethical rules of living: non-violence, truthfulness, non-theft, celibacy, and non-possessiveness. *Vitarks* are thoughts that run directly opposite to these.

## Vichars

> "Relative to *vitarks*, *vichars* are marginally easier to deal with. These are rational thoughts and reactions. And because they are subtle, they are slightly less disturbing."
> — Hingori, *Aatma Sutra*, Ch.III, p.31

*Vitarks* are a slightly more negative version of *vichars*, though in effect they are almost the same.

## Origin: The Karmashaya

Both *vitarks* and *vichars* arise involuntarily from the [[concepts/kaarna-sharir|karmashaya]] — they are not products of free choice. They happen "according to the trends of your life's journey." Since they are not voluntarily created, ownership over them is not warranted:

> "Since some thoughts from our *karmashaya* are involuntary, it resolves the apparent contradiction of *vitarks* and *vichars*. Though these are not thoughts from the atmosphere but from the storehouse of your *samskars*, they happen involuntarily according to the trends of your life's journey. Since they are not your own voluntary creation, you do not need to take ownership of these either."
> — Hingori, *Aatma Sutra*, Ch.III, p.32

## The Golden Rule

> "**Golden Rule:** The positive *samskars* stored will be converted in the future into *bhog* (pleasure and riches). The negative *samskars* into *rog* (debt, disease, and misfortune). The *samskars* related to spirituality and divinity will get converted into *yog* (spiritual evolution). Aim for *yog*!"
> — Hingori, *Aatma Sutra*, Ch.III, p.32

## Patanjali's Distinction: Painful vs Not Painful

Maharishi Patanjali classifies thought waves as either *painful* or *not painful* — a distinction that does not map straightforwardly onto what feels pleasant or unpleasant:

> "A 'painful' wave, according to Patanjali, is not necessarily painful when it first arises in the mind. It is only when one takes ownership of it does the wave acquire the qualities of an increased degree of ignorance, addiction, and bondage. Similarly, a wave which seems painful at first may actually 'not be painful', provided it impels the mind towards greater freedom and knowledge."
> — Hingori, *Aatma Sutra*, Ch.III, p.35

Examples:
- A **lustful thought** is *painful* — lust, even when pleasantly satisfied, causes addiction, jealousy, and bondage.
- A **thought of empathy** is *not painful* — it is unselfish and loosens the bonds of ego.

The discipline proceeds in two stages:
1. First, counterbalance the *painful* waves (anger, desire, delusion) with their opposites (love, generosity, truth).
2. Only much later, when the painful waves are calmed, proceed to quiet the *not painful* waves deliberately created.

## How to Handle Them

> "The way forward for a person who wants to meditate or calm the mind, is to accept the *vichars* and *vitarks* and let them pass. They are stored and need to be exhausted by steadily phasing them out. A positive effect of acceptance is the reduction of the load of the *samskars* and cleansing of the *citt*. Therefore, it is better to observe patience and let these *vichars* and *vitarks* die a natural death."
> — Hingori, *Aatma Sutra*, Ch.III, p.32

Two takeaways:
1. Acceptance of *vichars* and *vitarks* is the long-term solution.
2. Understanding this concept helps you be patient and let go.

When both are fairly exhausted: a quieter mind, a stiller *citt*, and a more effective meditative experience.

> [!tip] Guilt compounds the problem
> Embarrassing or self-defeating thoughts should not be allowed to become downers of self-esteem. Accepting negative thoughts without harbouring guilt is the way forward. Guilt converts the thought into a new samskar — precisely what Gurudev averted in [[stories/giri-embarrassing-dream]].

## A Note on Enjoyment

> "It is the nature of our mind to lead us to value pleasure and enjoyment. But, do remember, enjoyment is an expenditure of time and not an investment. 'We spent a lovely evening today', usually means we spent our time pleasurably but not productively. The word 'spent' is self-explanatory."
> — Hingori, *Aatma Sutra*, Ch.III, p.37
