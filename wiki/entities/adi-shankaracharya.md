# Adi Shankaracharya

## Who / What

Adi Shankaracharya (c. 788–820 CE) was the founding philosopher of *Advaita Vedanta* — the non-dualist school of Hindu philosophy that holds the individual self (*jivaatma*) and ultimate consciousness (*Brahman/Aatma*) to be identical in nature. He is cited in the Hingori corpus as the clearest classical formulation of [[concepts/non-doership|non-doership]] and the dissolution of the ego-self into pure consciousness.

## Key Teachings (as cited by Hingori)

The *Nirvana Shatakam* (Six Verses on Liberation) is the primary reference. Hingori quotes it as the poetic completion of the *karm-mukt* ideal:

> "I have neither virtue (*punyam*), nor vice (*papam*). I do not commit sins or good deeds, nor have happiness or sorrow, pain or pleasure, I do not need *mantras*, holy places, scriptures (*Vedas*), rituals or sacrifices (*yagnas*). I am none of the triad of the observer or one who experiences the process of observing or experiencing, or any object being observed or experienced. I am indeed, that eternal knowing and bliss, Shiva, love and pure consciousness."
> — Adi Shankaracharya, *Nirvana Shatakam*, quoted in *Aatma Sutra*, Ch.IX, p.104

The verse is presented by Hingori as evidence that the *karm-mukt* state he describes — transcending both virtue and vice, neither owning deeds nor their outcomes — has canonical precedent in the Advaita tradition.

## Quotes

> "I am indeed, that eternal knowing and bliss, Shiva, love and pure consciousness."
> — Adi Shankaracharya, *Nirvana Shatakam*, quoted in *Aatma Sutra*, Ch.IX, p.104

## The Guru-Seeking (*Guru Sutra* Ch.2)

*Guru Sutra* Ch.2 adds a dimension: despite being "one of the greatest spiritual whiz kids in history and a master of philosophy" — born enlightened — Shankaracharya was "aware he needed a guru to evolve." He knew who his guru would be, searched for him, and "wooed him for diksha." This is Hingori's strongest illustration of the thesis that even naturally exalted beings cannot bypass the guru-disciple relationship.

> "Adi Shankaracharya, despite being one of the greatest spiritual whiz kids in history and a master of philosophy, was aware he needed a guru to evolve. Since he was born enlightened, he knew who his guru would be, but had to search for him and woo him for diksha."
> — Hingori, *Guru Sutra*, Ch.2, p.13
