# Emperor Ashoka

> *The Mauryan emperor who, after the Kalinga war, renounced violence and devoted his life to the spiritual upliftment of his people. Cited by Hingori as a paradigmatic example of karmayoga.*

## Who

Ashoka is referenced in *Karma Sutra* Ch.1 not as a figure of the Hingori lineage but as a **historical example of the principles** the book teaches — specifically, that one may convert a past of violent action into a future of seva and spiritual service.

## Role in the Hingori Sutras

Hingori uses Ashoka's post-Kalinga transformation as a brief parable within the larger teaching on [[concepts/karma|karma]] and [[practices/seva|seva]]. Ashoka appears in a subsection titled **"A King's Tale"** (p.15).

> After the Kalinga war, emperor Ashoka renounced war or ahimsa and embraced the philosophy of dharma. He adopted the teachings of Gautam Buddha, and devoted his entire life in the service and spiritual upliftment of his people.
> — *Karma Sutra*, Ch.1, p.15

See [[stories/ashoka-kalinga-transformation]].

## The Quote That Stuck with Hingori

Hingori describes encountering the following translated line in his school history books and never forgetting it:

> Work I must for the common weal and the way to achieve is by effort and dispatch; to this end I toil to discharge my debt to human beings and to make some happy; in this world, and the next.
> — Ashoka, quoted in *Karma Sutra*, Ch.1, p.15

Hingori singles out this passage as *"one of the most brilliant quotes on the subject of karmayoga, especially the last line, 'in this world and the next' shows his desire to help people prepare for their future lives as well."*

## Why Ashoka Matters Here

Two things, in Hingori's framing:

1. **Spreading Buddhism as karmayoga.** Ashoka's propagation of Buddhism across South East Asia and his creation of prayer houses are framed as a step toward preparing people for their *next* lives — a central concern of the karma framework.
2. **The possibility of reversal.** Ashoka's life stands as evidence that even a past of violent and negative karma can be redirected. The Kalinga war, with its enormous loss of life, did not prevent Ashoka from becoming a byword for righteous rule. This matters for anyone who fears their sanchit is too weighted with past negatives to leave room for progress.

## Open Questions

> [!question] Historical vs hagiographic framing
> Hingori's Ashoka is the Ashoka of Indian devotional history — the benevolent Buddhist king. The historical Ashoka's record is more complex, and even his Buddhism is debated by modern historians. This wiki is not the place to litigate that, but it is worth noting that Hingori's Ashoka is used as a moral exemplar, not a historiographic claim.
