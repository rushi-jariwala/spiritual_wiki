# Eklavya

## Who

Eklavya is an archer from the Indian epic *The Mahabharat*. He sought training from Dronacharya — the royal weapons master — but was refused because Drona's teaching was reserved for the Kuru princes. Eklavya then built a clay statue of Dronacharya and trained alone before it, worshipping it as his guru.

Through this practice he became, by some accounts, a more accomplished archer than even Arjuna, Drona's prized pupil.

## Key Teaching

Hingori cites Eklavya as the pre-eminent example of a disciple who was **continuously inspired by a guru without being physically present**:

> "In principle, a guru is not a person, but a concept that embodies infinite wisdom and universal knowledge. Thus, in a guru-disciple relationship, the disciple emulates not the person he refers to as his guru but his gunas (qualities). There are several examples in history where a guru has inspired disciples without being physically present. One such example is of Eklavya, a proficient archer from the famed Indian epic, The Mahabharat, who acquired his skills by worshiping the statue of his guru, Dronacharya."
> — Hingori, *Guru Sutra*, Ch.2, p.11

Eklavya's story also illustrates Hingori's own relationship with the memory of Gurudev: inspiration by a guru who is no longer physically accessible.
