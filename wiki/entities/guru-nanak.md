# Guru Nanak

> *Founder of Sikhism. Cited by Hingori as an example of a great teacher who had no guru in human form — he received his realisation through direct communion.*

## Who

Guru Nanak is referenced in *Karma Sutra* Ch.1 as one of the renowned gurus in the world's teaching traditions. Hingori lists him alongside Guru Vashisht, Jesus Christ, Gautam Buddha, Ramakrishna Paramhansa, and others across India and the world (Aristotle, Plato, Lao Tzu, Prophet Mohammed, Khalil Gibran).

## Role in the Hingori Sutras

Guru Nanak occupies a specific functional role in Chapter 1's teaching on [[concepts/guru-disciple|the guru-disciple relationship]]: he is the **case study of enlightenment without a human guru**. Hingori uses him to establish that the do-it-yourself path is real — though as the chapter also notes, it is implicitly harder and "sometimes it works."

## The River Bain Story

> Early one morning, Guru Nanak, accompanied by Mardana, went to River Bain for his bath. After taking a dip into the river, Guru Nanak did not resurface and everybody feared that he must have drowned. The villagers searched everywhere, but there was no trace of him. Actually, Guru Nanak was in holy communion with god.
> — *Karma Sutra*, Ch.1, p.20

## The Opening of Japji

Upon re-emerging, Guru Nanak uttered what became the Japji — the opening of the Guru Granth Sahib:

> There is but one god. His name is truth. He is the creator, He fears none and he is without hate. He never dies. He is beyond the cycle of birth and death. He is self-illuminated, He is realised by the kindness of the True Guru. He was True in the beginning. He was True when the ages commenced and has ever been True. He is also True now.
> — Guru Nanak, *Japji*, quoted in *Karma Sutra*, Ch.1, p.20

Hingori notes that Guru Nanak did not believe in the trinity of gods or that god can be born in human form.

## The Devi Connection

A distinctive note from Hingori that is not widely emphasised in Sikh tradition:

> Guru Nanak did not have a guru in human form. Gurudev said, he worshipped a devi and that is how he got the attainment of OM on his palm and other spiritual powers.
> — *Karma Sutra*, Ch.1, p.21

This is **Gurudev's teaching**, transmitted through Hingori. It positions Guru Nanak not as guru-less in the absolute sense but as having a **non-human guru** — a devi — and it attributes his spiritual powers (including the OM on his palm) to that relationship. This parallels the description of Gurudev's own palm-symbols.

## "Nanak Dukhiya Sab Sansaar" (Ch.XI)

Hingori cites Guru Nanak's adage in *Aatma Sutra* Ch.XI when discussing destiny as a source of self-doubt:

> "*Nanak dukhiya sab sansaar*" — the world is always in a state of unhappiness.
> — Guru Nanak Dev, quoted in *Aatma Sutra*, Ch.XI, p.132

Hingori uses it to point out that no age, stage, or circumstance is exempt from discontent — making the search for self-acceptance through external conditions a circular, never-ending game.
