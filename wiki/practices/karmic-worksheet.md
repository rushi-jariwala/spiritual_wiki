# The Karmic Worksheet

## What It Is

A daily self-audit tool for tracking the karmic mix of one's actions. The worksheet is split into two halves — **Positive Karmic Assessment** and **Negative Karmic Assessment** — each built from the categories established in Chs.3–4 of *Karma Sutra*. The purpose is not moral score-keeping but the cultivation of self-witnessing: becoming a *conscious witness of yourself*.

> "Add or subtract these points to compete with yourself on a daily basis. Slowly but surely you will learn to be a conscious witness of yourself."
> — *Karma Sutra*, p.94

## Scoring System

| Response | Points |
|---|---|
| Yes / One | 10 |
| No / None | −5 |
| Some | (Number of people) × 10 |

The scale is designed for self-competition — not external comparison. The number grows or shrinks relative to yesterday's score.

## How to Do It

Hingori recommends tearing out or copying the sheets and pinning them on a board for daily use. The exercise takes roughly 10 minutes. Each question is answered Yes/No/Some (or One/None/Some for quantity-based items).

### Positive Karmic Assessment

1. Number of people I have made happy today and who are grateful for that.
2. Done something to repay debt to the green world?
3. Have I been good to other life forms?
4. Have I fed anyone — friend / stranger / beggar?
5. Have I done any favours or helped anyone with education / medical aid / errands?
6. Did I succeed in being righteous / empathetic / humble?
7. Have I accepted criticism or negative and unfair behaviour towards me?

### Negative Karmic Assessment

1. Was I rude / unfair / unkind to either human or other life forms?
2. Have I accepted any obligations like food / education / favours / gifts?
3. Am I obliged to the green kingdom by eating vegetables / cereals / plucking flowers / leaves / cutting trees?
4. Am I obliged to other life forms by eating non-vegetarian food / drinking milk / milk products. Have I hurt any other life forms?
5. Have I had a good time and encashed some of my good deeds of the past, reducing my positive balances?
6. Am I obliged to anyone for being nice to me / helping me / doing errands / favours to me?
7. Have I hurt anyone physically / mentally / emotionally? Criticised people?
8. Have I done any criminal act / cheated / lied to someone for my personal gain? Been deceptive / treacherous?

## Why / Purpose

The worksheet is the practical interface layer of the entire *Karma Sutra* framework. It converts the abstract accounting metaphor into a daily habit. The act of writing down deeds — positive and negative — triggers a shift in perspective from participant to observer, nudging the practitioner toward [[concepts/non-doership|non-doership]] by creating a gap between action and self-identification.

> [!tip] The CCTV principle
> Hingori elsewhere describes this as putting up an imaginary CCTV camera outside yourself. Becoming aware of a klesha (or a karma) in the moment reduces its hold: "I would laugh at myself and the strength of the klesha would fade." (*Karma Sutra*, p.114)

> [!tip] Compound witnessing
> Hingori tracked one disciple (Mr X) for 45 days. He observed that the practice changed the man's *perception, analysis and behaviour* — not just his deeds. The worksheet thus functions as a feedback loop that reconditions the habit layer.

Items 3 and 4 on the Negative side acknowledge obligations that are structurally unavoidable — eating plants, drinking milk — reinforcing the book's broader point that existence itself accrues debt (see [[concepts/karmic-debt]]). The worksheet does not pretend otherwise; it simply makes the debt visible.
