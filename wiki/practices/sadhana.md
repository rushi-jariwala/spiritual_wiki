# Sadhana

> *Spiritual practice. The umbrella term under which seva, tapasya, mantra, meditation, and other disciplines all sit.*

## What It Is

In Chapter 1 of *Karma Sutra*, sadhana appears as the general term for sustained spiritual effort. It is mentioned alongside tapasya as what a disciple brings to the guru-disciple relationship, and as what â€” together with the guru's grace â€” can produce supernormal powers.

## How It Appears in Chapter 1

Three moments are relevant:

1. **Sadhana as the disciple's effort, alongside tapasya.** Gurudev's bada guruvar queue was described as *"a test of their tapasya or sadhana"* â€” the two terms used interchangeably for the effort that earns the guru's response.

2. **Sadhana paired with grace produces supernormal powers.**

   > The power of the mind, it is said, can move mountains. This means, a person can, through sadhana and his/her guru's grace, achieve powers that are supernormal.
   > â€” *Karma Sutra*, Ch.1, p.22

   Neither alone is sufficient. Sadhana without grace, in this framing, does not cross the threshold into the supernormal. Grace without sadhana has no vessel to flow into.

3. **Gurudev taught sadhana explicitly to his disciples.**

   > He taught powerful mantras and other practices to his disciples, and helped them evolve mentally, emotionally and spiritually. When he found them evolved enough, he would invoke the power to heal others in some of them.
   > â€” *Karma Sutra*, Ch.1, p.16
