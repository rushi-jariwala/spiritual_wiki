# Significant Days

Gurudev observed certain days of the year in specific ways. *"Even though mantra-based meditation is essential on all days, its efficacy is significantly enhanced on certain days."* — *The Guru of Gurus*, p.195

## Bada Guruvar

**When:** The first Thursday of each month following a night of no moon (amavasya).

Seva is performed at sthans across the country on this day. Visitors are served tea and, if possible, khichdi. Nothing is accepted from visitors in return — *no exchange for seva rendered* is the absolute rule. However, visitors to the main sthan in Gurgaon may offer **sugar-coated barley grains** (*phuliyan*) and sugar pops (*pattisa*); these are energised at the sthan and returned to them.

During Gurudev's time, around **50,000 people** came to seek his help on a single Bada Guruvar. He met all of them for a few seconds or more. Many did not have time to detail what they needed — yet somehow they would receive the help or healing sought.

Gurudev held Bada Guruvar at 5:30 am, with Malhotra ji as strategic head and four key sevadaars (Nikku ji, Pappu ji, Bittu ji, Gaggu ji) managing operations: queues, the day's menu, prasad, meals for sevadaars, and rotating duties for shoe-stand, jal, and gaddi seva.

> [!tip] All sthans connected to this sthan are *"notes of the same spiritual symphony."* Visitors were requested to offer prayers at the sthan even if Gurudev was not physically present — any spoken word or unspoken thought at the sthan reached him.

## Monday Fasts

**Frequency:** Weekly.

Gurudev fasted on Mondays, living only on liquids until nightfall. In his early years as a mahaguru, he broke his fast with water, yoghurt, and jaggery — first dividing a portion among a cow, a crow, a dog, and a houseguest — followed by potato subzi and rotis. He eventually abandoned the cow/crow/dog/guest distribution. Today, the Monday fast is broken at sunset with a meal of potato subzi and rice or rotis. Devotees and disciples may eat fruits throughout the day if they choose.

**Why Mondays:**

> "Mondays are dedicated to your isht, so you should do as much paath as you can on this day."
> — Gurudev (via Ravi ji), *The Guru of Gurus*, p.187

The *isht* referenced is Shiv in the unmanifested state. Mondays are governed by the moon's rays, which affect both water and the mind. Since the body is nearly 70% water, lunar rays influence the body-mind complex. Fasting on Mondays is therefore beneficial to physical and mental health.

**General rationale for fasting:** By sacrificing a meal owed by destiny, you willingly give it to another being. Fasting rests the digestive system, releases toxins, repairs body cells, accelerates metabolism, and improves brain health. Salt and cereals withheld means fewer bodily secretions and a mind freer from activity. *Fasting increases the potency of magnetic qualities in humans* — allowing the body to absorb more energy from the earth or atmosphere.

## Ganesh Chauth

**When:** Usually in January; devotees and disciples fast without food or water from sunrise to moonrise.

Jaggery-sesame ladoos and jaggery tea are served to break the fast at the sthan or made at home.

**Spiritual significance:** The period from Diwali to Mahashivratri is the **Shakti period** — a person's masculine energy principle becomes passive while the feminine becomes dominant. On Ganesh Chauth, **a third of the masculine principle reawakens**, allowing a man to reclaim part of the energetic spark that augments his being.

## Mahashivratri

**When:** Forty-one days after Ganesh Chauth, usually in February or March.

Those fasting eat before sunrise and consume no food or water until midnight. The fast is broken with salted fried potatoes and lemon-pepper black tea, at the sthan or home. This fast is more rigorous than Ganesh Chauth — it lasts a few hours longer.

On Mahashivratri, the **masculine principle of a man's energy is fully awakened** and reasserts its dominance.

During Gurudev's time, queues stretched for several kilometres; it took three to four days to ensure all devotees had met him and received his blessings.

Mahashivratri is celebrated at all sthans; many devotees from across the world congregate at the Gurgaon sthan. Sevadaars manage food and lodging; langar is served from two days before to two days after.

**Mahashivratri jal** is distributed the day after the festival. See [[concepts/mantra-transfer]] for the full recipe and potency details.

## Guru Purnima

**When:** Usually in July. A day ritually dedicated to the guru.

For Gurudev, this was the celebration of a custom. Devotees offered him a **coconut wrapped in a yellow handkerchief** — the coconut is associated with the human head; offering it implies surrendering to the guru's grace. He blessed and returned it. He allowed disciples to present him with **nine clothes or accessories** (*nau vastr*); alternatively, writing *"nau vastr"* on a yellow handkerchief and offering that was accepted as completing the custom.

He allowed a few disciples to **wash his feet and drink the water** — which contained his energy and was extremely potent; many found it raised their consciousness.

Thousands of devotees came to have their coconuts blessed; some fasted on fruits until they could pay respects. Sweetened saffron rice and mangoes were served at the sthan.

## Dussehra, Dhanteras, and Diwali

**The dark twenty days (Dussehra to Diwali):** The darkest twenty days of the year according to the lunar cycle. As a guru in the making, Gurudev would do a lot of paath during this time, sleep on the ground, wear the same clothes without changing, and eat light or not at all. Later he stopped this practice himself but suggested it to a few disciples.

**Dhanteras** (one or two days before Diwali): A day to express gratitude to Laxmi Devi, the deity of wealth, and request *barkat* (the luck to make your income last). Devotees purchased a silver coin of Laxmi Devi and had it blessed. A saffron or vermillion tilak is applied to the coin for forty-one consecutive nights from Diwali; a specific mantra is chanted to awaken the latent power of barkat.

**Diwali** (darkest night of the year): Gurudev suggested lighting **diyas (oil lamps)** at seven specific locations:

1. Water body
2. Tulsi plant
3. Peepal tree
4. Road crossing
5. Temple
6. Cemetery
7. Home

*Diyas are lit to appease specific energies at each location.* Gurudev also suggested staying awake on Diwali night doing paath.

## Other Important Days

**Eclipse:** Abstinence from water and food; focus on paath instead.

**Shraddh days:** Feed people and animals on behalf of ancestors. Other charitable acts should be performed in their name.

**Birthdays:** Gurudev did not observe birthdays — he believed the jivaatma is neither born nor dies. Since his passing, devotees have begun to observe **Basant Panchami** as his birthday.

> [!warning] Gurudev did not buy into the concept of birthdays because he believed the jivaatma is neither born nor dies. The observance of Basant Panchami as his birthday is a post-death tradition, not one he initiated or endorsed. He did observe Basant Panchami as a day dedicated to Saraswati Devi, the deity of wisdom — the tradition of distributing sweetened saffron rice on this day dates to his time.
