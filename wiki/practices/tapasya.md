# Tapasya

> *Penance — austerity undertaken voluntarily to build spiritual capacity and to earn the grace by which karma can be re-balanced.*

## What It Is

Tapasya (literally "heat") is effortful spiritual discipline. In Chapter 1 of *Karma Sutra*, it is not unpacked systematically — but two concrete pictures emerge:

- **A sadguru guides disciples into tapasya** alongside seva, as part of the mechanism by which the guru can help them acquire positive prarabdh karmas in this life (p.21).
- **Waiting in queue for the guru is itself tapasya.** Standing for hours on cold winter mornings or in summer heat, returning for multiple visits — this endurance is the practice, and the practice is what earns the relief the guru grants.

## How to Do It

Chapter 1 does not prescribe specific forms, but the framework is clear:

- **Choose some form of effortful, voluntary difficulty.** It does not have to be dramatic. What matters is that it is *chosen*, not imposed, and that it requires sustained effort.
- **Pair it with seva.** Tapasya without service is austerity without direction. In the Hingori framing, the two go together.
- **Return again when asked.** The devotees of Gurudev were sometimes asked to make several visits before relief was granted. The repetition was part of the tapasya.
- **Do not expect instant return.** Tapasya is how the disciple contributes to the guru-disciple transaction. Relief, when it comes, is calibrated to the effort shown.

## Why — The Rationale

> Their waiting in line was a test of their tapasya or sadhana. Based on this effort, he gave them or willed them relief from their suffering. It was his way of seeking instant gratification of their karmas.
> — Hingori on Gurudev, *Karma Sutra*, Ch.1, p.24

Tapasya, in this framing, is the **disciple's currency**. A sadguru can alter prarabdh and unlock sanchit benefits in a disciple's current life — but not arbitrarily. The effort of the disciple *activates* that possibility. Tapasya is what makes the transaction possible.

It also serves a secondary purpose: it is how the power of the mind is developed. *"A person can, through sadhana and his/her guru's grace, achieve powers that are supernormal."* (Ch.1, p.22) Tapasya builds the mental capacity; grace is what the capacity becomes.
