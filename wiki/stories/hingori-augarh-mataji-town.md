# Hingori Meets Augarh — The Mantra and the Night Watch

**Source:** Hingori, *The Guru of Gurus*, pp.54–55
**Related entities:** [[entities/augarh]], [[entities/hingori]], [[entities/mataji]]

---

> One of my first encounters with Augarh happened when Gurudev instructed Subbhash Sabharwal and me to accompany Mataji to her hometown. After arriving at Mataji's family home, we walked to a corner shop to buy tobacco. As we made our way towards it, we noticed a drunk and dishevelled man heading in our direction. He was pulling along his bicycle as he walked toward us. When we turned back to the house, he stood in our path, blocking it. He looked at us with piercing eyes and started chanting a mantra. I was shocked when I heard him saying the same mantra that I was reciting mentally at the time! I sensed this man was not to be trifled with. So Subbhash and I beat a hasty retreat.
>
> I noticed him riding his bicycle around the house all night. He left when dawn broke. I don't remember if Gurudev confirmed the man's identity as Augarh or if instinct led me to that conclusion, but I do remember that Augarh spent the entire night guarding the house where his guru's wife was staying.
> — Hingori, *The Guru of Gurus*, pp.54–55

---

## Significance

This is Hingori's first direct encounter with Augarh — and its most unsettling element is the telepathic synchronicity: the drunk man chants aloud the exact mantra Hingori is silently reciting at that moment. This is neither coincidence nor performance for observers — it is Augarh demonstrating awareness of Hingori's private mental state.

The rest of the night reveals the purpose of the appearance: Augarh was there to guard. He circled the house all night — the guru's wife within — and left at dawn without explanation or acknowledgement.

Hingori notes he cannot be certain whether Gurudev later confirmed the identity or whether instinct told him. Either way, the episode fits Augarh's pattern: appearing unbidden at points of genuine spiritual need, in forms that combine ordinariness (drunk man, bicycle) with the unmistakably extraordinary.
