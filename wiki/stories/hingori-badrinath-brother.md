## Full Narrative

> Thank god, Gurudev was not a great cricketer and especially not a brilliant bowler! Else, he would never have bowled a straight ball, only googlies. He asked me to spend a few days with him at his camp in Srinagar, Uttarakhand. There were other disciples in the camp, and he decided to take us to the famous Badrinath Temple.
>
> On reaching the temple, Gurudev waited outside while he asked me to go inside, hug the deity and say, "*My dear elder brother, I have come to give thee a hug.*" In Punjabi it sounds more emphatic though slightly comical, "*Bade Bhaiya main twanu jhappi paan ayaan*." I went inside not knowing whom to hug but was certain he did not want me to hug the temple deity. I chanced upon a statue of Adi Shankaracharya, the founder of the temple. I found him easy to hug. He was tangible, measurable, and huggable. I quite enjoyed hugging his marble statue. Call it a spiritual version of a chilled-out experience.
>
> When I came out and gave Gurudev an account of what I had done, he sent me back in and asked me to mentally hug Lord Badrinath. Whew! Was I to go and hug the deity of one of the most powerful temples in the country? It did not make sense to me at all, but Gurudev was dead serious. It took me years to figure it out. It was one of the reasons that could have led to this book.
>
> What Gurudev told me, without voicing it, was that I should consider myself a younger brother to that very powerful force. He expected me to believe in the equality of one *aatma* with another. He knew this was going to be a huge concept for me to fathom, and yet he wanted me to do so. I certainly felt elevated that he considered me a brother to such an icon.
>
> I also found it justifiable to accept the concept of self-worship. My inferiority complexes were shaken and shattered!
