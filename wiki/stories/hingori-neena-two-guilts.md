## Full Narrative

> My distant cousin Neena, was married to my wife's cousin. As the marriage was not working, she asked me to help her end the relationship. This put me in an awkward position. Moreover, I knew she had not played fair in the relationship. I had no moral right to ask my wife's cousin to grant her a divorce. Being a shrewd woman, Neena tried to influence me by talking to one of my spiritual associates. When even that did not work, she went to Gurudev in Gurgaon and begged him to influence me.
>
> Neena was present at the *sthan* in Gurgaon when I went to pay my respects to Gurudev. He made no mention of Neena requesting him for help. A few days later when she visited me at the Mumbai *sthan*, I blatantly lied when I said that Gurudev had asked me to help her. I felt guilty about lying. She replied, "Yes, I know. He told me he had a conversation with you in Gurgaon, and you agreed to help me." At that very moment, the guilt of lying vanished.
>
> I remember Gurudev once telling me that we are beyond lies and truth, but I never understood its meaning. As long as we speak from our inner consciousness, what emerges is beyond the perceived truth. I now realise that truth can neither be limited to, nor interpreted by the five senses. Being pronounced guilty by reference to the senses alone lacks perfection. The point is that guilt makes us feel inferior and wrong at the conscious as well as the subconscious level. (That is because we keep playing and replaying the sentiments in our minds and it becomes the reverse of an affirmation if a person recognises the need of correcting a wrong. Inadvertently, if you program a punishment in your *samskars*, then you have to suffer it later. Guilt at a conscious level can be an insurmountable obstacle to self-acceptance.)
