# The Wrong Remedy — Healing Is Intention, Not Method

## Full Narrative

> Once on a Thursday seva, I gave a piles remedy to a hernia patient because I forgot the difference between the two treatments. However, later I realised my mistake. On Thursday of the following week, I met the patient again. I was expecting aggravated symptoms, but instead, he said he was much better than before. I realised that healing was not backed by the science we were conditioned to, and it wasn't only about the cardamoms, cloves, jal or any of what we were taught. It was more the magic of intention than the process of the cure. As proof of this point, I remember the great guru's words, "Even mud from the sthan would work as effectively as any other spiritual treatment."
> — Hingori, *The Guru of Gurus*, p.228

## Source

*The Guru of Gurus*, Healing — Modes of Healing, p.228.

## Notes

The story is Hingori's own first-person account. Its force comes from the gap between the expected outcome (aggravated symptoms) and the actual one (improvement). Hingori draws the conclusion not speculatively but from lived experience: the patient got better despite the wrong prescription. The explanation is [[concepts/intent-as-healing]] — the guru's intent (here, working through Hingori as the empowered disciple) is the operative cause. The spice or remedy is incidental.

The story also illustrates how Gurudev transmitted healing authority to his disciples — Hingori was empowered enough that even his mistakes became the vehicle for healing, because the intent animating his seva was correct.
