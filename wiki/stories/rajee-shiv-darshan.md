## Full Narrative

> One of Gurudev's older disciples, Rajee, had an enviable and intriguing experience. In his dream, Gurudev took him out of his body to a cave in the mountains, but not on Earth. On entering the cave, he saw three enormous "statues" in deep meditation. He instinctively knew these as the meditating forms of the holy trinity, Shankar, Vishnu, and Brahma. Shankar came to consciousness. Staring at him with a deadpan expression, and in a deep resonating voice, Shankar asked him what he desired. "*Nothing my Lord*," said Rajee, "*I need your grace.*" Shankar materialised holy water with a milky texture, and poured it into Rajee's hands three times, saying '*mrityu, mrityu, mrityu*', (death, death, death), which probably meant the end of existence. Rajee left the cave and returned to his body. People might call this a delusion of the mind, wishful thinking or creative visualisation (*buddhibrahm*). But, for this gentleman it was as real as life itself. It was also a mandate for self-faith.
>
> For Rajee being blessed by Lord Shankar was like receiving a spiritual Oscar. Naturally, the reflection in the mirror would change its own perception hereafter. For those who characterise this as make-believe or delusionary, my question to them is, what is not?
>
> We live in a world of *maya* and everything around us is delusion. The senses are the translators of delusion and we are susceptible to imagining a world of reality that is nothing more than an appearance. This is *maya*. Delusion within delusion is as real or as reflective as your perceived reality. The dream state is another state of reality within the circle of *maya*. Some of it is as real as life itself except, that unlike physical life, it does not have continuity. It is like a book of short stories.
