# Ram Kills Bali from Behind a Tree

## Source

Hingori, *Aatma Sutra*, Ch.XI, pp.138–139

## Full Narrative

> The story of Lord Ram is an example of his vastly different perception. He rose far above guilt, and the duality of good and evil, right and wrong, black and white, god and devil, heaven and hell. His actions were not always considered 'acceptable' by the people of his time.
>
> For example, one of the controversial stories in the Ramayan is about the fight between Ram and Bali (the brother of Sughreev). Bali had the boon of being able to seize half the power of those who fought him face-to-face. And hence, Ram being well aware of the boon, hid behind a tree and shot an arrow into Bali's back thereby killing him and ending his reign. Readers of the Ramayan have criticised Ram for attacking Bali from behind as his act defied all prevailing norms of valour. In fact, it was considered an act of total cowardice. But Ram was taught by his teacher, Guru Vashisht, that the world was nothing but *maya* — a delusion of the mind. Nothing was real. Everything was a figment of the cosmic imagination, making duality unworthy of merit. Ram was thus beyond guilt. Surmounting duality took him beyond doership, leaving no justifications for right or wrong.
>
> It is only when you reach a particular level of evolution can you practice such a philosophy. If Ram had not thought the way he did, would he not be susceptible to a loss of self-esteem due to guilt? Furthermore, he would be *karmically* responsible for Bali's murder and would have to pay the price by atonement.
